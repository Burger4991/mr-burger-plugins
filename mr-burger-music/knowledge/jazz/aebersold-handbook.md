# Aebersold Jazz Handbook

**Relevance to LHS:** Foundational reference for jazz improvisation fundamentals — scales, chord-scale relationships, ii-V-I, blues, and practice methodology that directly supports learning the jazz language on trumpet.

## Key Concepts

Jazz improvisation is a learnable skill — not a gift. The handbook's core premise is that "anyone can improvise." Jazz musicians are instant composers: the melodies they play are conceived in their minds just before they play them. The goal is to shrink the distance between what you hear in your head and what comes out of your instrument.

The foundational tools are: scales, chords, patterns, licks, songs, and ear training. Of these, the **ear** is primary. Aebersold's consistent instruction is to sing everything — sing the scale, sing the chord tones, sing the exercise, then transfer it to the instrument. The voice approximates pitch and rhythm more naturally than any instrument. Playing what you hear (rather than what your fingers know) is the entire point.

**The three most-used scales and chords in jazz** are Major (Ionian), Dominant 7th (Mixolydian), and Minor (Dorian). The handbook identifies five basic chord-scale families: Major, Dominant 7th, Minor, Half-Diminished (Locrian), and Diminished. All other scale choices are substitutions or tension layers over these five families.

**The 3rd and 7th of any chord are the most important notes.** The 3rd tells you major or minor quality. The 7th tells you whether the chord is stable (major 7th) or wants to move (lowered 7th). Chord tones (1, 3, 5, 7) are the best notes to begin and end phrases with — the ear hears them first and they provide harmonic stability.

**Repetition and sequence** hold solos together. Listeners need to hear an idea repeated 2–3 times before they can process it. This is not a weakness — it is how musical communication works. The longest musical phrase begins with a single note.

**Jazz music is organized in 2, 4, and 8 bar phrases.** When lost, listen to the rhythm section — move a note up or down a half-step and you are likely back in the scale.

**Practicing in all 12 keys** is non-negotiable. The Cycle of Fourths is the standard vehicle.

## Exercises / Reference

### Core Theory Concepts

**Scale construction (whole/half step formulas):**
- Major (Ionian): W W H W W W H
- Dominant 7th (Mixolydian): W W H W W H W
- Minor (Dorian): W H W W W H W
- Half-Diminished (Locrian): H W W H W W W
- Diminished (8-tone): W H W H W H W H (or begin with H)
- Blues Scale: 1, b3, 4, #4, 5, b7 (formula: -3 W H H -3 W)
- Major Pentatonic: 1 2 3 5 6
- Minor Pentatonic: 1 b3 4 5 b7
- Lydian (Major with #4): W W W H W W H
- Lydian Dominant: W W W H W H W
- Whole Tone: W W W W W W (only 2 unique whole-tone scales exist)
- Bebop Dominant: 1 2 3 4 5 6 b7 (natural 7 added as passing tone; must fall on upbeat)

**Chord symbol notation (Aebersold system):**
- Delta (Δ) = Major 7th
- Dash (−) = Minor (usually Dorian)
- 7 = Dominant 7th (lowered 7th)
- Ø = Half-diminished
- o = Diminished
- + or # = raise 1/2 step; − or b = lower 1/2 step
- -3 = minor third (3 half-steps)

**Five basic chord-scale families with common substitutions (arranged consonant to dissonant):**
1. **Major choices:** Major, Lydian, Lydian Augmented, Blues Scale, Harmonic Major
2. **Dominant 7th choices:** Mixolydian, Bebop, Lydian Dominant (#4), Whole Tone, Diminished (b9), Diminished Whole Tone (Altered/+9), Spanish/Jewish (b9), Chromatic
3. **Minor choices:** Dorian, Minor Pentatonic, Bebop Minor, Melodic Minor, Blues, Harmonic Minor, Phrygian, Aeolian
4. **Half-Diminished choices:** Locrian, Locrian #2
5. **Diminished choices:** Diminished 8-tone scale

**Modes of the C Major scale (all share the same key signature):**
- C: Ionian (Major)
- D: Dorian (Minor)
- E: Phrygian
- F: Lydian (Major with #4)
- G: Mixolydian (Dom. 7th)
- A: Aeolian (Pure/Natural Minor)
- B: Locrian (Half-Diminished)

Most-used modes in jazz: Major, Dorian, Mixolydian, Lydian, Half-Diminished.

**Bebop scale rules:**
- The added passing tone must fall on an upbeat — never a downbeat
- Good starting notes for ascending eighth-note lines: 1, 3, 5, b7 (all chord tones)
- Proper bebop articulation makes phrase endings land on chord tones naturally

**Dominant 7th scale choices (tree, arranged mild to dissonant):**
1. Dominant 7th (Mixolydian) — basic sound; treat the 4th as a passing tone
2. Bebop — add natural 7 as passing tone; always on the upbeat
3. Lydian Dominant — #4 adds color; formerly called "raised 5th"
4. Whole Tone — 6 tones, symmetrical; has #4 and #5
5. Diminished (b9) — 8 tones, symmetrical; has b9, #9, #4; Michael Brecker specialty
6. Diminished Whole Tone (Altered/+9) — 4 altered tones: b9, #9, #4, #5
7. Spanish/Jewish (b9) — same as F harmonic minor; used over minor key V7s
8. Chromatic

### ii-V-I Framework

The ii-V-I (also written II/V7/I) is the single most important harmonic progression in jazz. It appears in virtually every standard and jazz composition.

**Structure in a major key (example: key of C):**
- ii = D minor 7 (D−7): Dorian scale — WHWWWHW
- V7 = G dominant 7 (G7): Mixolydian or any Dom.7th scale choice
- I = C major (CΔ): Major (Ionian) scale

**Structure in a minor key:**
- ii = D half-diminished (DØ): Locrian or Locrian #2
- V7 = G7+9 or G7b9: Diminished or Diminished Whole Tone (Altered)
- i = C minor (C−7): Dorian or Melodic Minor

**Guide tone voice leading — the 7th resolves to the 3rd:**
- The 7th of the ii chord moves by half-step down to the 3rd of the V7 chord
- The 7th of the V7 chord moves by half-step down to the 3rd of the I chord
- Example: In D−7 → G7 → CΔ, the C (7th of D−7) resolves down to B (3rd of G7), and F (7th of G7) resolves down to E (3rd of CΔ)
- This half-step melodic motion is the engine of harmonic movement and what makes lines "outline the harmony"

**Distance relationships:**
- Root of ii to root of V7 = Perfect 4th (5 half-steps)
- Root of V7 to root of I = Perfect 4th
- All three chords in a major key ii-V-I share the same key signature

**ii-V-I in minor keys:** The ii chord is half-diminished (Ø), and the V7 chord is typically altered with b9 and +9, making it C7b9, G7+9, etc. The minor key V7 chord has 4 altered tones.

**Practice sequence for learning the ii-V-I (Aebersold's 12-step approach applied to ii-V-I):**
1. Play root/tonic of each chord
2. Play first 2 notes of each scale
3. Play first 3 notes
4. Play first 5 notes
5. Play the triad (1, 3, 5)
6. Play 7th chords (1, 3, 5, 7)
7. Play 9th chords (1, 3, 5, 7, 9)
8. Play entire scale up and down
9. Play 6th chords (1, 3, 5, 6)
10. Scale up to 9th, back down through chord tones
11. 9th chord up, back down the scale
12. Scale in broken thirds

Practice this sequence through all 12 keys via the cycle of fourths. Vol. 3 (The ii-V7-I Progression) is the dedicated play-along for this.

**Chord tone emphasis for ii-V-I phrases:** Emphasize the 3rd and 7th. On the ii chord, target the b7 and b3. On the V7 chord, target the 3rd (which is the tritone partner of the b7). On the I chord, land on the major 3rd or 7th.

### Play-Along Practice

The Aebersold Play-A-Long series provides professional rhythm section recordings for solo practice. The left channel carries bass and drums; the right channel carries piano and guitar. Pan left to practice with only bass and drums.

**How to use the play-alongs effectively:**
- Warm up with roots only, then first 5 notes of each scale, then full scales over the changes
- Record yourself and listen back — this is how you actually hear what you sound like
- Alternate: play 4 bars, sing 4 bars, play 4 bars — builds the ear-to-instrument connection
- Use slow-tempo versions to learn; raise tempo only after the material is solid
- Practice articulation while playing along — imitate what the pros do on recordings

**Recommended volumes for foundational trumpet work:**

- Vol. 2: Nothin' But Blues — 11 different blues progressions
- Vol. 3: The ii-V7-I Progression — the most important harmonic motion in jazz
- Vol. 21: Gettin' It Together — all levels, all 12 keys; ear training tracks included
- Vol. 24: Major and Minor — all 12 keys

**Jerry Coker's 75-minute daily practice schedule:**
1. Slow melody (tune) — 5 min
2. Scales and patterns — 15 min
3. Pattern application — 10 min
4. Improvisation exercise — 5 min
5. Transcribed solo — 15 min
6. Special disciplines — 10 min
7. Learn a tune — 15 min

Change materials weekly. Items 3–7 should be played with recorded accompaniment.

**David Liebman's principle:** One or two clear goals per session maximum. Always know exactly what you are practicing and why.

**Transcription as study:** Analyzing recorded solos reveals: what scale degree phrases start/end on, whether chord tones or scale passages dominate, how repetition and sequence are used, whether blues scale appears and how often, how chromatic notes function, how rest and silence are used.

### Key Vocabulary

**Changes** — the chord progression of a tune. Memorizing the changes is mandatory before improvising. "Playing the changes" means your improvised lines outline and follow the harmony.

**Guide tones** — the 3rd and 7th of any chord. The most harmonically essential notes. Moving guide tones by half-step from chord to chord (especially 7th to 3rd) is the foundation of bebop line construction.

**Chord tones** — 1, 3, 5, 7 of any scale. Best notes to begin and end phrases on. The ear identifies chord tones immediately as "inside" the harmony.

**Chord/scale relationship** — every chord symbol implies both a vertical chord and a horizontal scale. C7 implies Mixolydian (C D E F G A Bb) as the melodic resource. The improvisor chooses from this scale (and its substitutes) when soloing over that chord.

**Turnaround (I-VI-ii-V)** — the progression at the end of a section that cycles back to the beginning. Example in C: CΔ — A7 — D−7 — G7.

**Head** — the melody of a tune. Playing the head means playing the written melody. Typically played once at the start and once at the end.

**Tritone** — the interval of an augmented 4th / diminished 5th (6 half-steps = 3 whole tones). Found between the 3rd and b7 of a dominant 7th chord. This interval creates the harmonic tension that demands resolution to the I chord.

**Lick** — a short memorized melodic phrase applicable in multiple harmonic situations. Building a vocabulary of licks is central to developing an improvisational language.

**Sequence** — repeating a melodic idea starting on a different pitch. Repetition + Sequence = the primary glue of improvised solos. Average repetitions: 2–3 times.

**Tension and release** — the fundamental dynamic of jazz harmony and melody. Scale choices near the top of the syllabus categories are consonant (relaxed); choices further down are increasingly dissonant (tense). The improvisor controls the ratio of tension to release.

**Bebop** — the common practice period of jazz (1940s–50s). Key characteristics: complex harmony, chord extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths), fast tempos, eighth notes as primary melodic unit, chord substitution, vibrato-less tone, emphasis on technical facility. Chords serve as the improvisational reference rather than melody.

**Articulation** — how notes are attacked and connected. Jazz standard: legato swing style (tah-tah-tah, not tat-tat-tat). Articulation is "proper enunciation" — it carries your musical message. Takes 4–6 weeks of focused work to transform. Practice with eyes closed and listen to each note's attack.

**Tessitura** — the general register in which phrases occur (high, middle, low). Varying tessitura creates interest in a solo.

**Pick-up** — notes leading into the first strong downbeat of a phrase. Common pick-ups: 7 to 1, #2 to 3, #4 to 5, 4 to 3 (half-step leading tones).

**Transposition:**
- Bb instruments (trumpet, cornet, clarinet, tenor sax, soprano sax): written notes UP a whole step from concert pitch
- Eb instruments (alto sax, bari sax, Eb clarinet): written notes DOWN a minor third from concert pitch
- C instruments (piano, flute, guitar, trombone, bass): non-transposing

**Blues scale** — 1, b3, 4, #4, 5, b7. Conveys a "funky," "earthy," "bluesy" feel. Used over minor chords of 2+ bars. In Bb blues, use Bb blues scale throughout. Can alternate between Dorian minor and blues scale when on a minor chord. Do not confine improvisation to only the blues scale — it wears thin without context from the other scales.

**12-bar blues form** — the three core chords: I7, IV7, V7 (dominant 7ths). In F: F7, Bb7, C7. The form can be elaborated from the simplest 3-chord version through 17 different progressions of increasing harmonic complexity. Common jazz blues includes ii-V substitutions in bars 9–12.

**Bebop scale rule** — the added passing tone must always fall on an upbeat. When starting on the 2nd, 4th, or 6th scale tone, use a passing tone or chromaticism to keep the added tone on the upbeat. Starting on beats 1, 2, 3, or 4 (downbeats) — start on scale tone 1, 3, 5, or b7.

## Connection to LHS

The Aebersold Handbook is the foundational text for jazz self-education — everything in it maps directly to building functional improvisational ability on trumpet. The ii-V-I framework and guide tone voice leading (7th resolving to 3rd) are the most important concepts to internalize first; once the ear hears that half-step motion, the harmonic logic of jazz standards becomes audible rather than abstract. The bebop scale is the next high-leverage tool — it makes eighth-note lines resolve to chord tones on downbeats automatically when used correctly. The practice methodology (sing it, play it in all 12 keys, memorize the changes, transcribe recordings, use the play-alongs) is not supplementary — it is the actual method that all jazz players have used. Aebersold's repeated emphasis on listening to recordings is the real homework: the answers to every improvisational question are already on records.
