---

## Key Concepts

Windau adapts three major and three minor ii-V-I outlines from Bert Ligon's *Connecting Chords with Linear Harmony*. Each outline is a short melodic line (pickup note + ~4 notes per chord) that connects a ii chord, a V chord, and a I chord. The document presents all six outlines transposed into every key in one place, making it a comprehensive drill sheet.

- **Major set:** ii7 – V7 – Imaj7 (three outlines)
- **Minor set:** iiø7 – V7b9 – imi (three outlines)
- Each outline uses a diatonic pickup note before beat 1 of the ii chord.
- Windau's versions include **passing tones** that are not present in Ligon's bare chord-tone outlines (flagged in the Passing Tone Variations section below).
- The three outlines progress in complexity: Outline 1 is the simplest, Outline 3 adds the most motion.

## Exercises / Reference

### How to read these tables

Each cell shows the melodic line for that chord in the ii-V-I progression. Notes are listed left to right as they appear in time. The pickup note is listed first in parentheses before the ii chord. The I chord is typically a whole note (target/resolution tone).

> **Notation conventions used below:**
> - `b` = flat, `#` = sharp
> - Pickup note shown in parentheses: `(E)`
> - The I chord cell shows only the target/resolution whole note.
> - All outlines are written for concert pitch (treble clef, as notated in source).

### Outline 2 — Major ii7–V7–Imaj7 (Windau)

Adds passing motion and neighbor tones through the V chord; slightly more chromatic than Outline 1.

| Root | ii chord (pickup + notes) | V chord notes | I chord (whole note) |
|------|--------------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| C    | (D) F–A–C               | G–B–D–F       | E (3rd of I)         |
| Db   | (Eb) Gb–Bb–Db           | Ab–C–Eb–Gb    | F (3rd of Db)        |
| D    | (E) G–B–D               | A–C#–E–G      | F# (3rd of D)        |
| Eb   | (F) Ab–C–Eb             | Bb–D–F–Ab     | G (3rd of Eb)        |
| E    | (F#) A–C#–E             | B–D#–F#–A     | G# (3rd of E)        |
| F    | (G) Bb–D–F              | C–E–G–Bb      | A (3rd of F)         |
| F#   | (G#) B–D#–F#            | C#–E#–G#–B    | A# (3rd of F#)       |
| G    | (A) C–E–G               | D–F#–A–C      | B (3rd of G)         |
| Ab   | (Bb) Db–F–Ab            | Eb–G–Bb–Db    | C (3rd of Ab)        |
| A    | (B) D–F#–A              | E–G#–B–D      | C# (3rd of A)        |
| Bb   | (C) Eb–G–Bb             | F–A–C–Eb      | D (3rd of Bb)        |
| B    | (C#) E–G#–B             | F#–A#–C#–E    | D# (3rd of B)        |

> **Note on Outline 2:** The I chord resolution lands on the **3rd of the I chord** (not the root), creating a smooth voice-leading connection into the next bar. This is the key fingerprint of Outline 2.

### Passing Tone Variations — Windau vs. Bare Ligon

Windau's outlines are derived from Ligon's bare chord-tone versions but add specific passing tones. The second source PDF (Ligon Ch. 5) documents this explicitly: "The following outlines all include passing tones and all begin with added pick up notes."

> **Flag — these are Windau additions not present in bare Ligon outlines:**

| Location | Windau addition | What it does |
|----------|----------------|--------------|
| All outlines — pickup note | Diatonic note before beat 1 of ii chord | Ligon's bare version starts on beat 1 of the ii chord; Windau (following Ligon Ch. 5 passing tone discussion) adds a pickup |
| Outline 3 (major and minor) — V chord entry | Chromatic half-step approach note into the V chord (e.g., C# into D over G7 in C) | Creates leading-tone tension into the dominant; this chromatic passing tone is not in Outline 1 or 2 |
| Minor outlines — V7b9 chord | b9 of the V chord (e.g., Db over G7b9 in C minor) | Ligon notes this as a defining color of the minor ii-V; Windau preserves it consistently across all minor outlines |
| Outline 2 (major and minor) — I chord resolution | Resolution to 3rd of I chord rather than root | Smooth voice-leading; not a passing tone per se, but a consistent Windau/Ligon structural choice that differs from root-resolution practice |

**The Ligon Ch. 5 PDF context:** That document (pages 56–62 visible) shows how Cannonball Adderley, Clifford Brown, Tom Harrell, and Bill Evans all used variations of Outline 2 with passing tones — one added diatonic note, two added diatonic notes, lower neighbor tones, escape tones, and encircling upper/lower neighbor tones. Windau's systematized versions are the clean, drill-ready distillation of this variety.

## Practice Instructions

From the Ligon source (Ch. 5 Suggested Exercises, preserved here for reference):

1. Create your own lines using similar devices from the above examples.
2. Learn some of the above examples in all keys.
3. Write out solos on standard jazz progressions and incorporate some of the above examples. Practice the solos like any etude.
4. Try improvising over standard progressions and use some of the above examples in your solos.
5. Alter some of the above examples either by adding more notes, rhythmic displacing, fragmenting, etc. to come up with lines of you own.
6. Practice outline no. 2 anticipating the thirds over the barline in all major and minor keys.

**For Windau sheet specifically:** The document presents all 12 keys in sequence (C, Db, D, Eb, E, F, F#, G, Ab, A, Bb, B). The intended practice approach is to work through the sheet chromatically, playing each outline in all keys before moving to the next outline.

## Connection to LHS

These all-key tables are the transposition layer of the LHS (Linear Harmony System) practice framework. LHS is built on Ligon's outlines; Windau's document removes the need to mentally transpose in real time, allowing the player to internalize each outline as a key-specific physical pattern before developing ear-based transposition. The tables above extend that further into a text-scannable quick reference usable in any session without opening the PDF.
