## Key Concepts

Jazz standards are the vehicles jazz musicians use to improvise. Vaartstra's book organizes ten essential standards ("The 10 Master Standards") as studies rather than just songs — each one teaches a specific harmonic or improvisational concept that transfers to hundreds of other tunes.

The foundational framework across all ten standards is the **ii-V-I chord progression** — the single most important harmonic movement in jazz. In a major key it is Xmin7 → X7 → XMaj7 (e.g., Dmin7 → G7 → Cmaj7 in C major). In a minor key the ii chord becomes half-diminished: Xmin7(b5) → X7 → Xmin7 (e.g., Amin7(b5) → D7 → Gmin7 in G minor).

**Guide tones** — the 3rd and 7th of each chord — are the primary application targets. They define chord quality (major/minor/dominant) and voice-lead smoothly from one chord to the next: the 7th of a chord resolves down a half step to the 3rd of the next chord in a cycling-fourths progression. This is the direct LHS connection — LHS outlines are built to target and articulate these guide tones.

**The LIST Process** for learning standards by ear (not sheet music):
- **L**isten — find multiple recordings and immerse
- **I**nternalize — intentional, focused listening everywhere
- **S**ing — sing the melody before touching your instrument; sing bass notes to learn the harmony
- **T**ransfer — learn melody on instrument, then learn the chords

The book covers each standard with: a chord map (chord changes + chord tones + piano/guitar voicings), a guide tones chart, a chord analysis with Roman numerals, and an improv lesson drawing a specific concept from that tune.

Key harmonic vocabulary established across the ten standards:
- Major and minor diatonic series of 7th chords
- Relative major/minor key relationships
- Cycling dominant 7th chords (cycle of fourths)
- Modal harmony (So What — D Dorian / Eb Dorian)
- Motivic development and note mapping

## Exercises / Reference

### Beginner-Friendly Standards

| Standard | Concert Key(s) | LHS Applicability | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autumn Leaves** | G minor / E minor | Easy | Almost entirely ii-V-I in minor + relative major; one of the most-recorded standards; ideal first tune |
| **Blue Bossa** | C minor | Easy | Short form, minimal changes, key center modulation; great entry-level Bossa Nova tune |
| **All of Me** | C major | Easy | Chord-tone-based improvisation; strong tonal center; good for applying outlines on each chord |
| **So What** | D minor / Eb minor | Easy (modal) | Only two chords; long stretches of one tonality; ideal for exploring a single outline over many bars |
| **On Green Dolphin Street** | C major | Medium | Features motivic development; two distinct sections |
| **It Could Happen to You** | Bb major | Medium | Good vehicle for composing melodies; clean changes |
| **Sweet Georgia Brown** | F major | Medium-Hard | Cycling dominant 7th chords (all fourths); challenging but highly instructive |
| **Have You Met Miss Jones** | F major | Hard | B-section features rapid key center shifts (giant steps-adjacent); strong ii-V-I in the A section |
| **All the Things You Are** | Ab major | Hard | Multiple key centers; one of the most harmonically rich standards; benchmark tune |
| **Stella by Starlight** | Bb major | Hard | Chromatic key movement; requires note mapping and strong outline vocabulary |

### ii-V-I Spots by Standard

**Autumn Leaves (concert G minor / Bb major relative):**
- Bars 1–3: Cmin7 → F7 → Bbmaj7 (ii-V-I in Bb major, the relative major)
- Bars 5–7: Amin7(b5) → D7 → Gmin7 (minor ii-V-i in G minor, the parent key)
- These two progressions repeat throughout most of the form — the entire song is essentially alternating between these two ii-V-I patterns

**Blue Bossa (concert C minor):**
- Opens in C minor with a minor ii-V-I; briefly modulates to Db major with a ii-V-I there before returning to C minor
- Two key centers, both navigated with ii-V-I

**All of Me (C major):**
- Contains ii-V-I progressions in multiple key areas (C major, F major, D major, E major) as the song moves through secondary dominants
- Each chord change is a chance to target chord tones directly

**So What (D Dorian / Eb Dorian):**
- No ii-V-I in the traditional sense — the tune is modal; 16 bars on Dmin7, 8 bars on Ebmin7, 8 bars back to Dmin7
- Application: a single LHS outline extended over long stretches in one mode

**Sweet Georgia Brown (F major):**
- Entire A section is cycling dominant 7th chords moving in fourths: B7 → E7 → A7 → D7 → G7 → C7 → F
- Each dominant 7th is a V7 of the next — a chain of secondary dominants resolving to F
- High-density ii-V application environment; the V7→I of each link is a mini ii-V-I

### Most Common Keys

Based on the 10 Master Standards, these keys appear most frequently. Prioritize LHS outline practice in:

1. **G minor / Bb major** (Autumn Leaves)
2. **C minor / Eb major** (Blue Bossa)
3. **C major** (All of Me)
4. **F major** (It Could Happen to You, Sweet Georgia Brown, Have You Met Miss Jones)
5. **Bb major** (Autumn Leaves relative, Stella by Starlight)
6. **Ab major** (All the Things You Are)

The **key of Bb major and its relative G minor** is arguably the most important pair to master first — Autumn Leaves alone covers both.

### Practice Entry Point

**Autumn Leaves in G minor.** It is structurally simple (almost all ii-V-I), widely recorded (hundreds of versions), introduces both major and minor ii-V-I in one tune, and is commonly called at jam sessions at every level. Once you can navigate Autumn Leaves with LHS outlines on both the Cmin7-F7-Bbmaj7 and Amin7(b5)-D7-Gmin7 progressions, that material transfers directly to dozens of other standards.

## Connection to LHS

Jazz standards are the **Part 4 Application stage** of LHS practice. The sequence:

1. **Parts 1–3** (LHS internalization) — know the outline, its guide tones, its rhythmic shape
2. **Part 4 (Standards Application)** — drop the outline into real chord progressions from actual tunes

Each standard provides specific ii-V-I slots where an LHS outline can be placed. Autumn Leaves is the cleanest starting point because its ii-V-I progressions are long (typically two bars each) and clearly delineated — plenty of space to place and resolve an outline. As standards become more complex (All the Things You Are, Stella), the ii-V-Is get shorter and more densely packed, requiring faster outline deployment.

The guide tone framework (3rd → 7th voice leading) aligns directly with LHS outline construction. LHS outlines target the 3rd and 7th of each chord by design. Every ii-V-I in a jazz standard is an opportunity to voice-lead through chord tones the way LHS trains.
