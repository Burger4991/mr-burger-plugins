## Key Concepts

Bebop emerged in the 1940s as a shift from dance-oriented swing to a more cerebral, virtuosic art form. David Baker calls it the "common practice period of jazz." Its core characteristics are:

- **Harmonic complexity:** Extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) and high chromaticism layered over standard progressions.
- **Melodic phrasing:** Longer, asymmetrically constructed lines rather than short, symmetrical phrases.
- **Primacy of harmony:** The chord changes — not the original melody — are the primary reference for improvisation. This is why bebop players composed contrafacts (new melodies over borrowed progressions).
- **Rhythmic shift:** Eighth and sixteenth notes became the basic unit of time, replacing the quarter-note pulse of swing.
- **Technical virtuosity:** A clean, flowing execution across fast tempos is the expectation.

The ultimate goal, stated by Jamey Aebersold, is to play exactly what you hear in your mind. The bebop language is the vocabulary that closes the gap between inner hearing and physical execution.

Three technical tools define bebop melodic language: the **bebop scale**, **chromatic targeting**, and **enclosures**. All three work on the same principle — using non-chord tones to create momentary tension that pulls toward a stable chord tone. The bebop scale does this rhythmically (aligning chord tones to downbeats); targeting and enclosures do it melodically (approaching chord tones by half-step from below, above, or both).

Bebop lines also depend heavily on **guide tones** — the 3rds and 7ths of each chord — as harmonic signposts. These are the tones that define the chord quality and create the sense of movement from one chord to the next. A classic guide tone resolution in a ii-V-I: the b7 of G7 (F) resolves down by half-step to the 3rd of Cmaj7 (E). Targeting these resolutions is what separates a student running scales from an improviser telling a harmonic story.

## Exercises / Reference

### Bebop Vocabulary

**Bebop Scales** — Add one chromatic passing tone to create an 8-note scale. The 8-note structure ensures chord tones fall on downbeats and passing tones fall on upbeats when playing continuous eighth notes.

| Scale | Formula (in C) | Chromatic addition |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant Bebop Scale | C D E F G A Bb B C | Natural 7 added between b7 and root |
| Major Bebop Scale | C D E F G G# A B C | #5 added between 5 and 6 |
| Minor Bebop Scale (Dorian) | C D Eb E F G A Bb C | Natural 3 added between b3 and 4 |

**Practice method (Dominant Bebop Scale over C7):**
- Play C D E F G A Bb B C as continuous eighth notes starting on a downbeat.
- The chord tones (C, E, G, Bb) land on beats 1, 2, 3, 4.
- The passing tones (D, F, A, B) land on the "ands."
- This is the rhythmic "secret" behind Parker and Gillespie's flowing lines.

---

**Chromatic Targeting** — Approach a chord tone from a half-step below, a half-step above, or both.

Examples in C over a C7 chord (targeting chord tones C, E, G, Bb):

| Approach | Formula | Notes played |
|---|---|---|
| From below | Half-step below → target | B→C, D#→E, F#→G, A→Bb |
| From above | Half-step above → target | Db→C, F→E, Ab→G, B→Bb |
| Double (below + above) | Two chromatics below → target | Bb-B→C, D-D#→E, F-F#→G, Ab-A→Bb |

**Immediate practice exercise (from Comprehensive Curriculum):** Take the C7 arpeggio (C-E-G-Bb) and approach each note from a half-step below: B-C, D#-E, F#-G, A-Bb. Practice until automatic over all chord qualities.

---

**Enclosures** — "Box in" a target chord tone by playing one note above and one below it before landing on it. More elaborate than simple targeting; creates the characteristic "swirling" bebop sound.

Examples in C (targeting C, E, G, Bb):

| Enclosure type | Formula | Notes played (targeting C) |
|---|---|---|
| Enclosure 1 (diatonic above + chromatic below) | Scale tone above, half-step below → target | D, B → C |
| Enclosure 2 (chromatic below + chromatic above) | Half-step below, chromatic above → target | B, Db → C |
| Double enclosure (1 above + 2 below) | Chromatic above, 2 chromatics below → target | Db, Bb, B → C |
| Double enclosure (2 above + 1 below) | 2 chromatics above, 1 below → target | Db, D, B → C |

### Learning Sequence

From beginner to developing player, the curriculum maps a clear sequence:

**Stage 1 — Foundation (before any bebop language)**
1. Deep listening: absorb recordings daily (Kind of Blue, Saxophone Colossus, Study in Brown)
2. Ear training: sing roots of chord changes; sing scales and arpeggios; identify intervals
3. Learn chord/scale relationships: m7 → Dorian, 7 → Mixolydian, Maj7 → Ionian
4. Master guide tones: know the 3rd and 7th of every chord in the progression
5. Practice arpeggio + scale pairs: arpeggio up → scale down; scale up → arpeggio down

**Stage 2 — Harmonic Internalization**
6. Learn the melody of a tune by ear; be able to sing it from memory
7. Play only the root of each chord in time through the full progression
8. Apply simple patterns (1-2-3-5) to every chord
9. Arpeggiate the 7th chords through the full progression
10. Play scales for each chord through the progression
11. Play only guide tones through the changes

**Stage 3 — Bebop Language Introduction**
12. Learn the dominant bebop scale; apply to V7 chords first
13. Practice chromatic approach from below on each chord tone
14. Add approach from above
15. Add double chromatic approach (2 notes below)
16. Introduce Enclosure 1 (scale tone above + chromatic below)
17. Add Enclosure 2 (chromatic below + chromatic above)
18. Combine enclosures and chromatic approaches in improvised lines

**Stage 4 — Integration**
19. Transcribe a short solo; analyze enclosures, targeting, and bebop scale usage
20. Apply bebop vocabulary to full tune; use it over blues and rhythm changes

### Applying to Outlines

LHS outlines (Windau / Connecting Chords with Linear Harmony) provide bare chord-tone skeletons across a ii-V-I. Bebop embellishments decorate those skeletons:

| LHS Outline | What it provides | Bebop addition |
|---|---|---|
| Outline 1 | Basic chord tone connection across the changes (whole/half note rhythm) | Add chromatic approach notes below each outline target note to create pickup figures |
| Outline 2 | Passing tones and pickup notes added to outline; eighth-note feel begins | Add enclosures around the landing notes; apply bebop scale over the V7 chord |
| Outline 3 | Extended arpeggios, approach notes, escape tones; fuller bebop line | Combine all enclosure types and double chromatic approaches; construct continuous eighth-note lines using bebop scale |

The CLB (Chad Lefkowitz-Brown) approach note etudes demonstrate full Outline 2 and 3 application: lines over standard progressions (All the Things You Are, Autumn Leaves, Cherokee) use enclosures and chromatic targeting to decorate LHS-style outlines at performance tempo (168–240 bpm).

### First Steps

What to learn before diving into full bebop language (from Beginner's Guide, Aebersold):

1. **Listen first.** Pick one beginner-friendly tune (Blues in Bb, Maiden Voyage, Satin Doll). Find a master recording and listen until the melody and harmony are in your ear.
2. **Memorize the melody.** Sing it from memory. This is non-negotiable — it shortens the distance from mind to fingers.
3. **Learn the chord/scale connections.** For each chord symbol, know the corresponding scale (Dm7 = D Dorian, G7 = G Mixolydian, Cmaj7 = C Ionian).
4. **Pre-flight exercises over the changes:**
   - Play the first 5 notes of each scale
   - Play the triad (1-3-5)
   - Play up to the 9th and back
   - Play the 7th chord arpeggio up and down
5. **Start improvising now.** Don't wait for "ready." Use scale tones, land on chord tones at phrase endings, use repetition and sequence to build coherent ideas.
6. **Add bebop tools one at a time.** Start with approach from below on a single note per chord. Add one new technique per week.

The 75-minute practice framework (adapted from Jerry Coker):
- Slow melody/tune: 5 min
- Scales and patterns: 15 min
- Pattern application: 10 min
- Improvisation exercise: 5 min
- Transcribed solo study: 15 min
- Special disciplines (target weaknesses): 10 min
- Learn a new tune: 15 min

## Connection to LHS

In the LHS system (Connecting Chords with Linear Harmony), practice moves through three stages: bare outlines → passing tones and pickups → full embellishment. Bebop language is the content of that third stage.

Specifically:
- The **bebop scale** provides the rhythmic framework for LHS Part 3 lines — it guarantees chord tones land on downbeats even in continuous eighth-note passages.
- **Chromatic targeting** is the tool for creating the pickup notes and approach figures that turn LHS Outline 1 and 2 skeletons into bebop-style lines.
- **Enclosures** are the most sophisticated embellishment, used by master players (Clifford Brown, Cannonball Adderley, Tom Harrell) to elaborate on the landing notes of Outline 2 and 3.

The Jazz Exercises 2021 PDF (Outline Exercises, Chapter 10) provides the bridge: its 16-measure exercise over Dm7-G7-Cmaj7 in all 12 keys is the direct LHS Part 3 practice vehicle. The goal is to play non-stop through all 12 keys without hesitation — this is where bebop language becomes fluent rather than calculated.
