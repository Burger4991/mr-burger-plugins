## Key Concepts

Klobnak's central argument is that effective improvisation requires **purposeful forward motion toward specific goal notes** — what he calls targeting. Rather than noodling and hoping to land on good notes, the improviser identifies destination points (targets) in advance and deliberately moves toward them.

The analogy he uses: targets are like stops on a planned road trip. A good solo has clear destination points at the micro level (each measure) and macro level (climax of the solo). Without targets, improvisation becomes aimless melodic meandering.

**What makes a good target?** Guide tones — the 3rd and 7th of each chord — are the strongest targets because they:
1. Define the chord quality (major, minor, dominant, half-diminished)
2. Voice-lead smoothly from chord to chord (the 7th of one chord resolves to the 3rd of the next in a ii-V-I)
3. Are the same notes LHS outlines are built to hit

Klobnak expands the target vocabulary beyond just 3rds and 7ths to include: root, 5th, 9th, sharp 11th, 13th — essentially the full chord tone/extension set. Great melodies (composed and improvised) target all of these, not just guide tones. But for beginners, 3rds and 7ths are the primary focus.

**A target can be approached from above or below.** The approach can be diatonic or chromatic. Klobnak focuses primarily on chromatic targeting because it works across all tonalities — you don't need to know the specific key area to use a chromatic approach.

**The book's four targeting methods:**
1. **Chromatic Targeting** (Chapter 2) — approach by half step(s) from above or below; 10 distinct chromatic targeting approaches documented and drilled in all 12 keys
2. **Pentatonic, Blues, and Digital Pattern Targeting** (Chapter 3) — use pentatonic scales (major, Insen/minor pentatonic with natural 6th, major flat-6th), blues scale, or digital patterns (1-2-3-5, 5-3-2-1, etc.) as the vehicle to approach a target note
3. **Diminished/Altered Targeting** (Chapter 4) — use diminished or altered scale material to approach chord tone targets; particularly useful over dominant 7th chords
4. **Etudes** (Chapter 5) — musical application of all targeting types in composed studies

The prerequisite ("Essential Tools"): know your major, minor, and chromatic scales in all keys, and have a basic understanding of how chords are built.

## Exercises / Reference

### Targeting Concept

**Core definition:** Targeting is aiming and moving at a goal note with purpose.

The targeted note typically lands on:
- The downbeat (beat 1) of a measure — strongest placement
- Beat 3 — next strongest
- Later in the measure — creates "delayed resolution," adds interest

The approach pattern (the material before the target) can be short (1–2 notes) or longer (a full digital pattern or pentatonic run). The target note itself should feel like an arrival, not an accident.

**Chromatic targeting in detail:** There are 10 distinct ways to approach a note chromatically — combinations of approaching from a half step above, a half step below, a minor third above, or a minor third below, using one or two approach notes. All 10 forms are drilled in all 12 keys in the book. The key point: chromatic approaches work over any tonality as long as the target note fits the chord of the moment.

**Pentatonic targeting:** Use a pentatonic scale run (major, minor/Insen, or major flat-6th) as the approach vehicle. The target note is typically the root, 3rd, or 5th of the current tonality. Because pentatonics fit over multiple chord types (e.g., C major pentatonic works over Amin7, Dmin7, G7sus), they are flexible targeting tools.

**Digital pattern targeting:** Mathematical note groupings (e.g., 1-2-3-5 or 5-3-2-1 of a scale) used to approach a target. Patterns can be mixed, shifted to adjacent key areas, or combined with chromatic targeting. They define the harmony clearly while moving toward the goal note.

### Targeting + LHS Outlines

LHS outlines are themselves targeting vehicles. The connection:

| LHS Outline Element | Klobnak Targeting Equivalent |
|---|---|
| 3rd of the chord (outline target note) | Primary guide tone target |
| 7th of the chord (outline target note) | Primary guide tone target |
| Approach notes before the 3rd or 7th | Chromatic targeting (approaches #1–10) |
| Outline contour moving toward resolution | Forward motion toward a destination point |
| ii-V-I voice leading (7th → 3rd) | Klobnak's Example A: Dmin7 (F, C) → G7 (B, F) → Cmaj7 (E, B) — the 7th resolves to the 3rd |

**Strongest targets in an LHS outline by chord:**

For a minor ii-V-i in G minor (Amin7(b5) → D7 → Gmin7):
- On Amin7(b5): target the C (minor 3rd) or G (minor 7th)
- On D7: target the F# (major 3rd) or C (minor 7th) — the F# is the leading tone into G minor
- On Gmin7: target the Bb (minor 3rd) or F (minor 7th)

Voice leading path: C (7th of Amin7b5) → C (becomes 7th of D7) → B (3rd of Gmin7... wait, the Bb) — the resolution is: F# on D7 resolves down a half step to F on Gmin7 (the 7th). This is the core LHS → Klobnak intersection.

**Application rule:** On every ii-V-I, decide which guide tone (3rd or 7th) you are targeting at the resolution chord (the I or i), then construct your approach using one of Klobnak's targeting methods (chromatic, pentatonic, or digital pattern).

### Practice Method

Klobnak's recommended sequence for building targeting awareness:

1. **Identify the target** — before playing, decide which chord tone you are aiming for on the downbeat of each chord change. Start with just the 3rds and 7ths.

2. **Drill the 10 chromatic approaches** in all 12 keys — written in one octave, played across the full range of the instrument, with varying articulations (articulate every other note, every 3rd note, slurred).

3. **Add pentatonic approaches** — play the major pentatonic, Insen scale, and major flat-6th pentatonic in all 12 keys. Target the root, 3rd, and 5th of each tonality using pentatonic runs as the approach.

4. **Apply to a static tonality** — pick one chord (e.g., Cmaj7) and practice targeting its root, 3rd, and 5th using different approach methods. Hear how each approach sounds.

5. **Apply to a ii-V-I** — use the Dmin7 → G7 → Cmaj7 progression and practice targeting the 3rd and 7th of each chord, arriving on the downbeat. This is Example A in the book.

6. **Use recordings for aural reference** — listen to how great improvisers land on chord tones at key moments. The arrival feeling is what you are training.

7. **Mix approaches** — combine chromatic targeting with pentatonic or digital pattern approaches in the same line. Don't over-use any single approach.

## Connection to LHS

Klobnak targeting directly supports **LHS Parts 3 and 4**:

- **Part 3 (Outline Internalization):** Knowing *which notes* in the outline are targets (3rds and 7ths) gives each outline phrase a direction. Instead of running the outline mechanically, you hear where it is headed.

- **Part 4 (Application on Standards):** When placing an outline over a jazz standard, targeting awareness tells you *when* to land. The downbeat of each chord change is the primary target moment. Chromatic approach notes (Klobnak Chapter 2) give you multiple ways to arrive at the outline's guide tone on the beat.

The full integration: **LHS outline** provides the harmonic skeleton (which notes to play across a ii-V-I) + **Klobnak targeting** provides the rhythmic and melodic logic for *how to approach* those notes with direction and purpose. Together they solve the core improv problem: what to play and how to make it sound intentional.
