## Key Concepts

The ii-V-I progression (e.g., Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7 in C major) is the most common and important harmonic movement in jazz. Building a vocabulary for it means learning specific patterns — approach notes, enclosures, digital patterns, and licks — that fit the chords and can be combined fluidly.

The core principle across all vocabulary sources: **arpeggios are the skeleton; approach notes and enclosures are the flesh.** A plain 7th chord arpeggio (e.g., G-B-D-F over G7) outlines the harmony but sounds bare. Adding a single approach note from below (F#→G, A#→B, C#→D, E→F) immediately creates a bebop sound. Adding enclosures (one note above + one below before each arpeggio tone) creates the swirling, forward-motion characteristic of players like Clifford Brown, Cannonball Adderley, and Chad Lefkowitz-Brown.

**Long ii-V-I lines** (Adam Maness approach) address a common problem: players who can play short two-bar ii-V-I licks but run out of ideas when the progression lasts 4 bars (e.g., Cm7 | G7 | Cm7 | F7 | Bbmaj7). The solution is the "walk-down" — a simple guide-tone skeleton over the long ii chord — combined with vocabulary that fills the extra bars without relying on more licks.

**Digital patterns** are numeric templates (e.g., 1-2-3-5, 1-3-5-3, 1-b7-6-1) applied to the scale or arpeggio of each chord. They function as practice vehicles to internalize chord tones in musical rhythmic groupings before adding chromatic decoration.

The CLB etudes (Chad Lefkowitz-Brown's 20 Approach Note Etudes) demonstrate full application: complete solo choruses over standard progressions at performance tempos (168–240 bpm), using approach notes and enclosures as the primary vocabulary throughout.

## Exercises / Reference

### Approach Note Types

All approach note patterns are applied **to each note of the underlying arpeggio** in sequence. Examples given over a G7 arpeggio (G-B-D-F) in C:

**1. Approach from Below (1 note)**
Add one half-step below each arpeggio tone.
- Pattern: [half-step below] → [chord tone]
- Over G7: F#→G, A#→B, C#→D, E→F
- Sound: clean, forward-moving, most common in bebop

**2. Approach from Above (1 note)**
Add one half-step above each arpeggio tone.
- Pattern: [half-step above] → [chord tone]
- Over G7: Ab→G, C→B, Eb→D, Gb→F
- Sound: slightly more tense, opposite feel from below

**3. Two Notes Below (double chromatic)**
Add two chromatic notes below each arpeggio tone.
- Pattern: [whole-step below] [half-step below] → [chord tone]
- Over G7: E-F#→G, G#-A#→B, B-C#→D, D-E→F (using C7 example from source: Bb-B→C, D-D#→E, F-F#→G, Ab-A→Bb)
- Sound: takes more rhythmic space; target fewer notes per bar

**4. Two Notes Above (double chromatic)**
Add two chromatic notes above each arpeggio tone.
- Pattern: [whole-step above] [half-step above] → [chord tone]
- Over G7 (from source D7 example): E-Eb→D, Ab-G→F#, B-Bb→A, D-Db→C
- Sound: strong downward pull into the target note

**5. Enclosure 1 (one above, one below)**
One note above (diatonic or chromatic) + one note below (chromatic) → chord tone.
- Pattern: [above] [below] → [chord tone]
- Over Eb7 (from source): E-D→Eb, Ab-F#→G, B-A→Bb, D-Db→C
- Sound: classic "boxing in" bebop figure; most immediately recognizable enclosure sound

**6. Enclosure 2 (chromatic below + chromatic above)**
One chromatic below + one chromatic above → chord tone.
- Pattern: [half-step below] [chromatic above] → [chord tone]
- Over F7 (from source): E-Gb→F, G#-Bb→A, B-Db→C, D-E→Eb
- Sound: creates a slightly angular feel; effective over dominant chords

**7. Double Enclosure Below (one above + two below)**
One chromatic above + two chromatics below → chord tone.
- Pattern: [above] [two below] → [chord tone]
- Over G7 (from source): Ab-F-F#→G, C-A-A#→B, Eb-C-C#→D, Gb-Eb-E→F
- Sound: takes the most space; use selectively, targeting key chord tones

**8. Double Enclosure Above (two above + one below)**
Two chromatics above + one below → chord tone.
- Pattern: [two above] [below] → [chord tone]
- Over B7 (from source): A#-C#-C→B, D-F-E→D#, F#-G#-G→F#, G#-B-Bb→A
- Sound: descending before resolution; dramatic

### Digital Patterns

Digital patterns are fixed numeric sequences applied to the scale or chord tones of each chord. They create melodic interest without requiring chromatic decoration — they are the vocabulary building block before approach notes. Practice each pattern over a ii-V-I in all 12 keys.

From the Digital Patterns / Jazz Exercises sources (page 46 of Jazz Exercises, labeled by formula):

| Pattern # | Formula | Notes in C major (over Cmaj7) | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1-2-3-5 | C-D-E-G | Most common; natural melodic shape |
| 2 | 1-2-3-5 descending | G-E-D-C | Same pattern reversed |
| 3 | 1-2-3-5 (1235) | Labeled as such in source | Ascending step-skip |
| 4 | 1-3-5-3 (1353) | C-E-G-E | Chord tone emphasis |
| 5 | 1-3-5-b5 (135b5) | C-E-G-Gb | Blues inflection on dominant |
| 6 | 1-b7-1-b7 (1b71b7) | C-Bb-C-Bb | Dominant rocking figure |
| 7 | 1-b7-6-1 (1b761) | C-Bb-A-C | Minor/dominant release |
| 8 | 1-b7-5-1 (1b751) | C-Bb-G-C | Strong chord tone outline |

Additional arpeggio types practiced in the Jazz Exercises source (moving around the key circle):
- Half-diminished 7th (ø7): labeled as φ7
- Diminished 7th (o7): labeled as O7
- Major 7th (M7)
- Dominant 7th (Dom.7)
- Minor 7th (min.7)
- Major 9th (M9): labeled as M9
- Dominant 9th (Dom.9)

**Practice method:** Play each pattern over a single chord type (e.g., G7) for several minutes, then apply to a vamp, then over a full ii-V-I, then through all 12 keys (circle of fifths: C-G-D-A-E-B-F#/Gb-Ab-Eb-Bb-F-C, or chromatically).

### ii-V-I Licks

These are beginner-appropriate verbal descriptions of licks drawn from the sources, transposed to C. Each works as an extension of an LHS outline — the first note connects from the ii chord, and the final note is a chord tone of the I chord.

**Lick 1 — Simple Approach Note into I (from Adam Maness, "long ii-V-I" line 1 in C):**
Over Dm7 (G7) Cmaj7:
- On Dm7: play guide-tone walk (D, C, B, Bb — a descending stepwise line through chord tones and passing tones)
- On G7: chromatic approach from below into chord tones (F#→G, then scale run)
- On Cmaj7: land on the 3rd or 5th (E or G)
Character: smooth, stepwise, sounds like a complete sentence.

**Lick 2 — Enclosure into Resolution (from Maness, line 2 in C):**
Over Dm7 (G7) Cmaj7:
- On Dm7: quarter notes on chord tones with triplet enclosure figure approaching F (3rd of Dm7)
- On G7: use Enclosure 1 or 2 around the 3rd (B) of G7
- On Cmaj7: step down to resolve to 1 or 3
Character: creates tension on the V7 that resolves cleanly.

**Lick 3 — Walk-Down + Bebop Tail (from Maness, line 3 in C):**
Over long ii (2 bars Dm7, G7 Dm7) then F7, Bbmaj7 (key of Bb):
- "Walk-down" over Dm7: whole notes D → C → B → Bb (guide-tone descent)
- This fills the extra bars of a long ii chord without inventing new material
- On G7: scale run or approach figure into Dm7 resolution
- On F7: bebop scale descent to Bbmaj7
Character: the walk-down is the solution to long ii-V-I problems.

**Lick 4 — Approach Below on Each Chord (from Matt Warnock, Ex. 12 over Em7-A7-Dmaj7):**
Over Dm7-G7-Cmaj7 (transposed):
- Dm7: play arpeggio tones with half-step-below approach on each: C#→D, E#→F→A→C
- G7: approach from below on the 3rd and 7th (F#→G, E→F)
- Cmaj7: resolve to chord tones (E or G), step off
Character: most "bebop" sound, flows continuously, works at any tempo.

**Lick 5 — Double Enclosure on V7 (from Matt Warnock, Ex. 24 over Dm7-G7-Cmaj7):**
Over Dm7-G7-Cmaj7:
- Dm7: simple arpeggio or scale approach
- G7: double enclosure below on the root G (Ab-F-F#→G) and the 3rd B (C-A-A#→B)
- Cmaj7: land on 1 or 3, add a chromatic exit note
Character: most dramatic, best saved for the V7 chord landing moment.

### Long ii-V-I Lines

**The core problem:** A ii-V-I that lasts 4 or more bars (e.g., 2 bars of Cm7, then G7-Cm7, then F7, then Bbmaj7) cannot be handled with a standard 2-bar lick — there is a full extra bar of ii chord to fill.

**Adam Maness's solution — the Walk-Down:**
The "walk-down" is a simple guide-tone descent over the ii chord: in Cm7, play C → Bb → Ab → G as whole notes (or elaborated with passing tones). This is a structural device, not a lick — it occupies bars without requiring invention and sets up the V7 arrival with momentum.

The Tenor Madness reference in the Maness PDF shows how Sonny Rollins elaborates this walk-down with bebop rhythmic inflection: adding quarter-note pickups, chromatic passing tones, and syncopated entries into the V7 chord.

**Construction method for a long line:**
1. Establish direction on the ii chord using the walk-down (descending guide tones) or a digital pattern (1-2-3-5 ascending)
2. Apply one approach-note technique on the V7 chord (single approach below is simplest)
3. Resolve cleanly to a chord tone on the I chord
4. Vary the starting rhythm (on the beat, quarter-note pickup, eighth-note pickup) for each chorus

**Three-key practice routine (from Maness):**
Practice each line in Bb major (Cm7-F7-Bbmaj7), F major (Gm7-C7-Fmaj7), and C major (Dm7-G7-Cmaj7) to build transposition fluency before moving to all 12 keys.

## Connection to LHS

The LHS system (Connecting Chords with Linear Harmony / Windau Outlines) builds ii-V-I lines in three stages. Vocabulary connects at each stage:

| LHS Stage | Content | Vocabulary that applies |
|---|---|---|
| Part 1: Basic Outlines | Chord tones only, whole/half note rhythm | Digital patterns (1-2-3-5, 1-3-5-3) internalize chord tone positions before adding movement |
| Part 2: Passing Tones and Pickups | Diatonic passing tones, pickup notes, eighth-note feel | Single approach note from below on key chord tones; simple enclosure figures at phrase entrances |
| Part 3: Embellishment | Full bebop line with chromatic approach, enclosures, bebop scale | All approach note types (1-below, 1-above, 2-below, 2-above, Enclosure 1, Enclosure 2, double enclosures); long ii-V-I walk-down construction |

**Most immediately useful vocabulary for LHS Part 3:**
- Single approach from below (lowest barrier, highest return)
- Enclosure 1 (one diatonic above + one chromatic below) on the 3rd of the V7 chord
- The walk-down figure over the ii chord when it lasts 2+ bars

The Chapter 10 Outline Exercises (Jazz Exercises 2021) are the bridge: playing the 16-measure Dm7-G7-Cmaj7 exercise in all 12 keys is the drill that makes these vocabulary items automatic. The CLB approach note etudes (All the Things You Are, Autumn Leaves, Blues for Bird, Cherokee) are the model of what full vocabulary application sounds like at performance tempo.
