## Key Concepts

**The central problem Ligon solves:** Beginning improvisers play harmonically vague lines that float on top of the harmony rather than connecting through it. Sonny Rollins praised Dizzy Gillespie for being able to really *connect* the chords — Ligon's book is a systematic study of how that connection works.

**Linear harmony defined:** Melodic lines that connect chords smoothly using significant tones (thirds and sevenths) with careful rhythmic placement. Sevenths are dissonant and restless — they resolve downward to the third of the following chord. Thirds are consonant and stable — they appear on strong beats as arrival points. The cycle is: consonance (3rd) → dissonance (7th) → resolution (3rd of next chord).

**The note-narrowing principle:** From 12 chromatic pitches, eliminate the root (bass has it) and pitches outside the key center — you're left with 6 usable pitches. Of those, the third and seventh of each chord are the most harmonically specific. For Dm7-G7-Cmaj7 in C: the best target notes are F (3rd of Dm7), B (3rd of G7), and E (3rd of Cmaj7). The path F→B→E is the 7→3 resolution engine.

**Why only three outlines:** After transcribing hundreds of solos, Ligon found that virtually all harmonically specific lines over ii-V-I reduce to one of three skeletal frameworks — a descending stepwise line, an ascending arpeggiated line, or a descending arpeggiated line. These are public domain structures used by everyone from Bach to Brecker.

**Chord-tone targeting:** The third and seventh are the guide tones — they define chord quality and drive voice leading. The 7th of one chord resolves stepwise down to the 3rd of the next. This is not a rule to follow but a structural fact of tonal harmony. Playing to these targets is what creates the feeling of "connecting" the changes.

## Exercises / Reference

### The Three Outlines in C

All outlines shown over Dm7 (ii) → G7 (V) → Cmaj7 (I).

**How to read the note sequences:** Notes in parentheses indicate the outline skeleton. The 7→3 resolution happens at each barline: C (7th of Dm7) → B (3rd of G7) → E (3rd of Cmaj7).

| Outline | Dm7 (ii) | G7 (V) | Cmaj7 (I) | Character |
|---------|----------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | F–E–D–C | B–A–G–F | E (whole note) | Descending stepwise; most common; smooth scalar motion from 3rd of ii down through V to 3rd of I |
| 2 | D–F–A–C | B–D–F–A | E (whole note) | Ascending arpeggio of ii chord (1-3-5-7); 7th resolves to 3rd of V; "Round Midnight" outline |
| 3 | A–F–D–C | B–D–F–A | G (whole note) | Descending arpeggio of ii chord (5-3-1); 7th below resolves up to 3rd of V; line reverses direction and ascends to 5th of I |

**Outline 1 detail:** Start on F (3rd of Dm7), walk stepwise down: F–E–D–C. C is the 7th of Dm7 and arrives as the last note before the barline. On beat 1 of G7, C resolves to B (3rd of G7) — this is the core 7→3 resolution. Line continues down: B–A–G–F. F is the 7th of G7, resolves to E (3rd of Cmaj7).

**Outline 2 detail:** Called the "Round Midnight" outline (appears twice in the A section). Begins on D (root of Dm7), ascends the arpeggio: D–F–A–C. C (7th) at the top resolves down to B (3rd of G7). After landing on B, the line may continue down the scale to E, or arpeggiate up the G7 chord (B–D–F–A) resolving to E on Cmaj7. Extensions to the 9th (high E on Dm7) are common.

**Outline 3 detail:** Begins on A (5th of Dm7), descends: A–F–D. Then drops to C below the chord (the 7th, played below as the restless tone), which usually falls on the upbeat. C resolves up to B (3rd of G7) on the downbeat — this upbeat-to-downbeat resolution is the signature move of Outline 3. After B, the line reverses and arpeggiates up (B–D–F–A), landing on G (5th of Cmaj7). Variation: continue down from B to E (3rd of Cmaj7) instead.

**Minor key versions:** The ii chord becomes iiø7 (Dø7 in C minor), V7 gains a flat 9 (G7b9), and the I becomes minor (Cm). All three outlines work in minor — keep the key signature and raise the leading tone on V7.

### 7→3 Resolution

The structural engine behind all three outlines. The seventh of each chord resolves stepwise downward to the third of the following chord.

**In C major over ii-V-I:**
- C (7th of Dm7) → B (3rd of G7): half step down
- F (7th of G7) → E (3rd of Cmaj7): half step down

**Why this works:** Sevenths are dissonant — they create tension and pull downward. The third is consonant and stable. When a line lands on a seventh near a barline and resolves to the third on the downbeat of the next chord, the listener hears the harmony "click into place." This is what Rollins meant by connecting the chords.

**Rhythmic placement matters:** The seventh typically falls on beat 4 (or the upbeat of 4) and resolves on beat 1 of the next chord. Ligon is explicit: "the seventh creates tension and the desire to hear the resolution... it is often the last note played before moving to the next chord."

**In other keys (same principle):**
- Bb7 → Ebmaj7: Ab → G (7th of Bb7 → 3rd of Ebmaj7)
- Am7 → D7: G → F# (7th of Am7 → 3rd of D7)
- Em7 → A7: D → C# (7th of Em7 → 3rd of A7)

### Embellishment Techniques

Listed in order of complexity. Start simple — the outline in its bare form is already musical.

**Passing Tones:** Chromatic and diatonic steps between essential tones. Flatted notes pull downward; sharped notes pull upward. The tone between 5th and 7th of the ii chord is *not* recommended as a passing tone — save the 7th for the V chord; using it early gives away the resolution.

**Neighbor Tones (UNT/LNT):** The diatonic upper neighbor (UNT) and chromatic lower neighbor (LNT) surround an essential tone. Common practice from Mozart to Parker: use UNT above and LNT (half step) below. Example over C chord: D is UNT to C, B is LNT; F is UNT to E, D# is LNT.

**Arpeggiated Tones:** Leaping to other chord tones adds angularity. The 5th of the ii chord played in a lower octave is the most common added arpeggiated tone. Elaborates the line without leaving the harmonic framework.

**Extensions:** The arpeggio on Outline 2 is frequently extended to the 9th (and sometimes 11th) before descending to the 3rd of V7. This is common in Evans, Brown, and others.

**Chromatic Approaches:** A diatonic tone + chromatic passing tone leading to an essential tone. The 3rd of the ii chord may be approached from a whole step above (E→Eb→F on Dm7) or from below (C#→D on Dm7) or from both (encircling). The 3rd of V is often approached from below with diatonic + chromatic tone. The 3rd of I is often approached with a chromatic passing tone.

**Octave Displacement:** Placing one or more tones in another octave. Creates dramatic leaps and interest. Most common: leap from 3rd up to 9th (or flat 9th) after arrival on the target note. Leaps most often occur after the main guide tone on the downbeat, then continue in the new register. Rule: leap from downbeat to upbeat, not over a barline, rarely from weak beat to strong beat.

**Encircling Tones (C.E.S.H.):** Both neighbor tones (upper and lower) used before the essential tone. Also known as Jerry Coker's "Chromatic Elaboration of Static Harmony" — the descending chromatic motion D→C#→C→B from root of Dm7 to 3rd of G7, with diatonic tones played in between the chromatic descending notes (implies a compound melody).

**Anticipation:** Arriving at the next chord's target tone *before* the barline. Creates a small dissonance that resolves when the harmony catches up.

**Delayed Resolution:** Suspending the resolution *past* the barline — arriving at the target tone late. The melody catches up to the harmony rather than the reverse.

**Sawtooth:** Consistent up-and-down movement like the teeth of a saw. Often involves a pivot or arpeggiated note: G–D–F–D–E (over G7–Cmaj7).

**Iteration:** Repeated notes create eighth-note motion where the outline implies only quarter notes. Used across centuries (see Bach Brandenburg Concerto in D).

**Rhythmic Devices:** Displacement, augmentation, diminution, anticipation, complex subdivisions. Vary rhythmic placement of the skeleton to change the feel without changing the notes.

### Practice Sequence

Ligon's recommended progression from outline to improvisation (drawn from Chapter IX "Get It In Your Playing" and Chapter X "Outline Exercises"):

1. **Write it out.** On staff paper, write all three outlines in all 12 keys, identifying chord names (Dm7–G7–C, etc.). This forces you to know the notes, not just the shapes.

2. **Sing first.** Practice singing all three outlines before playing them. Ligon is explicit: ear training must accompany technique. Use the outlines to sing modulations to closely related keys, making sure to sing the necessary accidentals.

3. **Play slowly.** If you want to play at 300 bpm, start at 60–96 bpm. Make it swing at every tempo as you work your way up. "Practice slowly" is not a tip — it is the method.

4. **One outline at a time.** Don't mix outlines yet. Drill Outline 1 in all 12 keys until it is automatic. Then Outline 2, then Outline 3. Isolate before combining.

5. **Apply embellishments one at a time.** Take Outline 1. Add only passing tones. Then only neighbor tones. Then only chromatic approaches. Apply each device in isolation before combining.

6. **Limit and drill.** Ligon's "practice by limiting" principle from Comprehensive Technique: play a tune using only broken chords and arpeggios; or only scale step motion; or using only one outline. Limitation forces mastery.

7. **Apply to tunes.** Use short 4- and 8-bar phrases from standards. Identify the ii-V-I progressions. Play outline 1 through the changes. Then outline 2. Then fragment and combine.

8. **Integrated improvisation.** Mix outlines freely, use two or more within a phrase, combine with harmonic generalization and melodic paraphrase. This is the endpoint, not the starting point.

**Daily practice order (from Comprehensive Technique Typical Day):**
1. Routine warm-up (scales, arpeggios, broken chords) in 2-4 keys
2. Isolate technical exercises
3. Isolate melodic minor/diminished scale exercises as needed
4. Apply exercises to tunes
5. Practice outlines on tunes
6. Learn new tunes

## Connection to LHS

- **The outlines are the LHS skeleton.** Every line in the Linear Harmony System (LHS) is, at its core, one of Ligon's three outlines with embellishments applied on top. Knowing which outline you're in tells you where you are harmonically at all times.

- **7→3 is the LHS voice-leading engine.** The guide-tone resolution (C→B, F→E in C major) is the through-line that makes ii-V-I lines sound connected. All LHS exercise sequences target these resolutions on strong beats.

- **Embellishment sequence maps directly to LHS levels.** LHS Level 1 = bare outlines. Level 2 = passing tones and neighbor tones added. Level 3 = chromatic approaches and encircling. Level 4 = full embellishment toolkit with rhythmic variation.

- **Sing-first method is the LHS ear training protocol.** Ligon's insistence on singing before playing aligns with the LHS approach of internalizing the sound of each outline before executing it on the horn.

- **"Practice by limiting" = LHS focused repetition.** Ligon's drill structure (one device at a time, one outline at a time, slow tempos first) is the operational model for LHS daily practice sessions.

- **Outline 1 for trumpet:** The descending stepwise motion of Outline 1 sits naturally in the trumpet's comfortable upper-middle register. For trumpet in Bb, the C major outline (concert) sounds a major second higher — adjust target notes accordingly (G→F#→A in concert = A→G#→B for Bb trumpet).
