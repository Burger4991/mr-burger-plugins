# LHS Ear Training Protocol

**Core rule:** If you can't sing it, you can't play it with intention.

Ear training in the Linear Harmony System connects the inner ear to the instrument. Every exercise begins with singing — the voice is the most direct link between what you hear internally and what you produce. Playing comes after singing, never before.

---

## Outlines

Outlines are the melodic skeletons of harmony. Each outline traces the essential tones of a chord or progression.

| Outline | Notes (in C) | Function |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Outline 1 | C E G C | Major triad arpeggio — root position |
| Outline 2 | C E G B | Major 7th arpeggio |
| Outline 3 | C D E G | Scale fragment — pentatonic feel |
| Outline 4 | C E G A | Major 6th arpeggio |
| Outline 5 | C Eb G Bb | Minor 7th / dominant feel |

Start with Outline 1 in every new key. Do not advance to the next outline until you can sing Outline 1 from memory in at least 4 keys.

---

## Three Levels

### Level 1 — Sing-Then-Play (Foundation)

**Goal:** Match what you sing to what you play. No gap between intention and execution.

**Steps:**
1. Set metronome to 60–72 BPM
2. Play the root note on your instrument to establish the key
3. Sing the outline slowly — one note per beat, out loud
4. Without stopping, play the same outline on your instrument
5. Compare: did you play exactly what you sang?
6. If yes — repeat in a new key. If no — sing it again before replaying.

**Success criterion:** You play the note you intended before your fingers move. No searching, no correcting.

**Trumpet adaptation:** Stay in the comfortable range (C4–G4 for comeback players). Use only keys where the outline fits within your current range. Sing in whatever octave is comfortable — the pitch class matters, not the octave.

**Guitar adaptation:** Use shell voicings you already know to play the root/chord first, then sing the outline over it. Arpeggiate the chord, then sing the outline tones.

**Voice-only variation:** Skip step 4. Sing the outline, then check each note against the instrument one at a time. This is a valid standalone exercise.

---

### Level 2 — Sing With Chord (Harmonic Context)

**Goal:** Hear each outline tone against the harmony, not in isolation.

**Steps:**
1. Set metronome to 60–80 BPM
2. Play or strum the chord (or have a drone/backing track)
3. While the chord sustains, sing each outline tone — listen for how it sits against the harmony
4. Name the tension or resolution you feel on each note (e.g., "the 3rd wants to stay, the 7th wants to resolve")
5. Now play the outline on your instrument while the chord sustains (loop a backing track or use a sustain pedal)
6. Sing and play simultaneously — voice doubles the instrument

**Success criterion:** You can describe the quality of each note against the chord (stable, tension, leading) before you play it.

**Trumpet adaptation:** Use a drone app or backing track since trumpet can't sustain chords. Sing first, then play over the drone.

**Guitar adaptation:** Strum the chord, let it ring, sing the outline over it. Then finger the outline notes on the fretboard while the chord shape is still held (where possible).

---

### Level 3 — Full Audiation (Internalization)

**Goal:** Hear the outline internally — without singing out loud or playing — then execute it perfectly on the first attempt.

**Steps:**
1. Set metronome to 72–92 BPM
2. Play the root note once to establish the key
3. Silently audiate the full outline — hear every note in your mind's ear
4. When you can hear the entire outline internally, play it on your instrument in tempo
5. Check: was every note exactly what you heard internally?
6. If any note was wrong, drop back to Level 1 for that outline in that key

**Success criterion:** First-take accuracy. You play the outline correctly on the first attempt without any correction, hesitation, or searching.

**Trumpet adaptation:** Finger the valves silently while audiating (no air) before playing. This bridges the mental image to the physical motion.

**Guitar adaptation:** Visualize the fretboard positions while audiating. See the shape, hear the sound, then play.

---

## Practice Sequence

A complete ear training session follows this arc:

1. **Warm-up** (2 min) — Sing a major scale ascending and descending in the key you'll work in. No instrument.
2. **Level 1 work** (5 min) — Sing-then-play the target outline in 3–4 keys
3. **Level 2 work** (5 min) — Same outline with harmonic context in 2 keys
4. **Level 3 attempt** (3 min) — Audiate-then-play in 1–2 keys. If it fails, note it and return to Level 1 next session.
5. **Cool-down** (1 min) — Sing the outline one final time, slowly, with eyes closed

**Total: ~15 minutes.** This is enough. Ear training is high-concentration work — longer sessions produce diminishing returns.

---

## Key Progression

Work through keys in this order (circle of fourths, most common keys first):

C → F → Bb → Eb → Ab → G → D → A → E → B

For comeback trumpet players: start with C, F, G, Bb — these cover the majority of beginning and intermediate repertoire.

For beginner guitar: start with C, G, D, A, E — open-string friendly keys.

## When to Advance

- **Level 1 → Level 2:** You can sing-then-play the outline correctly in 4+ keys without hesitation
- **Level 2 → Level 3:** You can name the harmonic quality of each note and sing/play simultaneously in 3+ keys
- **Outline N → Outline N+1:** You can do Level 2 work with the current outline in at least 4 keys

There is no rush. Depth beats breadth. One outline mastered across all keys is worth more than five outlines attempted shallowly.
