## Key Concepts

Herbert L. Clarke (1867–1945) was the premier American cornetist of the early 20th century, soloist with the Gilmore, Innes, and Sousa bands for 30+ years. He opened the Clarke School for the Cornet in 1906 and spent the rest of his career teaching the "Clarke Method," built on one core principle: **keep the embouchure relaxed and pliable at all times.**

Clarke did not believe in brute force or volume. His system is built on "moving long tones" — exercises that develop breath control and embouchure strength through flexibility rather than pressure. His instructions are direct: always play softly, rest before the lips fatigue, never hold the lips rigid. Playing after the lips are tired weakens them; stopping before fatigue and resting keeps them fresh and responsive. The softer one practices, the stronger the embouchure becomes.

The four books form a logical sequence. **Elementary Studies** (1909) builds first tone, mouthpiece placement, and basic embouchure from scratch — 30 graded lessons, 116 exercises, in duet form with a teacher part. **Technical Studies** (1912) develops finger and tongue dexterity through scale patterns, arpeggios, and melodic sequences through all keys — considered among the best-known exercises for this purpose; patterns appear universally in all types of music. **Characteristic Studies** (1915) are 24 advanced etudes covering all major and relative minor keys (C major through B major), emphasizing every imaginable articulation pattern and rhythmic assignment. **Setting Up Drills** (Calisthenic Exercises) is Clarke's personal daily warm-up routine — 38 exercises in four groups combining all of the flexibility, articulation, breathing, and range-building ideas from the three study books, completable in 30 minutes.

Clarke identifies seven points that must each be developed individually: (1) the lips, which vibrate to produce the tone; (2) the muscles of lips and face, trained for every interval across the compass; (3) the tongue, which produces all articulations and works with the lip muscles on contracting and relaxing; (4) the left hand, holding the instrument firmly with a supple wrist; (5) the fingers of the right hand, each trained separately; (6) the air, which passes through the lips to cause vibration; (7) the wind-power, the most important factor of all, controlling tone for both loud and soft playing across the full range. Mouthpiece placement is centered — equal on upper and lower lip — so low tones are rich, middle register is clear, and high tones are brilliant.

Tongue placement: "tu" in the middle register (tongue at the base of the upper front teeth, backward motion like a valve). High register shifts naturally toward "te," low toward "tu." Never place the tongue between the teeth ("thu"). Single tongue must be mastered before attempting double or triple.

The Hickman edition adds crucial practice guidance: practice in several short daily sessions of 10–20 minutes rather than one long session; mix the tessituras of exercises rather than going through all keys in order (so the embouchure is ready for any register at any time); always use a metronome and keep a log of tempos mastered; pay strict attention to tone quality and pitch centering, not just finger speed.

## Exercises / Reference

### Clarke Overview

|--------|------|------|-------|----------|

| Second | Technical Studies | 1912 | Finger and tongue dexterity, all keys | Scale/arpeggio/melodic sequence patterns, chromatically organized |
| Third | Characteristic Studies | 1915 | Full articulation, advanced etudes | 24 etudes (C major / A minor through B major / G# minor), tonguing method |
| Fourth | Setting Up Drills | — | Daily warm-up, endurance, comprehensive | 38 exercises in 4 groups, completable in 30 min |

### Essential Studies

**First Series: Elementary Studies — Lessons 1–16**
The foundation. Begin on whichever first note speaks more easily (C or G, open tones). Start with whole notes in the middle register, fingerings labeled. Each lesson ascends a step or two as the lips grow stronger. Lessons 1–16 are appropriate for most players; Lessons 17–30 progress very rapidly and are more suited to players with several years of experience. Key operating rules from Clarke: play each exercise many hundreds of times; use a metronome; always play softly; rest the moment any fatigue is noticeable.

**Second Series: Technical Studies — First Study**
The signature Clarke study. Contains 26 exercises running chromatically through all keys. Hickman's practice schedule: Day 1 — three practice sessions per day, each mixing different exercises and articulations, playing each as many times as possible on one breath. Day 2 — different exercise groupings. Days 3/5 repeat Day 1 faster; Days 4/6 repeat Day 2 faster; Day 7 — Etude I with various articulations. The objective is to mix registers so the embouchure is never locked into one tessitura. Vary dynamics somewhat so articulations are clean at all volume levels.

**Second Series: Technical Studies — Studies 2–5**
Progress through increasingly complex melodic patterns. The editor notes these studies can be altered to develop jazz vocabulary by practicing them in modes: Second Study in whole tone or diminished, Fifth Study in Dorian. This makes Technical Studies directly applicable to jazz context.

**Third Series: Characteristic Studies — Tonguing Method (Single, Double, Triple)**
The Characteristic Studies open with Clarke's "Remarks on Tongueing" and systematic tonguing exercises before the 24 etudes. Single tonguing on open G, staccato, metronome 120 building to 144. Double tonguing: "Te-Ke" (high), "Ta-Ka" (middle), "Tu-Ku" (low) — "Ke/Ka/Ku" must equal the attack of "Te/Ta/Tu" in clarity. Triple tonguing: "Ta-Ta-Ka," "Te-Te-Ke," "Tu-Tu-Ku" — but the editor recommends a double-tongue grouping for extended triplet passages at maximum speed (TKT KTK pattern). The 24 etudes themselves are meant for advanced players with well-developed embouchure; they encompass every articulation pattern and rhythmic assignment.

**Fourth Series: Setting Up Drills — Group One (Ex. 1–8)**
Clarke's personal morning warm-up. Chromatic flexibility exercises in all enharmonic keys, each repeated four times in one breath at Met. = 80. The lips relax on descent and contract on ascent. Rest between groups. Group One takes about 4 minutes. This is the most accessible entry point from Setting Up Drills.

**Fourth Series: Setting Up Drills — Group Three (Ex. 14–37)**
Scale exercises for coordinating tongue and fingers across all keys. Each played in one breath. The most substantial technical content in the daily drill — takes about 20 minutes. Lines over upper-register passages marked "omit" can be skipped according to embouchure strength.

**Fourth Series: Setting Up Drills — Group Four (Ex. 38)**
Single exercise, very staccato, for rapid single-tongue articulation. Start Met. = 112, build toward Met. = 144. Entire exercise in one breath. Dedicated tongue-speed builder.

### Comeback Player Selection

Current range C4–C5, embouchure rebuilding. Priority order:

**Start here — Elementary Studies, Lessons 1–10.**
No exceptions, regardless of prior experience. Play whole notes in the middle register, softly, metronome at 60. The goal is to re-establish the embouchure without strain, not to demonstrate former ability. Rest every 2 minutes. If any fatigue is felt, stop — Clarke is explicit that playing through fatigue sets progress back by weeks.

**Progress to — Elementary Studies, Lessons 11–16.**
Add range gradually toward one octave (C4–C5) as lessons permit. If a note in the upper portion requires visible effort or sounds pinched, drop back. The range will return on its own timetable.

**Add — Setting Up Drills, Group One (Ex. 1–3 only), slow tempo.**
Once Lesson 10 is reliable, begin a brief Group One session: just the first two or three exercises, at Met. = 60 rather than 80. This re-trains the relax/contract motion without demanding range. Clarke describes this as appropriate for rebuilding the "machine." Rest between each exercise.

**Then add — Technical Studies, First Study, low keys only.**
When air support and finger coordination are solid, begin the First Study starting in the lowest-key exercises and working only through those that stay comfortably within C4–C5. Do not push through higher keys until the range is restored naturally through the Elementary Studies work.

**Hold — Characteristic Studies and Setting Up Drills Group Two/Three.**
Group Two of Setting Up Drills explicitly cautions: "I would not advise practice of this second group unless you have an excellent embouchure and can play the higher tones with ease, and without strain of any kind." The Characteristic Studies are advanced etudes; they assume a fully functional embouchure and will cause harm if attempted too early in a comeback.

The throughline: soft practice builds the embouchure faster than loud. Short sessions with rest build it faster than long sessions without rest. Patience is not optional — it is the method.

### Integration with Adam

The William Adam / James Stamp lineage (long tones → flexibility → scales → etudes) maps cleanly to Clarke:

| Adam Phase | Clarke Component | Notes |
|------------|-----------------|-------|
| Long tones / buzz | Setting Up Drills, Group One (Ex. 1–8) | Chromatic, soft, relax/contract; 4 min |
| Flexibility | Setting Up Drills, Group Two (when range supports it) | Only with solid embouchure |
| Scales / technical | Technical Studies, First Study (or Studies 2–5) | 10–20 min; mix tessituras, vary articulations |
| Jazz vocabulary | Technical Studies in modes (whole tone, diminished, Dorian) | Editor-recommended adaptation |
| Etudes / musicality | Characteristic Studies (when range and endurance support it) | Full articulation; advanced players |

For a comeback player the sequence is compressed: Group One warm-up (2–3 exercises, abbreviated) → Elementary Studies lesson work (10 min max) → full rest. No jazz application until the embouchure handles the demands without fatigue.

## Connection to LHS

Clarke's seven-point breakdown (lips, face muscles, tongue, left hand, right-hand fingers, air, wind-power) provides a direct curriculum framework for the LHS trumpet track: each point is a teachable concept with a corresponding Clarke exercise series. The "moving long tones" philosophy — building strength through flexibility rather than pressure — should underlie all beginning and intermediate trumpet pedagogy in the program. Clarke's insistence on short sessions with built-in rest is especially relevant for young players who tend to over-practice and break down their embouchures before they develop. The Technical Studies' chromatic organization also makes them a natural bridge to jazz — students who internalize the patterns across all keys have the finger vocabulary for any key center.
