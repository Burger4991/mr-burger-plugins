## Key Concepts

Klobnak's routine operates on a core principle: technique and jazz vocabulary are not separate things. Standard brass routines (long tones, Arban, Clarke) develop physical fundamentals in isolation from music. Klobnak integrates both — every technical exercise is also a harmony exercise and a vocabulary exercise at the same time.

The spine of the routine is **Clarke II with a Twist** — a riff on the Herbert Clarke Technical Study No. 2 pattern, played through all 12 keys. Clarke II in its original form is a scalar finger exercise. Klobnak adds a **chromatic targeting approach** (labeled "chromatically targeting the root" in the score) where chromatic neighbor tones approach chord roots as a jazz player would, not as a classical player would. This turns a mechanical scale exercise into bebop phrasing practice. The pattern hits all 12 keys via a cycle-of-fourths/fifths path, with a mandatory rest of **4 bars longer than you played** between each key. This rest-to-play ratio is structural, not optional — it's built into the music. For a comeback player with embouchure rebuilding concerns, this ratio is essential.

The routine separates into two day-types — **Major Day** and **Minor Day** — which rotate to cover the full harmonic landscape. Major Day focuses on major scales and major ii-V-I harmony. Minor Day uses the harmonic minor scale for the Clarke II patterns and adds **diminished work** and **motion in minor thirds** (symmetrical diminished vocabulary) as distinct sections not present on Major Day.

The final section of every version of the routine is an open instruction: **"Work on targeting, licks, or etudes to finish"** (8 bars indicated). This is the jazz vocabulary application window — where Klobnak expects you to apply what you've warmed up to actual musical content: licks, transcriptions, etudes, or free application over changes.

For a player in the C4–C5 range doing embouchure rebuilding, this routine is well-suited because: the built-in rest ratios prevent overblowing; the Clarke II patterns stay in the middle register for the bulk of the work; the chromatic targeting vocabulary does not require high-range chops to be musically meaningful; and the structure is modular enough to truncate without losing the concept.

## Exercises / Reference

### Routine Overview

Klobnak's routine is designed to follow a standard warm-up, not replace it. His note explicitly reads: **"The exercises below are meant to be done after your normal warm-up."** This positions it clearly in the session:

```
[William Adam fundamentals / mouthpiece work / long tones / flow studies]
        ↓
[Klobnak routine — Major Day or Minor Day]
        ↓
[Free application: licks, etudes, transcriptions, tunes]
```

Adam covers the physical fundamentals — embouchure, air, centered tone, flexibility. Klobnak picks up where Adam leaves off and applies that physical setup to jazz harmonic vocabulary. They are complementary, not redundant.

---

### Major Day Structure

Labeled "A Jason Klobnak Jazz Trumpet Practice Routine — Major Day." Performed after normal warm-up. Moves through all 12 major keys in a cycle-of-fourths pattern (Bb → Eb → Ab → Db → Gb → B → E → A → D → G → C → F, or equivalent).

**Components in order:**

1. **Clarke II with a Twist** — major scale-based technical pattern, played through all 12 keys
   - Each key: play the pattern, then rest 4 bars longer than you played
   - Chromatic targeting of the root embedded in the pattern (chromatic approach from above and below before landing on root)
   - This is the bulk of the routine — physically the most demanding section

2. **Prep exercise for chromatic targeting** (m. 261 in the full score)
   - Dedicated chromatic approach-note drill
   - Isolates the targeting gesture before the more chromatic sections

3. **Chromatic targeting studies** (extended, mm. 265–291)
   - Dense chromatic lines; two distinct blocks with rest between
   - These are long, slurred chromatic lines — more upper-register demand; scale back or stop here if embouchure fatigues

4. **Flexibilities with 4ths** (m. 299)
   - Lip slur flexibility patterns built on fourths rather than scalar motion
   - Pairs with the LHS emphasis on quartal harmony; fourths as structural intervals, not just passing tones

5. **Ascending 4ths** (m. 321)
   - Melodic pattern ascending through perfect fourths
   - Builds quartal vocabulary and interval accuracy simultaneously

6. **Free application — "Work on targeting, licks, or etudes to finish"** (8 bars indicated, m. 341)
   - Open time; structured intentional practice, not noodling

---

### Minor Day Structure

Same architecture as Major Day, with these differences:

1. **Clarke II with a Twist** — uses the **harmonic minor scale** (note in score: "This can be done on any minor scale. We are using the harmonic minor.")
   - Same chromatic targeting of the root, same rest ratios
   - Harmonic minor gives the raised 7th (leading tone), which maps directly to dominant-function harmony — V7 in a minor ii-V-i

2. **Moving in minor 3rds** (m. 209 in minor day score)
   - Symmetrical motion through minor thirds — the diminished/octatonic principle
   - Lines move through all 4 symmetrical positions of a diminished seventh chord
   - This is the same interval logic as the altered scale (Bolvin's Jazz Warm-Up) applied melodically

3. **Diminished work** (m. 247)
   - Extended section of fully diminished and half-diminished vocabulary
   - Triplet-heavy phrasing; chromatic neighbor motion; cycle through all 3 diminished chord positions
   - Directly applicable to dominant substitution (tritone subs, #9 chords)

4. **Free application** — same closing instruction as Major Day

### Jazz Warm-Up (The-Jazz-Warm-Up-2 — Eric Bolvin)

Note: this PDF is authored by **Eric Bolvin** (bolvinmusic.com), not Klobnak. It is a companion/supplementary resource that covers overlapping territory and can substitute for or precede the Klobnak routine on lighter days.

**Three sections:**

**I. Altered Scale Flow Studies**
- One page per day; rotates through keys (F major, G major, Eb major shown in PDF)
- Over a major ii-V-I: the altered scale (diminished/whole-tone) is used over the V chord
- Altered scale content: root, M3, b7, plus all altered tones — b5, #5, b9, #9
- Format: 13 lines of ii-V-I flow studies per page; each line is a variation on the approach
- Long note values (half notes); low register; minimal physical demand — good for embouchure rebuilding
- Use with a play-along to hear the lines in context

**II. Intervals 1 — Fourths in 1/2 Steps (Bolvin)**
- Three exercises: 1A, 1B, 1C (increasingly difficult)
- 1A: fourth intervals moving chromatically; tongued; may continue to high C
- 1B: same concept, different contour
- 1C: Bolvin's benchmark — "If you can play each half in one breath with no misses, then you are ready for anything"
- Practice models included at bottom of page
- Source: Bolvin's *Modern Jazz Trumpet Method* (20 interval studies available)
- Connects directly to LHS quartal framework — fourths as structural vocabulary, not ornament

**III. Range Builder 1 — Aeolian Pattern with Passing Tones**
- Swing articulation (jazz feel); slur the "and" to the beat
- Aeolian (natural minor) pattern with chromatic passing tones; 4/4 swing feel
- 12 keys; "Continue as high as possible" instruction
- Alternate faster articulation: slur first three notes of each group of four, tongue the fourth
- Source: Bolvin's *Be-Bop Range Book*
- For embouchure rebuilding: stop at the top of comfortable range; do not chase high notes

### Connection to LHS

| Klobnak/Bolvin Element | LHS Connection |
|---|---|
| Chromatic targeting of the root | LHS guide tone approach — landing on the 3rd, 7th, or root of each chord via chromatic enclosure |
| Altered scale flow studies (Bolvin) | LHS altered dominant vocabulary — b5/#5/b9/#9 extensions over V7 |
| ii-V-I format throughout | LHS core progression; every Klobnak exercise is implicitly a ii-V-I drill |
| Fourths — flexibilities and ascending 4ths | LHS quartal outlines — stacked fourths as the skeleton of modern jazz lines |
| Diminished work (minor day) | LHS diminished substitution — fully dim7 as a dominant sub or tritone sub approach |
| Moving in minor 3rds (minor day) | LHS symmetrical diminished/octatonic system — same 4 keys, 3 positions each |
| Interval study in fourths (Bolvin 1A–1C) | LHS quartal melodic vocabulary — fourths as structural building blocks |

The chromatic targeting technique is the most direct LHS connection. In LHS, outlining means defining a chord by hitting its key tones (guide tones) in a logical voice-leading sequence. Klobnak's "chromatically targeting the root" is exactly that principle applied to one tone — the root — as a physical habit. Combining both: use LHS to determine which notes to target (3rd, 7th, root); use Klobnak's chromatic enclosure technique to arrive at them.

## Connection to LHS

Klobnak functions as the **physical delivery mechanism** for LHS vocabulary. LHS identifies what to play over chord changes (outlines, ii-V-I vocabulary, guide tones, altered extensions). Klobnak trains the muscle memory and ear to navigate those tones through all 12 keys, at tempo, with jazz articulation and phrasing. Without Klobnak (or equivalent), LHS vocabulary stays theoretical. Without LHS (or equivalent), Klobnak stays physically useful but harmonically unanchored. Together they form a complete jazz language acquisition loop: LHS tells you what, Klobnak trains you how.

For the current embouchure rebuilding phase (C4–C5 range), the Bolvin Altered Scale Flow Studies are the lowest-demand LHS-relevant exercises available — long tones, low register, harmonic context, no range pressure. Start there. Add Klobnak's Clarke II (major day, first few keys only) as embouchure strengthens. The chromatic targeting and fourth-interval sections come later.
