## Key Concepts

### Who Was William Adam

William Alexander Adam (1917–2013) taught applied trumpet at Indiana University's Jacobs School of Music for 42 years (1946–1988), then continued teaching privately until six weeks before his death at age 96. His students occupy positions across major orchestras, jazz bands, studio sessions, and university professorships worldwide — including Jerry Hey, Larry Hall, and Chris Botti. He received the ITG Lifetime Achievement Award in 2004.

Adam resisted writing a method book because he believed his process required one-on-one attention. He evaluated each student individually, diagnosed weaknesses, and created a tailored path — not a fixed formula. His teaching was described by students as almost entirely nonverbal: he played, you imitated, you improved without knowing how.

### The Core Framework: Causes and Results

Adam's central principle is that most players have the relationship between causes and results backwards. Beginners try to do things "correctly" — set the embouchure, control the tongue, manage the air — and hope that good sound results. Adam flipped this completely.

**The result is the sound. The causes take care of themselves.**

When you pursue the vivid imagined ideal sound with total commitment, the body adjusts to produce it. This is not a metaphor — Adam studied anatomy, visited the IU Medical School, and grounded this in his understanding of physics, physiology, and psychology (he was a few credits shy of a master's in psychology). The physical events that produce a great tone happen automatically when the mental goal is clear enough. Trying to consciously manage those physical events interferes with the process.

Practically: do not think about embouchure, tongue position, aperture, or muscle engagement while playing. Think only about the sound you want to hear. "The only awareness you should have is of the music — you're not aware of me, or the trumpet in your hand, only the music."

### The Trumpet Player's Mind: 90 / 9 / 1

Adam drew a pie chart he showed students repeatedly:

- **~90%: Imagination of the sound.** This is the dominant thought at all times. He called it "THE sound" — a vividly conceived, ideal, opulent tone. Not vague beauty, but a specific model heard in your inner ear so clearly it triggers the body to produce it.
- **~9%: The breath.** Always present, always energized, but secondary to the sound concept. "Take a big breath" was one of his most frequent spoken reminders.
- **~1%: Everything else.** Embouchure feel, tongue position, posture, hardware, the conductor, the person next to you. These are irrelevant when the sound concept is dominant and the breath is trained.

### Kinesthetic Response

The mechanism linking mind to body is what Adam called kinesthetic response: physical and motor actions are triggered by thought. When the thought is clear enough and specific enough, the body follows without conscious instruction. This is why obsessing over embouchure feel is counterproductive — it moves thought away from the sound and into the 1% zone, where the body can only handle one thing at a time. When frustrated players stubbornly focus on feel rather than sound, they guarantee continued frustration.

The corollary: stay in the "aural." Trumpet playing must always be in the aural — listening, imagining, hearing. The moment "feel" becomes focal, aural becomes peripheral, and problems multiply.

### Sound Models

Adam was highly selective about which players to imitate. His Big 3: **Adolph Herseth, Doc Severinsen, Maurice Andre** — for their "silver" or "opulent" quality. He also valued Horst Eichler. Students were assigned to listen studiously (not passively) to these players and others, jazz and classical, to build a vivid internal sound concept. The very moment your sound resembles theirs, your mechanics adjust accordingly (kinesthetic response). You cannot chase that sound mechanically; you have to hear it first.

### Embouchure: What Adam Actually Said

- "The embouchure forms as a RESULT of the act of blowing."
- "The area under the mouthpiece ALWAYS stays soft."
- "A great embouchure allows you to do whatever you want to do on the trumpet for a sufficient time."
- The strength of a good embouchure is in the buccinators (cheek muscles engaged outward), not in tight lips. These engage as a product of blowing — not as something you consciously set.
- Awareness of embouchure has no place in trumpet playing. He meant it.

### The Breath

Every note is preceded by a full inhale. Breath is delivered with balanced energy — "in freedom vs. muscled." The motive power originates from what Adam called the "ki" — a centered, energized release rather than a forced push. Slack's term: "It takes a great deal of energy to play the trumpet, but it shouldn't take any force." Think accelerating air through the sound, not blowing into the horn. The breath accelerates through the imagined pitch/sound, and that acceleration is what produces resonance.

On the leadpipe: blow the fundamental pitch (concert Eb/F on a Bb pipe), hear it in your mind first, then accelerate the air through it. The pipe is like a small trumpet — complete with harmonics — so it sets up breath and sound together before adding the bell and valves.

### "Playing Flat Out"

Adam's phrase for the mental approach to all registers: never think up, never think down. Everything is straight out in front of you, linear. The same sound concept — the same commitment — applies to low C as to high C. You never sacrifice sound opulence for range or volume. If the sound concept stays constant, the body adjusts for register automatically. This is by design in how the routine is structured: it starts in the middle register and expands out and down before going high.

### Bob Slack on Why the Routine Is Ordered as It Is

From Slack's 2021 essay "The Routine, Why We Do What We Do":

> "Sound or Tone: Our individual sound IS our calling card. The sound must never change irrespective of register, dynamic, speed, or style of music."

> "Technique must be fluid and connected, never sacrificing sound for speed. Lasting facility (technique) on the horn comes from the methodical repetition of accelerating our breath through the sound."

> "Tonguing: Imagine playing as if you are pronouncing a word. The syllables float on the breath and are enunciated by the tongue. The tongue never stops the breath — it only divides it. Never ever think about your tongue. Keep your mind in the aural goal of what you want it to sound like."

> "Endurance and range: The daily deep dive into exhaustive, thorough practice is absolutely necessary in order to truly master these skills. We must be willing to work through the challenges without getting frustrated or trying some hair-brained idea received from the internet."

> "Moving our conscious thought away from the sound into the awareness of muscle activity stops the creative process."

The routine is ordered to warm up the air and the sound before adding complexity. In the earliest part of the day, never play too loud, too fast, or too high too soon. The system needs time for thoughts and physiology to warm up together.

### This Is Not a Method

Adam would have rejected the label. He evaluated each person and created a path for them to become free of their limitations. The "routine" as codified is a starting framework, not a prescription. He resisted writing it down precisely because the magic is in the execution — the sound he produced while modeling — not in the notes on the page. Successful instruction happened when the student was unaware of any potential problem; through subtle emphasis on sound, the physical adjustment occurred without the student knowing it.

## Exercises / Reference

### Daily Routine Sequence

The later routine (post-1977, as codified in the Davis tribute) runs in this order. Not every section is done every day at full length; the Davis book recommends cycling through variations.

1. **Leadpipe long tones** — 5–10 min. Blow without the bell. Hear the pitch (concert Eb/F), take a full breath, accelerate the air through the imagined pitch. No buzzing focus; this is about setting up the breath and the sound concept before adding the instrument.
2. **Long tones, Set 1** — 10–15 min. Mid-register, expanding chromatically up and down. mf to f. Full breath before every note.
3. **Clarke Technical Study #1** — 10–15 min. Starting on F# in the staff, then F, G, expanding outward in half steps. Play slowly first, gradually speed up without losing the core of the sound. Fingers float on the breath.
4. **Schlossberg exercises** (the core of the routine) — 20–30 min. Nos. 6, 13, 14, 15, 17, 23, 24, 25, 27, 31, 36B, 95, 97, 99, 100, 102a. Play "flat out" — no thinking up or down. Same sound in every register.
5. **Expanding scales / additional scale and chord studies** — 10 min. All keys, various patterns.
6. **Ernest Williams Pronunciation Exercise** (or similar articulation/flexibility study) — 10 min.
7. **Literature / etudes** — as assigned (Charlier 36 Etudes, solo repertoire, transposition).

**Earlier routine (mid-1950s–1977):** Began with Clarke #1 (on F#, expanding), then expanding scales. Long tones were added around 1977, and leadpipe blowing was added shortly after that.

### Long Tones

**Purpose:** Establish the sound concept for the day before adding technique. Every long tone is a study in sound, not just pitches held.

**How:**
- Hear the pitch clearly in your mind before you play it.
- Take a full, relaxed breath.
- Accelerate the air through the imagined sound (not into the horn — through the sound).
- Hold as long as comfortable at mf to f.
- Rest after each note (Adam used fermata + breath mark notation — no rushing).

**Set 1 (standard):** Begins on the G below the staff (bottom-line G in bass clef position — low concert G), moves chromatically up and down through the full practical range. Pattern: G, G#, Ab, A... expanding in both directions. Ends around low F# and high F#.

**Set 2 (advanced):** Extended range, same concept. Staves show two-octave span per exercise, with both upper and lower voice written.

**What to listen for:** Resonance — not brightness, not edge, but what Adam called "opulence." The tone should bloom naturally from the breath, with no audible effort. If it sounds effortful, the air is being forced rather than accelerated.

**Comeback player starting point:** Begin only in the comfortable middle register. C4 to G4 is fine. Do not push range. Adam's philosophy fully supports this: play what you can play with the ideal sound, and let the range expand organically as the embouchure rebuilds.

### Lip Slurs / Flexibility

**Schlossberg No. 6** — Half notes slurring through a chromatic sequence in the mid-register. The notation shows fermatas over each note with breath marks between, indicating full breath and full rest between each slurred pair. Never think up to the higher note; think linear, "flat out."

**Schlossberg No. 31** — The signature flexibility exercise in the routine. Quarter-note slurred arpeggios (root-third-fifth and back) moving chromatically. Standard section then ADVANCED section that extends into the upper register. This is the exercise where "playing flat out" is most critical — the higher notes are just as "straight ahead" as the lower ones.

**Schlossberg No. 14 and 15** — Longer slurred lines covering wider intervals. No. 14 is scalar with a leap; No. 15 is extended slurred arpeggios. Both have ADVANCED sections.

**What to feel:** Nothing specific. You should not be aware of the aperture changing. The lip slur is caused by the air and the sound concept, not by lip manipulation. If you're aware of what your lips are doing, you're in the wrong mental space.

### Clarke #1 (Technical Studies)

**Purpose in the routine:** Connect the breath to the fingers. Technique is the natural byproduct of breath moving through a clear sound concept — not separate from it.

**How:** Start on F# in the staff. The pattern is a five-note chromatic figure repeating up a half step each time. Play slowly enough that the sound concept stays intact. Never let the fingers get ahead of the air. "Go slow enough so the notes themselves are being blown... so that there is no muscle restriction that will diminish the sound."

**Range for comeback players:** Do not extend above C5 or wherever the sound becomes effortful. The exercise expands naturally; stop before strain.

**Adam's own words (from Trumpet Magazine routine):** "Any time we play Herbert L. Clarke exercises, it's a good idea to think of the acceleration of the air. Play the first note with a firmata, accelerate the air through the trumpet, and when you start to use the valves, continue to accelerate the air so the tone stays free."

### Additional Schlossberg / Glantz Studies

**Schlossberg No. 95** — Scale/arpeggio patterns in all keys with fermatas on top notes. Major keys: C, B, Bb, A, Ab, G, Gb. Goal: sustain the top notes while resting/trading off with a partner at each breath mark.

**Schlossberg No. 97** — One-breath exercise. Goal: play the full exercise in one breath, keeping air relaxed. Can be extended out to F#.

**Schlossberg No. 99 and 100** — Flexibility patterns. No. 100 starts on low F#, extends upward (or keep going as high as you can).

**Glantz Studies (Progressive Technique by Charles Colin)** — Reinforces all Adam concepts with progressively wider intervals and various articulations. Slack recommends doing these several times a week, alternating in various keys.

## Extended Routine (Jerry Hey / Larry Hall)

Jerry Hey and Larry Hall, both prominent studio/jazz trumpeters and IU Adam students, compiled and shared their expanded daily routine (published via Bob Reeves Brass, 2015). This is what serious players built toward after internalizing the standard routine.

**Key parameters:**
- Total daily practice: 4–5 hours, broken into sessions with real breaks
- Adam's minimum: 3 hours (1 hour maintenance, 1 hour technique, 1 hour literature)
- Practice with a partner when possible — ensures equal play/rest ratio and builds performance comfort

**Session 1 (performed every day, consistent):**
- Clarke #1 — start on F# in staff, then F, then G, expanding outward
- Schlossberg #6 — start soft, crescendo to next bar, decrescendo
- Schlossberg #23, 24, 25, 27
- Schlossberg #36B — start with variation A, then 2, 3, 4, 5, going up a half step each variation
- Schlossberg #95 — sustain top notes, rest or trade off at each breath mark
- Schlossberg #97 — one breath goal, relaxed air, extendable to F#
- Schlossberg #99 and #100 — start #100 on low F#, extend to F# an octave up or higher
- Schlossberg #102a — each day pick a different variation (major, minor, harmonic minor) and a different interval variation (3rds, 4ths, 5ths); extend outward to F#
- Schlossberg #120–123, #128

**Session 2 (pick and choose to keep it fresh):**
- Clarke 2nd, 3rd, or 4th Study — always start on E below staff, expand to low F# and C in staff; when playing the 2nd Study, change modes on each repetition (major, minor, diminished)
- Arban p.14 #16–27 — play each twice (1st time all slurred, rest, 2nd time all single tongued)
- Arban #46–50 — transpose each in all keys
- Schlossberg #45
- Arban double tonguing p.175 #77–94 — #88–90 in all keys
- Arban triple tonguing
- Schlossberg chord studies #70, 71, 73
- Arban p.297 Characteristic Study #13 — goal: one breath ("never heard anyone make it!" — J. Hey)
- Saint-Jacome #157 — always single tongue; variations in triplets, sixteenths, sextuplets

**Session 3:**
- Start with another Clarke study
- Arban chromatic triplets p.76 #1–5, p.80 #10–23
- Saint-Jacome Bugle Call p.19 #26, p.24, p.49 #3, p.68, p.81
- Selection of Charlier or Bitsch etudes, or Art of the Trumpet (Thiecke)
- Maggio Long Range Chromatic (end of day): slur from low C to C in staff, lip trill between G and C, back to low C; go down by half steps to low F#; then chromatic patterns ascending by half steps as high as you can go — this is done without trading off, and "you WILL be done at this point"

**What the extended routine adds:** Scale, chord, and mode variations force deeper ear training. The articulation work (double/triple tongue, Arban studies) covers what the standard routine leaves out. The Maggio chromatic at the end is a structured endurance push at the close of the day's final session. The principle stays the same throughout: never sacrifice sound for speed, never sacrifice technique for range.

**When to use it:** This is professional-level daily volume. For comeback players, Session 1 of the standard routine is the target. The extended routine content is reference for what the full system looks like and what specific Schlossberg/Arban numbers are in play.

## Comeback Player Notes

These notes draw directly from Adam's philosophy and from the sources — these are not generic comeback tips.

**The most important thing Adam would tell a comeback player: the approach is the same.** Sound first, breath second, everything else 1%. The only difference is where you start in the register and how long you play before resting. Adam worked with players of all levels and backgrounds, including those who had been away from the horn. His belief was unwavering: limits are defined only by imagination and work ethic.

**Specific guidance derived from the sources:**

1. **Start on the leadpipe.** This is the gentlest possible entry. Blow the fundamental pitch (concert Eb/F) with a relaxed breath. No strain, no embouchure setting — just air moving through an imagined pitch. Do this until the embouchure is responding easily to the breath.

2. **Long tones only in comfortable range.** The Davis routine notation says: "For advanced players, start on 3rd place C and expand chromatically." For a comeback player, start where you are — C4 to G4 is appropriate for a player with range currently limited to C4–C5. The Trumpet Magazine notation is explicit: "Hold each pitch as long as comfortable at a volume of mf to f... Do not play so high that you have to strain for the notes." Stop before strain. Stop before the sound degrades.

3. **Rest as long as you play.** Adam's "rest as long as you play" concept was literal — especially when practicing with a partner (trading off exercises). For a solo comeback player: if you play 4 bars, rest 4 bars. Set a timer. Comeback embouchures fatigue faster than they feel like they are fatiguing; the collapse comes suddenly if you push through mild discomfort.

4. **Do not extend Clarke exercises into strain.** The sources are explicit: "Do not extend any of these exercises to the point where you are running out of air and tension creeps into your chest. If you are a developing trumpet player, do not play so high that you have to strain for the notes." C4–C5 is a safe ceiling for Clarke work during early comeback. The exercise itself will keep expanding; you stop at your current ceiling and return the next day.

5. **Never think about what the embouchure is doing.** This is where comeback players are most likely to go wrong. The urge to "check in" on how the lips feel, whether they are positioned correctly, whether the aperture is right — this is the single biggest obstacle. Adam's model is unambiguous: awareness of embouchure has no place in trumpet playing. If the embouchure is uncomfortable, the answer is to redirect focus to the sound and the breath, not to examine the embouchure. The embouchure forms as a result of blowing.

6. **Psychological and physiological problems are overcome through positive mental outlook and goal orientation.** This is directly from Adam's clinic address. The vast majority of problems — including the frustrations of a comeback — respond to committing fully to the sound goal rather than to fixing what feels broken. Adam recommended Psycho-Cybernetics and The Inner Game of Tennis to students for exactly this reason: self-image is the substrate on which all technical improvement rests.

7. **Play every day, but don't play long.** "You must play enough hours per day, day after day, over a long period of time, in order to develop the face, the breath, and the mental discipline you'll need to play your best." For a comeback player, "enough" means consistent short sessions that build without injuring. 20–30 minutes of actual playing (with equal rest) is better than one 90-minute session followed by three days off.

8. **No quick fixes.** Slack's essay is direct: "Now go fire it up! — his not-so-subtle acknowledgment that this method of playing takes a great deal of time, effort and patience if you want to turn the corner. There are no quick fixes." The internet is full of equipment advice, physical gimmicks, and shortcuts. None of them are the answer. Thoughtful, consistent practice with a clear sound goal will do it.

9. **The range will come back.** It comes back through the process — long tones, lip slurs, Clarke, the breath, the sound concept — not through range exercises specifically targeted at high notes. The upper partials open up when the lower register is full and free and the air is moving correctly. Chasing high notes directly before the middle register is solid is putting the cart before the horse, as Adam would say.

## Connection to LHS

The Adam approach provides the philosophical foundation and warm-up methodology for the LHS trumpet track: the leadpipe-to-long-tones-to-Clarke-to-Schlossberg sequence maps directly onto how an LHS practice session should open, and the sound-first / breath-second / embouchure-never principle governs how exercises are coached and how students are redirected when they get stuck in physical awareness.
