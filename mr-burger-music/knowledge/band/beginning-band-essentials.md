## Key Concepts

Beginning band trumpet instruction rests on a few non-negotiable fundamentals that must be established before any repertoire or technical work. The physical setup comes first: instrument held horizontally, mouthpiece centered on the lips (equal upper and lower), first three fingers of the right hand curved over the valves with fingertips on the pistons, left hand supporting the instrument gently. The instrument is parallel to the floor — not angled up or down.

The first sound is produced by placing the mouthpiece in the natural center of the lips and pronouncing "tu" (not "thu") while releasing a stream of air. The tongue acts like a valve: it begins at the base of the upper front teeth and moves backward to release the air. Never let the tongue come between the teeth. The tone comes from breath and lip vibration, not from pressing the mouthpiece harder. Soft practice is more productive than loud: pressing harder tightens the lips and produces a squeezed, weak tone rather than a free vibration. Clarke's guidance from the trumpet tradition applies directly: always play softly at first, rest before fatigue, and build strength through repetition rather than force.

The first notes for trumpet are open G (concert F — no valves) and C (concert Bb — no valves, lower partial). Most beginning methods start with G because it speaks more easily for most beginners; C is introduced after G is stable. The beginner range goal for the first weeks is C4–G4 (concert Bb3–F4). By the end of the first semester, F4–C5 should be accessible. The Bb scale (concert Bb major: C D E F G A B C for trumpet) is the first scale because the trumpet is a Bb transposing instrument — when a trumpet player reads and plays a C major scale, the concert pitch is Bb major. This is why beginning band materials are written "in Bb."

Rhythm literacy builds in sequence. Whole notes → half notes → quarter notes must be internalized before eighth notes are introduced. Eighth notes are taught first as pairs (two eighth notes = one quarter note), then in groups of four, then mixed with quarter and half notes. Counting syllables anchor the rhythm: quarter note = "1," "2," "3," "4"; eighth notes = "1-and," "2-and," etc. The physical experience of tapping and counting out loud precedes playing rhythms on the instrument.

Ensemble playing introduces new demands beyond individual technique. Students must listen horizontally (across the section) and vertically (across the ensemble). The chorale format — slow, sustained, harmonically simple — is the most effective tool for teaching blend, balance, intonation, and phrase shape in a band setting. Long tones as an ensemble warm-up establish a common pitch center before any technical work begins.

## Exercises / Reference

### Beginning Trumpet Fundamentals

**Posture and hand position:**
- Sit tall, bell pointing straight ahead, instrument horizontal
- Left hand: grip the instrument with the ring of fingers around the valve casing; keep the wrist easy and supple
- Right hand: first three fingers curved naturally over valve caps, fingertips (not the middle of the finger) on top of the pistons; thumb beneath the leadpipe
- No squeezing — hold firmly but stay relaxed

**Mouthpiece placement:**
- Center of the lips, vertically and horizontally
- Equal upper and lower lip coverage (neither 2/3 on upper nor 2/3 on lower)
- The two lips vibrate together in the center of the cup

**First tones — C4 and G4 (open, no valves):**
- Start on G4 if it speaks easily; C4 if G4 does not respond
- Syllable: "tu" — tongue at base of upper front teeth, backward motion to release air
- Play softly; never force; rest when the lips feel any fatigue

**Fingering reference (concert pitch in parentheses):**

| Trumpet Note | Valves | Concert Pitch |
|-------------|--------|---------------|
| C4 | 0 (open) | Bb3 |
| D4 | 1-3 | C4 |
| E4 | 1-2 | D4 |
| F4 | 1 | Eb4 |
| G4 | 0 (open) | F4 |
| A4 | 1-2 | G4 |
| B4 | 2 | Ab4 |
| C5 | 0 (open) | Bb4 |

**First range goal:** C4–G4 (weeks 1–4), then C4–C5 (weeks 5–12).

### Warm-Up Sequence

Recommended daily order for a beginning band rehearsal. Each segment builds on the previous:

1. **Long Tones** — sustained whole notes or half notes on a single pitch or slowly ascending through the Bb scale. Use *Young Ensemble Warm-ups* Long Tones 1 through 3 (McAllister): each exercise holds a pitch for 2+ beats, with rests between phrases for the director to give instruction. Note marked "Air!" means take a full breath. This teaches breath support, tone centering, and listening across the ensemble.

2. **Flexibility / Lip Slurs** — *Young Ensemble Warm-ups* Flexibility 1 and Flexibility 2: slurred patterns moving between adjacent partials or intervals. Teaches the embouchure to relax and contract smoothly. Appropriate once basic tone is established (typically mid-first semester).

3. **Scales** — Bb major scale ascending and descending (trumpet reads C major). Use *Band Basics in Bb* "Bb Scale" section: stepwise, whole notes or half notes, all sections together. Then "Solidify the Key" — short melodic patterns that reinforce the key center.

4. **Chorale** — *Band Basics in Bb* "Chorale" section or a chorale from *36 Chorales for Band*. Sustain each note fully; watch the director for balance and blend adjustments. This is the most important blend exercise in the rehearsal.

5. **Technique** — *Band Basics in Bb* "Technique #1" and "Technique #2": eighth-note scale passages and articulated patterns that build finger dexterity. Keep the tempo modest; accuracy over speed.

### Chorale Content

**Band Basics in Bb — Chorale section (McAllister)**
Appears at the end of the warm-up sequence. Trumpet 1 carries the soprano voice; Trumpet 2 carries the alto. The chorale is in Bb major, moderate tempo, with sustained half notes and whole notes. Rests provide breathing points. Use this to teach: (1) matching tone quality across the section, (2) listening to the pitch center, (3) tapering phrase endings. Director gives instruction during written rests.

**36 Chorales for Band (Aaron Cole) — Trumpet instructions:**
Trumpet players read Soprano (S) and Alto (A) voice parts. Tenor and Bass parts are included for reference. Cue notes are provided when a passage goes too high or too low — always play the cue note for a good sound rather than cracking a note in the primary range. Notes in parentheses are too low to produce on trumpet; they show where the line goes but are not expected to be played. Split the trumpet section: Trumpet 1 players take Soprano, Trumpet 2 players take Alto.

**Chorale 1 — I-IV-V-I (A simple chord progression):** Four voices in Bb major, whole notes. Best starting chorale for beginners. Teaches the most basic harmonic motion.

**Chorale 2 — Canon in D (Pachelbel, ca. 1680):** Quarter notes in the soprano. More rhythmic activity; appropriate once quarter-note reading is solid.

**Chorale 3 — Circle of Fifths Chorale:** Explores harmonic movement around the circle of fifths. Good for hearing key-center shifts.

**Chorales 1–7** are appropriate for first-year players. **Chorales 8–15** require more range and rhythmic facility. **Chorales 16–36** are progressively more difficult.

### Scale Structure

**Major scale pattern — from the Structure of the Major Scale table:**

| Scale Degree | Name | Sol-Fa | C Major | Bb Major (concert) | Interval from previous |
|-------------|------|--------|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Tonic | Doh | C | Bb | — |
| 2 | Supertonic | Ray | D | C | Major 2nd (whole step) |
| 3 | Mediant | Me | E | D | Major 2nd (whole step) |
| 4 | Sub-Dominant | Fa | F | Eb | minor 2nd (half step) |
| 5 | Dominant | Soh | G | F | Major 2nd (whole step) |
| 6 | Sub-Mediant | La | A | G | Major 2nd (whole step) |
| 7 | Leading Note | Te | B | Ab | Major 2nd (whole step) |
| 8 | Tonic | Doh | C | Bb | minor 2nd (half step) |

**The pattern in semitones:** 2 - 2 - 1 - 2 - 2 - 2 - 1
**The pattern in tones/steps:** W - W - H - W - W - W - H
**Abbreviated:** Whole, Whole, Half, Whole, Whole, Whole, Half

Bb major is the first key because the trumpet is a Bb transposing instrument — when the trumpet reads C major, the concert pitch is Bb major. This is why virtually all beginning band methods are written "in Bb." When a student learns the concert Bb scale, they are reading and fingering C major on the page.

### Rhythm Curriculum

**Sequence of introduction (one stage must be solid before advancing to the next):**

**Stage 1 — Whole, half, quarter notes**
Whole note = 4 beats; half note = 2 beats; quarter note = 1 beat. Count aloud: "1-2-3-4," "1-2," "1." Tap the beat while counting. Introduce in that order over the first 4–6 weeks.

**Stage 2 — Eighth note introduction** (*Eighth Note Introduction*, Wendy Stevens)
Two eighth notes = one quarter note. Count: "1-and-2-and-3-and-4-and." Worksheet activities: write in the counts below each rhythm, tap and count aloud, then set metronome and practice. First drills mix all quarter notes with pairs of eighth notes (no mixed patterns yet). Students must write counts before tapping — reading the rhythm first.

**Stage 3 — Eighth notes mixed** (*Eighth Notes 2*, Wendy Stevens)
Eighth notes mixed freely with quarter notes, half notes, and whole notes in 4/4. No dotted rhythms yet. Students practice: write counts, tap and count, metronome. Exercises progress from simple (eighth-note pairs followed by whole note) to continuous eighth-note runs.

**Stage 4 — Food Rhythms sorting activity** (*Food Rhythms!*, Carolyn Bennett)
Kinesthetic/conceptual activity: sort food words by their natural syllable rhythm into boxes labeled with four rhythm patterns (quarter+quarter, quarter+eighth-pair, eighth-pair+quarter, eighth-pair+eighth-pair). Examples from the source: "cof-fee" = two quarter notes; "can-dy" = quarter + eighth pair (or vice versa depending on stress); "marsh-mal-lows" = three syllables over two beats; "i-ce cream sun-dae" = four syllables in eighth notes. This builds rhythmic vocabulary through language before notation. Use as a class activity before introducing mixed rhythms on an instrument.

**Stage 5 — Alternating hands / call-and-response** (*Eighth Notes 1, Alternating Hands*, Wendy Stevens)
Two-stave rhythm exercises where one voice plays while the other rests, then alternates. Teaches rhythmic independence, listening, and ensemble precision. Both voices are notated on percussion staves (no pitch). Use for clapping, body percussion, or mallet percussion. Dynamics (f/p) are specified — this also introduces basic dynamic contrast.

**Counting syllables summary:**

| Note Value | Count |
|-----------|-------|
| Whole note (4 beats) | 1 - 2 - 3 - 4 |
| Half note (2 beats) | 1 - 2 |
| Quarter note (1 beat) | 1 |
| Eighth note pair (1 beat) | 1-and (or "ti-ti") |
| Four eighth notes (2 beats) | 1-and-2-and |

### Beginner Lesson Sequence

Suggested progression for first-year trumpet players (6-day cycle or weekly):

**Weeks 1–2: First sound**
- Mouthpiece only: buzz "tu" to get a tone, then add the trumpet
- First note: open G (or C whichever speaks). Whole notes only
- Introduce: quarter notes on G; count aloud; metronome at 60
- No fingerings yet — just open tones

**Weeks 3–4: First notes C, D, E**
- Introduce C (open), D (1-3), E (1-2) — all fingerings labeled
- Simple stepwise exercises: C-D-E-D-C; whole and half notes
- Begin *Elementary Studies* Lesson 1 (duet format — director plays teacher part)
- Rhythm: whole and half notes only; count "1-2-3-4"

**Weeks 5–6: G4 and the first octave C–G**
- Add G4 (open) above — students will have already played it as a first note
- F4 (valve 1) introduced
- First 5-note scale (C-D-E-F-G) ascending and descending
- Rhythm: introduce quarter notes with counting; *Food Rhythms* activity for syllabic rhythm recognition

**Weeks 7–8: Bb scale (concert key)**
- Complete Bb major scale for trumpet (C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C)
- Use *Band Basics in Bb* Bb Scale section as ensemble exercise
- Begin *Band Basics in Bb* warm-up sequence: Long Tones → Solidify the Key → Chorale
- Rhythm: introduce eighth-note pairs; *Eighth Note Introduction* worksheet Stage 1

**Weeks 9–10: Technique and ensemble**
- *Band Basics in Bb* Technique #1 (scale-based eighth-note runs)
- *Chorale 1* from *36 Chorales for Band* (I-IV-V-I, whole notes)
- *Young Ensemble Warm-ups* Long Tones 1 as full band warm-up
- Rhythm: *Eighth Notes 2* worksheet; mixed eighth and quarter

**Weeks 11–12: Range C4–C5, articulation**
- Full C4–C5 range in exercises
- Introduce tongued vs. slurred articulation
- *36 Chorales* Chorale 2 (Canon in D — quarter-note rhythm)
- *Alternating Hands* rhythm drills for call-and-response and ensemble listening
- Review major scale structure table; explain W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern

**Ongoing:** Long Tones → Chorale → Scale → Technique every rehearsal. Rotate chorales from *36 Chorales*. Add flexibility exercises (*Young Ensemble Warm-ups* Flexibility 1) once basic embouchure is stable.

## Connection to LHS

A band director working with beginning trumpet players needs exactly what these sources provide: a systematic warm-up protocol (*Young Ensemble Warm-ups*), an ensemble key-center framework (*Band Basics in Bb*), a scalable chorale library (*36 Chorales for Band*), clear theory grounding (*Major Scale Structure Table*), and a graded rhythm literacy sequence (*Stevens* and *Bennett* worksheets). Together they cover the full scope of first-year band instruction — individual technique, ensemble blend, music reading, and rhythmic vocabulary — and they can be adapted flexibly based on where students are in any given week.
