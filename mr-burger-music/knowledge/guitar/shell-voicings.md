## Key Concepts

A shell voicing uses only three notes from a seventh chord: the root, the third, and the seventh. The fifth is dropped entirely. This is not a shortcut — it is a deliberate harmonic reduction that does several things at once.

**Shells are unambiguous.** The third tells you whether the chord is major or minor. The seventh tells you whether it is a major seventh, dominant seventh, or minor seventh. Everything essential to the chord's harmonic function is present. Nothing redundant is added.

**Shells are voice-leading machines.** Because you are only moving two non-root voices (the third and seventh), you can track their motion from chord to chord with precision. On a ii-V-I in C: the b3 of Dm7 (F) is the same note as the b7 of G7 (F) — it stays in place. The b7 of Dm7 (C) moves down a half step to become the 3rd of G7 (B). Then on the resolution to Cmaj7, the 3rd of G7 (B) resolves up a half step to C, and the b7 of G7 (F) resolves down a half step to E (the 3rd of Cmaj7). This contrary, stepwise motion between the third and seventh is what makes shells sound harmonically complete and resolved.

**Shells sit on two or three adjacent strings.** This keeps the voicing physically compact and quick to grab. You do not need to stretch across four or five strings the way full jazz voicings often require.

**Shells are immediately transposable.** Once you know a shell family on one string set, you slide the shape up or down to a new root and the chord quality follows automatically. The Jazz Chord Survival Kit demonstrates this: the same shell shapes cycle through Bb major and F major by simply moving up the neck, and the ii-V examples sequence through multiple keys by stepping down two frets at a time.

**Two main shell families on guitar:**
- Root on 6th string (E): root on string 6, third and seventh on strings 5 and 4
- Root on 5th string (A): root on string 5, third and seventh on strings 4 and 3

Each family has two voicing options depending on whether the third or seventh is on top, giving you four basic shell shapes that cover the entire fretboard.

For LHS guitar specifically, shells work because they leave the upper strings free for melodic content and prevent harmonic clutter when a second part is playing. They also make ii-V-I progressions in multiple keys physically manageable — you are moving a small two-finger shape rather than re-fingering a full five-note voicing.

## Exercises / Reference

### Shell Voicing Concept

A shell = root + 3rd + 7th only. The 5th is omitted.

| Chord type | Intervals kept | Interval dropped |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| maj7 | R, 3, maj7 | 5 |
| m7 | R, b3, b7 | 5 |
| 7 (dominant) | R, 3, b7 | 5 |
| m7b5 | R, b3, b7 | b5 |

The 3rd and b7 of the ii chord swap roles on the V chord. This is the engine of shell voice leading.

### ii-V-I Shell Shapes in C

Standard tuning. String 6 = low E, string 1 = high e.

**Family 1 — Root on 6th string (strings 6-5-4)**

Two voicing options based on which note is on top:

```
OPTION A — b7 above b3 (3rd on bottom, 7th on top):

Dm7  (root D on str 6)
  Str 6, fret 10: D  (root)   — finger 1
  Str 5, fret 12: F  (b3)     — finger 3 or 4
  Str 4, fret 12: C  (b7)     — finger 3 or 4

G7   (root G on str 6)
  Str 6, fret 3:  G  (root)   — finger 1
  Str 5, fret 2:  B  (3)      — open/finger 1
  Str 4, fret 3:  F  (b7)     — finger 2
  — Note: F (b3 of Dm7) stays as F (b7 of G7). C moves to B.

Cmaj7 (root C on str 6)
  Str 6, fret 8:  C  (root)   — finger 1
  Str 5, fret 9:  E  (3)      — finger 2
  Str 4, fret 9:  B  (maj7)   — finger 3
  — Note: B (3rd of G7) resolves up to C (root). F (b7 of G7) resolves down to E (3rd).

OPTION B — b3 above b7 (7th on bottom, 3rd on top):

Dm7 (root D on str 6, lower neck)
  Str 6, fret 5:  D  (root)   — finger 1 (alt: fret 10 above)
  Str 5, fret 5:  G  (4/11, or use: str 5 fret 3 = C as b7)
  Str 4, fret 5:  A  (5, or use: str 4 fret 7 = F as b3)
  [Adjust: for clean shell, use str 5 fret 3 = C, str 4 fret 7 = F]
```

### Voice Leading Notes

**The core mechanics of shell voice leading (ii → V → I in C):**

**Dm7 → G7:**
- b3 of Dm7 = F. b7 of G7 = F. **F stays in place.**
- b7 of Dm7 = C. 3rd of G7 = B. **C moves down a half step to B.**
- Result: one note holds, one moves down by semitone. Very smooth.

**G7 → Cmaj7:**
- 3rd of G7 = B. This resolves up a half step to C (root of Cmaj7), OR stays as maj7 if the Cmaj7 voicing keeps the B.
- b7 of G7 = F. Resolves down a half step to E (the 3rd of Cmaj7).
- Result: both voices move by half step in contrary motion (one up, one down). Maximum smoothness.

**Practical fingering principle (from Jens Larsen):**

Stay in one position as long as possible. When you must shift, move the minimum number of fingers the minimum distance. On the ii-V, the b3/b7 swap means you can often keep one finger planted and just move the other.

When practicing shells:
1. Play the chord, name each note aloud (root, 3rd quality, 7th quality)
2. Move to the next chord, tracking which note stays and which moves
3. Do this before adding any rhythm — voice leading first, time second
