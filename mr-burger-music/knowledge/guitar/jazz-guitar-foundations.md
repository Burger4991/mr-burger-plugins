## Key Concepts

**Jazz guitar is chord-first, not scale-first.** Unlike a lot of beginner guitar instruction, jazz starts with understanding 7th chords and how they function in progressions. The three most important chord types to know immediately are: major 7th (Maj7), dominant 7th (7), and minor 7th (m7). These three cover nearly every chord you will encounter in a standard jazz tune.

**7th chords are the basic unit of harmony.** In jazz, plain major and minor chords are almost always replaced with their 7th chord equivalents. A plain C major chord becomes Cmaj7 (C-E-G-B). A plain C minor becomes Cm7 (C-Eb-G-Bb). A C dominant 7th chord (C7) is spelled C-E-G-Bb — it has a natural 3rd like a major chord and a flatted 7th. These three chord types form three families: major family (Maj7, Maj9, Maj13, 6), minor family (m7, m9, m11, m6), and dominant family (7, 9, 11, 13). Chords within the same family are interchangeable in comping.

**The ii-V-I progression is the backbone of jazz.** Built from the 2nd, 5th, and 1st notes of the major scale, this three-chord movement appears in virtually every jazz standard. In the key of C: Dm7 (ii) — G7 (V) — Cmaj7 (I). Learning to recognize and play this progression in multiple keys and positions is the central task of a beginning jazz guitarist. The extended turnaround form adds a VI7b9 chord: iim7 — V7 — Imaj7 — VI7b9 (in C: Dm7 — G7 — Cmaj7 — A7b9).

**Drop 2 and Drop 3 voicings are how jazz chords are built.** Jazz guitarists don't use full 6-string chords — they use 4-note voicings on specific string sets. Drop 3 voicings use strings 6-5-4-3 or 5-4-3-2 (two string sets). Drop 2 voicings use strings 5-4-3-2, 4-3-2-1, or 6-4-3-2 (three string sets). To build a Drop 3 chord: take a closed-position 4-note chord (1-3-5-7) and drop the 3rd note from the top down one octave. To build a Drop 2 chord: take the same closed chord and drop the 2nd note from the top down one octave. These voicings are moveable — learn one shape in one key, then slide it to all 12 keys.

**Comping is rhythmic as much as harmonic.** Comping (accompaniment) means supporting a melody or soloist with chord stabs — it is not strumming. The key variables are note length (long vs. short) and placement (on the beat vs. off the beat). A beginner comping approach: learn one or two simple rhythmic patterns, apply them to a single chord type, add variety gradually. The "Charleston rhythm" (beat 1 and the "and" of beat 2) is a foundational jazz comping rhythm.

**Listening is non-negotiable.** Aebersold is emphatic: internalize the sound before trying to play it. The ear leads the fingers. Suggested listening for a beginner: Joe Pass, Kenny Burrell, Wes Montgomery, Jim Hall, Grant Green, Charlie Christian.

**As a trumpet player, you already understand jazz phrasing.** Jazz guitar lines use the same approach as horn lines — emphasis on chord tones (root, 3rd, 5th, 7th), 8th notes as the primary rhythmic unit, tension and release through targeting. The Dorian scale (the minor mode used over m7 chords) is built the same way you already think about minor keys on trumpet.

## Exercises / Reference

### Starting Point

Before touching jazz chord shapes, confirm you can play and change between the following open-position chords cleanly: A, A7, Amin; C, C7, Cmin; D, D7, Dmin; E, E7, Emin; G, G7, Gmin. Fisher (Beginning Jazz Guitar, Ch. 1) treats these as the minimum required vocabulary. Barre chord forms (6th-string root and 5th-string root, major/dominant/minor) are also required before the jazz material is approachable. If chord changes are still slow, slow down the target tempo — not the chord sequence.

### First Jazz Chord Shapes

The five Drop 3 chord qualities to learn first (from Warnock, Ch. 1), all in root position, on the 6th-string and 5th-string string sets:

| Quality | Formula | Example (C root) | Mode source |
|---------|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| Maj7 | R-7-3-5 | C-B-E-G | Ionian (major) |
| 7 (dominant) | R-b7-3-5 | C-Bb-E-G | Mixolydian |
| m7 | R-b7-b3-5 | C-Bb-Eb-G | Dorian |
| m7b5 | R-b7-b3-b5 | C-Bb-Eb-Gb | Locrian |
| dim7 | R-bb7-b3-b5 | C-Bbb-Eb-Gb | — |

Shortcut for building these in sequence from the same root: start on Maj7, lower the 7th one fret to get the dominant 7, lower the 3rd one fret to get m7, lower the 5th one fret to get m7b5, lower the 7th one more fret to get dim7. Practice the sequence Maj7 — 7 — m7 — m7b5 — dim7 from a single root before moving to the next key.

From Fisher (Lesson 3A), the moveable Cmaj7 fingerings on strings 5-4-3-2:
- III position: x-1-3-2-4-x (R-5-7-3-5)
- V position: x-x-1-1-1-4 (5-R-3-7)
- VII position: x-x-4-3-2-1 (R-3-5-7)
- VIII position: 1-x-3-4-2-x (R-7-3-5)

From Fisher (Lesson 3A), Cmin7 moveable fingerings:
- III position: x-1-3-1-2-1 (R-5-b7-b3-5)
- III position variant: x-1-x-2-4-3 (R-b7-b3-5)
- VIII position: 1-3-1-1-4-x (R-5-b7-b3-7)

From Fisher (Lesson 3A), C7 (dominant) moveable fingerings:
- III position: 3-x-2-4-1-x (5-3-b7-R)
- III position variant: x-1-3-1-4-1 (R-5-b7-3-5)
- X position: 1-x-2-4-3-x (R-b7-3-5)
- XII position: 3-x-1-4-2-x (3-R-5-b7)

### Rhythm Guitar Basics

Comping is not strumming — it is placing chord stabs rhythmically. From Aebersold (p. 80-84):

- **Two variables:** chord length (long/short) and beat placement (on/off the beat).
- **Starting approach:** use one voicing and one rhythm pattern over a one-chord vamp. Add a second rhythm after the first is solid.
- **Basic rhythm catalog:** build from simple patterns first — quarter note on beat 1, quarter note on beat 3, "Charleston" (beat 1 and the "and" of beat 2), and variations with rests.
- **Anticipation:** when a chord change happens on the "and" of beat 2 or 4, it is standard practice to play the chord slightly early (anticipate the change rhythmically).
- **Chord families in comping:** if a chart says Cm7, you can use Cm7, Cm9, Cm11, or Cm6 — they are interchangeable within the minor family. This gives you flexibility to use whatever voicing sits best under your hand.

From Warnock (Ch. 3): the Charleston Rhythm is a foundational jazz guitar comping rhythm — one chord attack on beat 1 of the bar, then another on the "and" of beat 2. Learn it applied to the ii-V-I-VI turnaround progression first.

### Beginner Sequence

This is the recommended order of study drawn from all four sources:

1. **Confirm open chords and barre chord fundamentals** (Fisher Ch. 1 / Fisher Basic Chords reference sheet). Target: clean changes between any two chords without hesitation.
2. **Learn Drop 3 Maj7 on 6th-string root, one key** — memorize the shape from C. Warnock recommends mastering one shape before taking it to all 12 keys.
3. **Learn the Drop 3 chord quality sequence: Maj7 — 7 — m7 — m7b5 — dim7** from the same root (C). This reveals how the shapes are related — one note changes at a time.
4. **Repeat on the 5th-string root** for all five Drop 3 qualities.
5. **Play your first ii-V-I:** Dm7 — G7 — Cmaj7 using Drop 3 shapes in one position. This is the goal of steps 2-4. (Warnock Ch. 3; Aebersold p. 80)
6. **Add the turnaround:** extend the progression to Dm7 — G7 — Cmaj7 — A7b9 (VI7b9 played as C#dim7). Now you have a complete jazz turnaround.
7. **Add Drop 2 voicings** for the same ii-V-I-VI on the middle four strings (strings 4-3-2-1). Drop 2 shapes sit well for comping because they leave space in the sonic register for a soloist.
8. **Apply the Charleston Rhythm** to the turnaround progression.
9. **Practice comping over a play-along or backing track** — Aebersold Vol. 1 Track 2 (F minor Dorian, Eb minor Dorian, D minor Dorian) is a good starting point.
10. **Begin ii-V-I in other keys** by moving the same Drop 3 shapes chromatically around the neck.

Fisher's Etude #7 (7th Chords, p. 39) provides a written study in 3/4 time cycling through Gmaj7 — Em7 — Am7 — D7 — Bm7 — E7 — Am7 — D7 — Cmaj7 — D7 — Bm7 — Em7 — A7 — D7 — Gmaj7, using moveable shapes at multiple positions. This is an excellent early reading and comping exercise.

### Connection to LHS

The Linear Harmony System's approach to melody and chord tones maps directly onto jazz guitar: chord tones (root, 3rd, 5th, 7th) are the target notes; passing tones and approach notes create motion between them. The Dorian scale (used over m7 chords) is the same structure you use over minor tonal centers on trumpet. The ii-V-I progression is the same functional harmonic motion that underlies virtually all of the repertoire in the LHS.

## Connection to LHS

The ii-V-I progression is the harmonic engine of jazz standards, and the same voice-leading logic that drives LHS — chord tones resolving stepwise into the next chord — applies directly to jazz guitar comping. Playing chord melody or comping on guitar reinforces the same harmonic ear you are building on trumpet: you hear where the 3rd of the G7 (the note B) wants to resolve into the root of Cmaj7 (C), the same tension-resolution movement that LHS trains through melodic targeting. Because both instruments share the same theoretical vocabulary (7th chord qualities, Dorian mode, ii-V-I form), guitar practice directly reinforces trumpet practice rather than competing with it — the mental models transfer.
