# Benchmark Organizer Design

## Design Principles

### Scaffold Progression
Create organizers with graduated support levels:

1. **Guiding questions** (provided to focus thinking)
2. **Modeled example** (teacher demonstrates complete)
3. **Partial sample** (partially completed for students to finish)
4. **Sentence starters** (frames provided, students fill in)
5. **Full response** (student completes independently)

### Alignment to Benchmarks
Map rows explicitly to Planning Cards/9-10 Guide task demands:
- **Example: ELA.10.R.1.2 (Theme)**
  - Row 1: Theme identification → "What universal theme is present?"
  - Row 2: Development tracking → "How is theme introduced? (dialogue, setting, conflict, etc.)"
  - Row 3-5: Evidence citation → "Beginning/Middle/End evidence"
  - Row 6: Synthesis → "How does theme develop across the text?"

**Use exact language from benchmark clarifications**

### Mini-Lessons Embedded
Organizers should teach students HOW to do the thinking, not just WHAT to think:
- Strategy instruction (unpacking theme, tracking tone, citing evidence)
- Explicit modeling of analytical process
- Think-alouds during I Do phase

### Visuals for Accessibility
Include for ESOL/ESE concept-building:
- Diagrams showing relationships
- Graphic organizers with clear structure
- Anchor charts for reference
- Icons/symbols to aid comprehension

## Creating Organizers from Benchmark Cards

**CRITICAL: Use Benchmark Cards as the blueprint for all organizers**

### Step 1: Identify the Grade 10 Benchmark Steps
- Open the relevant benchmark card (e.g., R.1.2_Theme.pdf for theme units)
- Find the Grade 10 section (color-coded, usually yellow)
- Each step in the card becomes a row or column in your organizer

### Step 2: Convert Steps to Organizer Structure
**General Pattern:**
- **Step 1-2** = Columns or early rows (identifying element, where introduced)
- **Step 3-4** = Evidence gathering rows (beginning → middle → end)
- **Step 5-6** = Analysis/comparison rows (synthesis, connections)

**Example: Theme Organizer from R.1.2 Card**
| Universal Theme #1 | Where/How Introduced? | Evidence from Beginning | Evidence from Middle | Evidence from End | How Theme Develops |
|--------------------|----------------------|------------------------|---------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| (Step 1) | (Step 2: dialogue, setting, conflict, etc.) | (Step 3) | (Step 3) | (Step 3) | (Step 4) |
| Universal Theme #2 | | | | | |
| Universal Theme #3 | | | | | |
| **Compare themes** | How are they similar? | How are they different? | Which is more prominent? | Bleak or uplifting? | Individual vs. societal? |

### Step 3: Add Scaffolds Progressively
- **I Do row**: Fully completed with text citations (teacher model)
- **We Do row**: Partially completed (some evidence provided, students add more)
- **You Do w/ Partner row**: Sentence starters only
- **You Do row**: Blank (students work independently)

## Gradual Release Stages

### I Do (Teacher Models)
- Teacher completes first row with think-aloud
- Students follow along, annotate their copies
- Show the exact thinking process: "I'm looking for where the theme first appears..."
- Demonstrate text evidence location with paragraph numbers
- **Use Step 1-2 from benchmark card as teaching focus**

**Script Example:**
> "Let me show you how I identify the theme. First, I ask myself: What universal message could apply to anyone, anywhere, anytime? In paragraph 3, I notice the character says [quote]. This suggests the theme of perseverance because..."

### We Do (Collaborative)
- Teacher and students complete row together
- Call on students for contributions: "What evidence do you see in paragraph 8?"
- Guide and correct in real-time: "That's close, but remember we need UNIVERSAL themes..."
- Chart responses on board/screen for all to see
- **Use Step 3 from benchmark card - evidence gathering**

### You Do with a Partner
- Partners work on one row together
- Teacher circulates, monitors, provides support
- Use proximity and questioning to guide: "Where in the text do you see that theme developing?"
- Share out and discuss as class: cold-call pairs to present findings
- **Use Step 4 from benchmark card - analysis**

### You Do (Independent)
- Students complete remaining rows alone
- Teacher monitors completion without direct instruction
- Use for formative assessment data: Who's getting it? Who needs reteaching?
- Collect or review as exit ticket
- **Use Steps 5-6 from benchmark card - additional themes, comparison**

## Organizer Examples by Benchmark

### Literary Elements (ELA.10.R.1.1)
| Character | Setting Interaction | Plot Event | Development of Meaning |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Sentence starters: "The character [name] is introduced as..." | "The setting contributes by..." | "When [event] occurs..." | "This reveals the theme of..." |

### Central Idea (ELA.10.R.2.2)
| Central Idea | How Introduced | Supporting Detail 1 | Supporting Detail 2 | How Developed |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| "The author's main point is..." | "Introduced in paragraph ___ through..." | "Detail: ..." | "Detail: ..." | "The author develops this by..." |

### Argument (ELA.10.R.2.4)
| Author's Claim | Evidence Type | Evidence from Text | Reasoning | Effectiveness |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|-----------|---------------|
| "The author argues that..." | (statistics/anecdote/expert/logic) | "Quote + paragraph #" | "This supports the claim because..." | "This is effective/ineffective because..." |

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing an organizer, verify:
- [ ] Rows map to Planning Card/9-10 Guide steps
- [ ] I Do row is completely filled in with strong examples
- [ ] We Do row is partially completed (50-70%)
- [ ] You Do w/ Partner row has sentence starters
- [ ] You Do row is blank or minimal scaffolds
- [ ] Exact benchmark language used in headers
- [ ] Space for paragraph number citations
- [ ] Fits on 1-2 pages (not overwhelming)
- [ ] Visually clear (adequate white space, readable font)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Too many rows** - Keep to 4-6 rows maximum; more overwhelms students
2. **Vague prompts** - Use specific question stems from Planning Cards
3. **Insufficient modeling** - I Do row must be exemplary, not just "ok"
4. **Inconsistent scaffolding** - Each row should have LESS support than previous
5. **Missing benchmark alignment** - Every row must connect to benchmark task demands
6. **No space for evidence** - Students need room to write paragraph numbers and quotes

## Integration with Lesson Plans

Organizers drive Days 3-4 instruction:
- **Day 3 TL**: I Do + We Do rows
- **Day 3 IND**: Students copy We Do, start You Do w/ Partner
- **Day 4 TL**: You Do w/ Partner sharing + corrections
- **Day 4 IND**: You Do independent completion

By Day 5-6, organizer is complete and used as reference for assessment writing.
