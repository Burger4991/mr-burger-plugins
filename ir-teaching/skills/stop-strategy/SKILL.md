# STOP Strategy — 10th Grade Intensive Reading

## Purpose

STOP is the answer-elimination strategy students use on **every multiple choice question**. It teaches students to stop guessing, start proving. Each answer choice falls into one of four categories — students learn to identify all four before selecting an answer.

**The core habit:** "Don't pick an answer. PROVE an answer."

**Where STOP fits in the workflow:**
1. **CUBES the Question** (from `cubes-annotation`) — break down what the question asks
2. **Return to Text** — find key details with targeted rereading
3. **STOP** — eliminate wrong answers, prove the right one

---

## The STOP Categories

### S — Silly
The obviously wrong choice. Off-topic, factually incorrect, or contradicted by the text.

**What makes it Silly:**
- Contradicts what the passage clearly states
- Introduces information not found anywhere in the text
- Uses absurd or extreme language ("always," "never," "completely")
- Relates to a totally different topic

**Teaching language:** "If you read the passage at all, you can cross this one out."

**Example (Theme question about a tragedy):**
- "The story's message is that life is always easy and fun." → **SILLY** — contradicts the entire passage

### T — Tricky
The most dangerous choice. Contains some truth but is incomplete, overgeneralized, or distorts the text. This is the choice that catches students who skim.

**What makes it Tricky:**
- Contains accurate details FROM the text but doesn't answer the question
- Addresses only part of what the question asks
- Uses words from the passage but changes their meaning
- Is true in general but not supported by THIS specific text
- Answers a DIFFERENT question than the one asked

**Teaching language:** "This one SOUNDS right. It might even have words from the passage. But read the question again — does it actually answer what's being asked?"

**Example (Theme question):**
- "The author uses descriptive language throughout the passage." → **TRICKY** — true statement, but it describes a technique, not the theme

**Why Tricky matters most:** On FAST PM and state assessments, the Tricky choice is designed to trap students who:
- Read the passage but don't reread for the specific question
- Recognize familiar words and assume the answer is correct
- Answer too quickly without checking against the question stem

### O — Opposite
States the reverse or contrary of the correct answer. Tests whether students truly understood the text or reversed the meaning.

**What makes it Opposite:**
- Says the reverse of what the text actually states or implies
- Uses similar vocabulary to the correct answer but with negated meaning
- Flips cause and effect, before and after, or positive and negative
- Contradicts the proven answer directly

**Teaching language:** "This one says the OPPOSITE of what the text supports. If you mix up the details, you might pick it."

**Example (Theme question where theme = perseverance leads to growth):**
- "Hard work always leads to failure and disappointment." → **OPPOSITE** — reverses the theme

### P — Proven
The correct answer. Can be proven with specific text evidence — a quote, detail, or example from the passage.

**What makes it Proven:**
- Directly answers the question asked
- Supported by specific evidence you can point to in the text
- Uses academic/benchmark language (mirrors how the standard is assessed)
- Addresses the FULL scope of the question
- Would still be the best answer even after rereading

**Teaching language:** "You should be able to point to the text and say 'HERE — this is why it's right.'"

**Example (Theme question):**
- "Perseverance through adversity leads to personal growth and transformation." → **PROVEN** — supported by paragraphs 12 and 18

## Two Modes

### Simplified Default (Used on most assessments)
Students select the Proven answer and write a 1-sentence justification explaining WHY it is correct.

**When to use:** Daily work, mini-assessments (Days 5-6), unit assessments, exit tickets with MC.

**Student task:**
1. Read all four choices
2. Identify the Proven answer
3. Select it
4. Write 1 sentence: "This is proven because [text evidence]."

**Format on student materials:**
```
**[Question stem]** (paragraph X)
○ A. [Choice]
○ B. [Choice]
○ C. [Choice]
○ D. [Choice]

Why is your answer proven? ___________________________________
_____________________________________________________________
```

### Full STOP (Teaching exercise — once per unit)
Students label EVERY choice as S, T, O, or P and write a justification for EACH label. This deep analysis builds the elimination habit.

**When to use:** Once per unit as a focused teaching and learning exercise (typically during Days 3-4 Teacher-Led center).

**Student task:**
1. Read all four choices
2. Label each choice: S, T, O, or P
3. Write justification for each label
4. Confirm Proven choice with text evidence

**Format on student materials:**
```
**[Question stem]** (paragraph X)

○ A. [Choice]
   Label: _____ Why? ________________________________________

○ B. [Choice]
   Label: _____ Why? ________________________________________

○ C. [Choice]
   Label: _____ Why? ________________________________________

○ D. [Choice]
   Label: _____ Why? ________________________________________
```

## Teaching Sequence Across the 6-Day Cycle

### Days 1-2: Introduce and Model STOP

**Day 1 (if first unit teaching STOP):**
1. Display STOP anchor chart
2. Explain each category with a non-text example (e.g., "What color is the sky?" with S/T/O/P choices)
3. **I Do:** Teacher reads a passage question, models labeling all four choices aloud
4. **Key script:** "Watch what I do. I don't just pick the one that looks right. I check ALL FOUR. I ask: Is this Silly? Is this Tricky — does it SOUND right but not answer the question? Is this the Opposite? And which one can I PROVE?"

**Day 2:**
1. Review STOP poster
2. **I Do:** Model on a new question from the unit text (Full STOP with labels and justifications)
3. **We Do:** Class works through a question together — students suggest labels, teacher confirms with evidence
4. **You Do:** Students practice 2-3 questions independently (Full STOP mode)
5. **Debrief:** "Hold up — show me your labels. Who found the Tricky one? What made it tricky?"

### Days 3-4: Practice and Deepen

**Teacher-Led center:**
- Full STOP exercise (once per unit) — 2-4 questions with all labels and justifications
- Teacher circulates, checking labels: "You said B is Tricky — tell me WHY it's tricky and not Proven."
- Focus on Tricky vs Proven distinction (the #1 error students make)

**Independent center:**
- Students use Simplified STOP on practice questions (select + justify Proven)
- Organizer work includes question-driven rereading that feeds STOP analysis

### Days 5-6: Apply Independently

**Assessment (Simplified Default mode):**
- All MC questions include space for "Why is your answer proven?"
- Students select answer AND justify with 1 sentence
- Teacher scores both selection and justification

**Key shift:** By Days 5-6, students should use STOP mentally (internal process) even without explicit labels. The justification line keeps the proving habit active.

## Common Student Errors and Fixes

| Error | What's Happening | Fix |
|-------|-----------------|-----|
| Confusing Tricky and Proven | Student sees accurate words from the text and assumes it's Proven | Ask: "Does this answer the QUESTION, or does it just describe something true about the passage?" |
| Confusing Tricky and Opposite | Student sees a partially wrong answer and labels it Opposite | Teach: "Opposite means it says the REVERSE. Tricky means it's partially right but incomplete." |
| Skipping STOP entirely | Student picks first answer that "looks right" | Require written justification for every MC — no justification = no credit |
| Writing weak justifications | "Because it's in the text" without specifics | Require: "In paragraph ___, the text states '___'" format for justifications |
| Labeling everything as Tricky | Student doesn't distinguish categories | Simplify: Start with just S and P (Silly and Proven). Add T and O once those are solid. |
| Not returning to text before STOP | Student tries to STOP from memory | Enforce Step 2 (Return to Text) before STOP — "Show me your highlighted evidence BEFORE you label." |

## ESOL Modifications

### By Proficiency Level

**ESOL Levels 1-2 (Entering/Beginning):**
- Introduce with **S and P only** first — "Find the Silly one. Find the Proven one."
- Add T and O after S/P are consistent (typically 2-3 units later)
- Provide bilingual STOP poster (English + home language)
- Justification can be 1 phrase instead of 1 sentence
- Allow oral justification to partner before writing

**ESOL Level 3 (Developing):**
- Use full S/T/O/P with sentence frames for justification
- Sentence frame: "This is [Proven/Silly/Tricky/Opposite] because in paragraph ___, ___."
- Allow extended time for labeling and justification

**ESOL Levels 4-5 (Expanding/Bridging):**
- Full STOP with standard justification expectations
- May need vocabulary support for question stems only

### ESOL Sentence Frames for STOP

```
Simplified Mode:
"My answer is [letter] because in paragraph ___, the text says '___.'
 This proves that ___."

Full STOP Mode:
"[Letter] is SILLY because ___."
"[Letter] is TRICKY because it sounds right, but ___."
"[Letter] is OPPOSITE because it says the reverse — ___."
"[Letter] is PROVEN because in paragraph ___, ___."
```

### Distractor Design Rules (for assessment-builder)

When creating MC items, each choice MUST be intentionally designed:

| Category | Design Principle | Common Pitfall |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **S — Silly** | Make it clearly wrong but not absurd — should require reading the text to eliminate | Making it SO silly that students don't practice elimination |
| **T — Tricky** | Include accurate details from the text that don't answer the specific question | Making it too similar to Proven (unfair) or too different (not actually tricky) |
| **O — Opposite** | Reverse a key relationship: cause/effect, positive/negative, before/after | Making the reversal obvious by using "not" or "never" |
| **P — Proven** | Use benchmark-aligned academic language. Must be fully provable with text evidence. | Using simpler language than T, making P obviously "the academic one" |

**Balance rule:** S should be eliminable by anyone who read the text. T should catch students who skim. O should catch students who misread details. P should reward students who reread carefully.
