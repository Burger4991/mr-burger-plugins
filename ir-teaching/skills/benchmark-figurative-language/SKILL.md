# ELA.10.R.3.1 - Figurative Language & Mood (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.3.1**: Interpret figurative language and analyze its contribution to mood.

## Benchmark Clarifications
This benchmark has two interconnected parts:
1. **Interpret figurative language** (what it means)
2. **Analyze its contribution to mood** (what emotion/atmosphere it creates)

**Common Figurative Language Types:**
- **Simile**: Comparison using "like" or "as" (The moon was like a silver coin)
- **Metaphor**: Direct comparison without "like/as" (The moon was a silver coin)
- **Personification**: Giving human qualities to non-human things (The wind whispered)
- **Hyperbole**: Exaggeration (I'm so hungry I could eat a horse)
- **Idiom**: Phrase with non-literal meaning (It's raining cats and dogs)
- **Symbolism**: Object representing abstract idea (dove = peace)
- **Imagery**: Descriptive language appealing to senses

**Mood:**
- The **atmosphere** or emotional feeling created for the reader
- Not the same as tone (author's attitude)
- Common moods: ominous, peaceful, tense, joyful, melancholy, mysterious, hopeful

**Key Focus**: Connect figurative language to the mood it creates—NOT just identify devices

## Prerequisite Skills & Common Gaps

**Students may struggle with:**
- Confusing simile and metaphor
- Interpreting what figurative language means (literal vs. figurative)
- Confusing mood (reader's feeling) with tone (author's attitude)
- Identifying figurative language but not analyzing its effect on mood

**Built-in Remediation:**
- Create anchor chart: figurative language types with visual examples
- Practice "what it says" (literal) vs. "what it means" (figurative)
- Start with obvious mood words (dark, scary) before subtle analysis
- Use images to teach mood (show picture, identify feeling, then analyze how elements create it)

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify Figurative Language
Find non-literal language in the text:
- Look for comparisons (like, as, is, was)
- Notice human qualities given to objects/nature
- Find exaggeration or symbolic objects
- Identify sensory details (imagery)

**Label the device**: Simile, metaphor, personification, etc.

**Organizer prompt**: "What figurative language is used? Quote the example."

### Step 2: Interpret the Figurative Meaning
Determine what the figurative language means:
- What is being compared to what?
- What qualities are being transferred?
- What does this suggest or reveal?
- Translate figurative to literal meaning

**Key questions:**
- "What does this literally say?"
- "What does this figuratively mean?"
- "What is the author suggesting?"

**Organizer prompt**: "What does this figurative language mean?"

### Step 3: Identify the Mood
Determine the atmosphere or emotional feeling:
- What emotion does the passage evoke?
- How does it make you feel as a reader?
- What is the overall atmosphere?

**Common mood words:**
- Dark/ominous, peaceful/serene, tense/anxious, joyful/celebratory
- Melancholy/sad, mysterious/eerie, hopeful/optimistic, nostalgic

**Organizer prompt**: "What mood is created?"

### Step 4: Analyze How Figurative Language Contributes to Mood
Connect the figurative language to mood:
- How does this device create or reinforce the mood?
- What specific words create emotional response?
- How do comparisons/imagery evoke feelings?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the figurative language contribute to mood?"

### Step 5: Evaluate the Effect on Reader
Synthesize the analysis:
- What is the cumulative effect of multiple figurative devices?
- How does mood affect our understanding or emotional response?
- What would be lost without this figurative language?

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the overall effect on the reader?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Identification Questions
- What figurative language is used in [line/stanza/paragraph]?
- Identify the simile/metaphor/personification in this passage.
- What sensory details create imagery?

### Interpretation Questions
- What does this metaphor/simile reveal or suggest?
- What multiple meanings could this line/image have?
- How does ambiguity contribute to the poem's effect?
- What is the author suggesting through [figurative language]?

### Mood Questions
- What mood or atmosphere does the author create?
- How does figurative language contribute to mood?
- What emotions or feelings does this passage evoke?

### Analysis Questions
- How does [device] create or reinforce the mood?
- What would be lost if this were stated literally?
- How do multiple figurative devices work together?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Interprets sophisticated or ambiguous figurative language
- Analyzes complex contributions to mood with nuance
- Evaluates effectiveness of author's figurative choices
- Explains how multiple devices create cumulative effect

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Accurately identifies and interprets figurative language
- Analyzes specific contributions to mood with evidence
- Distinguishes mood from tone
- Connects figurative language to emotional effect

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies common figurative language (simile, metaphor)
- Provides basic interpretation of meaning
- Recognizes general mood (happy, sad, scary)
- Makes simple connections to effect

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Difficulty distinguishing figurative from literal language
- Limited or inaccurate interpretation
- Confusion between mood and other concepts
- Minimal analysis of contribution to mood

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Poetry with clear figurative language
- Literary prose with rich imagery
- Texts with identifiable mood
- Passages where figurative language creates specific effects
- Multiple examples for analysis

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Technical or informational texts without figurative language
- Texts with purely literal language
- Passages requiring extensive cultural/historical knowledge
- Texts where mood is absent or neutral

## Common Figurative Language Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- figurative (non-literal, symbolic)
- literal (actual, exact meaning)
- interpret (explain meaning)
- mood (atmosphere, emotional feeling)
- tone (author's attitude)
- contribute (add to, help create)
- evoke (bring out emotion)
- atmosphere (overall feeling)
- ambiguity (multiple possible meanings)

**Figurative Language Terms:**
- simile (comparison with like/as)
- metaphor (direct comparison)
- personification (human qualities to non-human)
- hyperbole (exaggeration)
- imagery (sensory details)
- symbolism (object representing idea)
- idiom (non-literal phrase)

**Mood Words:**
- ominous (threatening)
- melancholy (sad, reflective)
- serene (peaceful, calm)
- tense (stressful, anxious)
- mysterious (puzzling)
- nostalgic (longing for past)
- hopeful (optimistic)

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For excerpt from "The Raven" by Edgar Allan Poe:**

| Quote/Example | Type of Figurative Language | What It Means | Mood Created | How It Contributes to Mood |
|---------------|---------------------------|---------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| "Once upon a midnight dreary, while I pondered, weak and weary" | **Alliteration** (repetition of "w" sound) and **Imagery** (midnight, dreary, weak, weary) | The repetition of the "w" sound creates a weariness you can almost feel. "Midnight dreary" establishes time and tone—not just night, but the darkest, most depressing part. "Weak and weary" shows physical and emotional exhaustion. | **Ominous and melancholy**: The mood is dark, tired, and foreboding. We sense something bad is coming. | The "w" alliteration makes the line drag, mimicking exhaustion. "Dreary" and "weary" are emotionally heavy words that pull down the mood. Midnight (not evening or night, but specifically midnight) is the "witching hour"—mysterious and slightly scary. This opening line immediately submerges us in sadness and dread before anything has even happened. If Poe wrote "One night, I was thinking and felt tired," the factual statement would have no mood at all. |

## ESOL Scaffolds for Figurative Language

**Sentence frames:**
- "This is a [simile/metaphor/personification] because _______."
- "This literally means _______, but figuratively it means _______."
- "The mood is [ominous/peaceful/tense] because _______."
- "The [device] creates this mood by _______."
- "The author compares _______ to _______, which suggests _______."

**Vocabulary support:**
- Create anchor chart with figurative language types, definitions, and visual examples
- Provide "mood word bank" with pictures showing emotions (use faces or atmospheric photos)
- Post simile vs. metaphor anchor: Simile = like/as, Metaphor = IS

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Show image, identify mood together
- Partner interpretation of figurative language before individual writing

**Graphic organizers:**
- Two-column: "What it says" (literal) | "What it means" (figurative)
- Three-column: Device | Meaning | Mood

**Visual supports:**
- Color-code mood words by category (dark = black, peaceful = blue, tense = yellow, etc.)
- Use mood ring analogy for identifying feelings

## Prerequisite Mini-Lessons (Built-In Remediation)

**Day 1 Focus**: Literal vs. Figurative - Practice with obvious idioms ("It's raining cats and dogs" doesn't mean actual cats and dogs)

**Day 2 Focus**: Simile vs. Metaphor - Create anchor chart, practice identification in song lyrics

**Day 3 Focus**: What is mood? - Use images/film clips to identify feeling, discuss how visual elements create it

**Day 4 Focus**: Interpreting figurative language - "What it says" vs. "What it means" with guided practice

**Day 5+**: Connect figurative language to mood in target text with gradual release

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Interpret figurative language and analyze its contribution to mood

**Key Process**: Identify figurative language → Interpret meaning → Identify mood → Analyze contribution → Evaluate effect

**Essential Question**: "What does this figurative language mean, and how does it create mood?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students identify devices but don't interpret meaning OR they skip connecting figurative language to mood

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "What does this mean? What feeling does this create? How does the figurative language create that feeling?"

## Feedback Checkpoints

### Day 4 Checkpoint: Identifying Figurative Language & Mood

**What to check:**
- Students correctly identify types of figurative language (metaphor, simile, personification, hyperbole, imagery)
- Students interpret what the figurative language means (not just label the device)
- Students identify the mood/atmosphere created
- Students explain how the figurative language creates or contributes to mood
- Students cite evidence with paragraph/line numbers

**Quality indicators:**
- Device correctly labeled (simile vs. metaphor, personification, etc.)
- Interpretation goes beyond literal meaning
- Mood words are precise (ominous, melancholy, serene—not just "happy" or "sad")
- Connection between device and mood is explicit ("The personification creates tension because...")
- Multiple examples show pattern of mood creation

**Common errors to address:**
- Only identifying device type without interpreting meaning
- Confusing mood with tone (author's attitude vs. reader's feeling)
- Listing figurative language without analyzing effect
- Vague mood labels without textual support
- Missing citations or paragraph numbers

**Intervention moves:**
- Conference: "What does this metaphor mean? What feeling does it create?"
- Model: "Watch me interpret this simile and connect it to mood"
- Redirect: "You found the personification—now how does it make us feel?"
- Anchor chart reminder: Post mood word bank with precise vocabulary

**Target for Day 5-6:**
Students who can identify and interpret should move toward analyzing HOW multiple devices create cumulative mood effects and evaluating effectiveness of author's choices.

### Day 6 Checkpoint: Analyzing Figurative Language & Mood Connection

**What students should demonstrate:**
- Claims that connect figurative language to mood (not just device identification)
- Analysis of HOW language creates specific emotional effects
- Precise mood vocabulary with textual evidence
- Explanation of author's purpose in using figurative devices

**Quality issues to watch for:**

*Issue 1: Device identification without effect*
- Student identifies simile/metaphor but doesn't explain what it means or how it creates mood
- Fix: "You found the device—now tell me what feeling it creates and WHY"

*Issue 2: Missing connection between language and mood*
- Student identifies mood separately from figurative language analysis
- No explicit link showing HOW the device creates the feeling
- Fix: Model with sentence frame: "The [device] creates a [mood] mood because [specific words/comparison]..."

*Issue 3: Generic mood labels without evidence*
- Uses vague words like "happy," "sad," "scary" instead of precise mood vocabulary
- Mood claim lacks supporting quotes or analysis
- Fix: Provide mood word bank; require quote + explanation for each mood claim

*Issue 4: Lists devices without showing purpose*
- Student catalogs figurative language but doesn't synthesize or evaluate effectiveness
- No analysis of cumulative effect or author's choices
- Fix: Ask "What would be lost if this were stated literally?" or "How do these devices work together?"

**Teacher focus targets:**
- Show HOW language creates mood (not just that it does)
- Connect device to mood/tone explicitly
- Move beyond identification to interpretation and analysis
- Use precise mood vocabulary (ominous, melancholy, serene vs. scary, sad, peaceful)

**Proficiency indicators (Level 4):**
- Accurate identification AND interpretation of figurative language
- Specific analysis of contributions to mood with evidence
- Distinguishes mood from tone
- Connects figurative language to emotional effect on reader

**Extension for advanced students (Level 5):**
- Analyze complex or ambiguous figurative language
- Evaluate effectiveness of author's figurative choices
- Explain cumulative effect of multiple devices
- Consider what would be lost with literal language
