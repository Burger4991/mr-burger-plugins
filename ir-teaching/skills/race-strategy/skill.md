# RACE Strategy — 10th Grade Intensive Reading

## Purpose

RACE is the **default constructed response framework** used in every unit unless the unit is argument-heavy (in which case CER is used instead — see `cer-writing-guide`). RACE teaches students to write complete, evidence-based analytical responses.

**The core habit:** "Don't just answer. PROVE your answer with the text."

**ACE** (Answer, Cite, Explain) is the **shortened form** of RACE — RACE without the Restate step. ACE is used for quick, low-stakes responses (exit tickets, bellringer written items, short-answer questions). RACE is used for full constructed responses on assessments and organizer analysis.

**Choosing per unit:** RACE or CER is chosen once per unit. All CR activities, scaffolds, rubrics, and assessments within that unit use the chosen framework. RACE is default; CER is for argument-heavy units (R.2.4, R.2.3). See `cer-writing-guide` for the full CER framework.

## The RACE Components

### R — Restate the Question
Turn the question into a statement. This forces students to re-read and understand what's being asked before they start writing.

**What students do:**
- Read the question carefully
- Identify the key words
- Rewrite the question as the beginning of a statement

**Why it matters:**
- Prevents answering a different question than the one asked
- Creates a clear topic sentence for the response
- Builds the habit of engaging with the question before writing
- **Key struggle area:** Students skip this and jump straight to answering, which leads to off-topic or incomplete responses

**Quality progression:**

| Level | Example (Question: "How does the author develop the theme?") |
|-------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Below | Starts writing without any reference to the question |
| Approaching | "The theme is about..." (paraphrases but doesn't restate) |
| Meets | "The author develops the theme by..." (restates using question language) |
| Exceeds | "The author develops the theme of perseverance through adversity by..." (restates with specificity) |

**Teaching language:** "Before you answer, rewrite the question as a statement. Use the same words from the question."

**Sentence frame:** "The question is asking about _____. [Key words from question] _____."

### A — Answer the Question
Provide a clear, direct answer to what's being asked. This is the student's claim or interpretation.

**What students do:**
- State their answer clearly and directly
- Use academic vocabulary from the benchmark
- Be specific — not vague or overly general

**Why it matters:**
- Forces a commitment to a position before looking for evidence
- Ensures the response addresses what was asked
- Academic language practice aligns with how benchmarks are assessed

**Quality progression:**

| Level | Example (Theme question) |
|-------|--------------------------|
| Below | "It has a theme." |
| Approaching | "The theme is about hard work." |
| Meets | "...the character's journey from failure to success." |
| Exceeds | "...the protagonist's repeated failures and eventual transformation, showing that growth requires struggle." |

**Common error:** Students give vague, one-word answers ("symbolism," "the theme") instead of specific, complete answers.

**Teaching language:** "Your answer should be specific enough that someone who didn't read the text would understand your point."

### C — Cite Text Evidence
Support the answer with specific evidence from the passage. This is where students prove their answer.

**What students do:**
- Select the most relevant quote or detail from the text
- Put direct quotes in quotation marks
- Include the paragraph number
- Keep quotes brief (1-2 sentences max)

**Why it matters:**
- Builds the evidence-based thinking required on BEST assessments
- Prevents unsupported opinions or guesses
- Teaches proper citation habits

**Requirements by tier:**
- **Lite:** No citation required (Semester 1 — focus is R+A only)
- **Standard:** 1 relevant quote with paragraph number
- **Full:** 1-2 relevant quotes with paragraph numbers

**Quality progression:**

| Level | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Below | No evidence or vague reference ("The text says stuff about it") |
| Approaching | Evidence present but no citation ("The character says she'll try again") |
| Meets | "In paragraph 12, the text states, 'After three failed attempts, Maria finally succeeded.'" |
| Exceeds | "In paragraph 12, the narrator reflects, 'After the third rejection, something shifted,' and later in paragraph 18, 'The mountain had not grown smaller; she had simply learned to climb.'" |

**Citation format:** "In paragraph [#], the author/text states, '[exact quote].'"

**Common errors:**
- Starting a sentence with the quote (teach: introduce the quote first)
- Quoting 3+ sentences (teach: keep it to 1-2 sentences max)
- Choosing evidence that relates to the topic but doesn't prove the answer
- Forgetting paragraph numbers

**Teaching language:** "Don't just drop a quote. Introduce it, cite it, and make sure it PROVES your answer — not just relates to the topic."

### E — Explain the Connection
Connect the evidence back to the answer. This is where analytical thinking happens — the "so what?" step.

**What students do:**
- Explain HOW the evidence supports their answer
- Use analytical language (not summary)
- Connect specifically to the benchmark skill being assessed
- Make the thinking visible — don't assume the reader sees the connection

**Why it matters:**
- This is the hardest component and where most students lose points
- Separates summary ("this happened") from analysis ("this shows that...")
- Directly assesses the analytical thinking required by BEST standards
- **Key struggle area:** Students cite evidence then stop, assuming the connection is obvious

**The Explain Test:** If you can remove the evidence and the explanation still makes sense, the explanation is too general. Good explanation is anchored to the specific evidence.

**Quality progression:**

| Level | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Below | No explanation |
| Approaching | "This shows the theme." (names it but doesn't explain HOW) |
| Meets | "This demonstrates that the author uses the character's journey to show that persistence overcomes obstacles." |
| Exceeds | "This reveals that the author develops the theme not through triumph but through transformation — the character's growth comes from within, suggesting that perseverance changes the person, not the circumstances." |

**Summary vs. Analysis:**

| Summary (Wrong) | Analysis (Right) |
|------------------|-----------------|
| "This shows that the character worked hard." | "This demonstrates that the author uses the character's repeated failures to develop the theme that growth requires struggle — the failures are not obstacles but necessary steps." |
| "The author uses figurative language." | "The metaphor comparing her journey to 'climbing a mountain with no summit' reveals that perseverance is valuable not for its destination but for the strength gained during the ascent." |

**Key analytical verbs:** demonstrates, reveals, illustrates, suggests, emphasizes, reinforces, highlights, underscores, conveys, develops

**Sentence starters (scaffold progression):**
- Days 1-2: "This shows that _____ because _____."
- Days 3-4: "This demonstrates/reveals/illustrates that _____."
- Days 5-6: Remove starters — students explain independently

**Teaching language:** "Don't just tell me WHAT the evidence says. Tell me WHY it matters and HOW it connects to your answer."

## ACE — The Shortened Form

ACE = RACE without the Restate step. Used for quick, low-stakes responses where a full RACE would be too heavy.

### When to Use ACE vs RACE

| Context | Use |
|---------|-----|
| Exit tickets | ACE |
| Bellringer written response (1 per day) | ACE |
| Short-answer questions during Independent work | ACE |
| Assessment constructed response (Days 5-6) | RACE |
| Organizer analysis rows (Days 3-4) | ACE (analysis column = Explain practice) |
| Any prompt asking for a "complete paragraph" | RACE |
| Anything scored with the CR rubric | RACE |

### ACE Format

**A** — Answer the question directly
**C** — Cite text evidence with paragraph number
**E** — Explain how the evidence supports your answer

**Length:** 1-3 sentences (vs. RACE's 4-6 sentences)

**Student format:**
```
[Answer]. In paragraph ___, the text states, "___." This shows that ___.
```

**Example:**
*Question: What does the word "resilient" most likely mean in paragraph 4?*

**ACE Response:** The word "resilient" most likely means able to recover from difficulty. In paragraph 4, the author describes how "she bounced back after every setback, stronger than before." This shows that "resilient" refers to someone who recovers and grows from challenges.

## Three-Tier Progression

Students build toward full RACE across the school year. The tier determines how many components are expected.

### Tier 1: Lite (Semester 1)
**Components:** R + A only (Restate and Answer)

**Purpose:** Build the foundation — the habit of restating the question and giving a clear, direct answer.

**What students practice:**
- Turning questions into statements
- Giving specific, complete answers
- Using academic vocabulary

**What's NOT expected yet:**
- Text evidence citation
- Analytical explanation

**Scoring:** 2-point scale (Restate quality + Answer quality)

**ACE at Lite tier:** A only (just the Answer)

### Tier 2: Standard (Semester 2 Start)
**Components:** R + A + C (Restate, Answer, Cite)

**Purpose:** Add rigor through text evidence. Students now must prove their answers.

**What students practice:**
- Everything from Lite PLUS
- Selecting relevant evidence
- Proper citation format (quotation marks + paragraph number)

**What's NOT expected yet:**
- Full analytical explanation (though teachers model it)

**Scoring:** 3-point scale (Restate + Answer + Evidence quality)

**ACE at Standard tier:** A + C (Answer and Cite)

### Tier 3: Full (Semester 2 Mid+)
**Components:** R + A + C + E (complete RACE)

**Purpose:** Complete analytical response — the target by year-end.

**What students practice:**
- Everything from Standard PLUS
- Explaining HOW evidence supports the answer
- Analytical language and benchmark-level thinking

**Scoring:** 4-point scale (Restate + Answer + Evidence + Explain quality) — see Scoring Rubric below

**ACE at Full tier:** A + C + E (Answer, Cite, Explain)

## Teaching Sequence Across the 6-Day Cycle

RACE skill-building is embedded in every unit, regardless of tier level. Each phase of the cycle targets the component students struggle with most.

### Days 1-2: Restate Practice

**Focus:** Build the habit of turning the question into a statement before answering.

**Activities:**
- Bellringer includes 1 written response where students must restate before answering
- Teacher models restating 2-3 questions during Teacher-Led center
- Independent work includes "Restate the question" line above every written response

**I Do script (Day 1):**
"Watch what I do before I answer. I don't just start writing. I take the question and turn it into the beginning of my answer. The question says 'How does the author develop the theme?' So I start with: 'The author develops the theme by...' See? I used the SAME WORDS from the question. Now I know exactly what I'm answering."

**We Do (Day 2):**
Teacher shows 3 questions. Class collaborates on restating each one. Students practice independently on 2 more.

**Check:** "Read me your restate. Did you use words from the question?"

### Days 3-4: Explain Scaffolding

**Focus:** Build the analytical "so what?" habit — connecting evidence to the answer.

**Activities:**
- Organizer "Analysis" column = Explain practice (gradual release: I Do → We Do → You Do)
- Teacher models explaining evidence aloud during I Do rows ("Watch how I connect this back...")
- Students practice with sentence starters during We Do and You Do w/ Partner
- Peer review: Partners check — "Did you explain WHY the evidence matters?"

**I Do script (Day 3):**
"I found my evidence — great. But I'm not done. Now I have to explain WHY this matters. Watch: The text says '...' [reads evidence]. So what does this SHOW? It demonstrates that... [models explanation]. See how I didn't just repeat the evidence? I told you what it MEANS."

**We Do (Day 3-4):**
Teacher provides evidence. Students write only the Explain component. Share and compare.

**Sentence starters:** "This shows that... because..." / "This demonstrates..." / "This reveals..."

### Days 5-6: Full RACE Independent

**Focus:** Students write complete RACE independently using the current tier.

**Activities:**
- Assessment includes 1 CR prompt (always benchmark-aligned)
- No sentence frames provided (except ESOL modifications)
- Students use full framework at current tier level
- Teacher scores using the CR rubric

**Key teaching moment:** "You've been practicing each piece all week. Restate — you did that Days 1-2. Explain — you practiced that in your organizer Days 3-4. Now put it ALL together."

## Scoring Rubric

### RACE Rubric (4-point scale — Full Tier)

| Component | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|-----------|---|---|---|
| **R — Restate** (0-2) | Missing or off-topic | Partial — some question language used | Clear restatement using key words from question |
| **A — Answer** (0-2) | Missing or vague | Present but incomplete or too general | Clear, specific, addresses the full question |
| **C — Cite** (0-2) | Missing | Evidence present but no citation or irrelevant | Relevant quote with paragraph citation |
| **E — Explain** (0-2) | Missing | Present but summarizes instead of analyzes | Connects evidence to answer, explains HOW |

**Total: 8 points**

**Score interpretation:**
- 7-8: Exceeds — ready for grade-level analytical writing
- 5-6: Meets — demonstrates benchmark understanding
- 3-4: Approaching — needs targeted support on weak component
- 0-2: Below — needs intensive modeling and scaffolding

### Tier-Adjusted Scoring

| Tier | Components Scored | Max Points |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| Lite (Sem 1) | R + A | 4 points |
| Standard (Sem 2 start) | R + A + C | 6 points |
| Full (Sem 2 mid+) | R + A + C + E | 8 points |

### ACE Scoring (Quick Responses)

| Component | 0 | 1 |
|-----------|---|---|
| **A — Answer** | Missing or vague | Clear and specific |
| **C — Cite** | Missing or irrelevant | Relevant quote with paragraph number |
| **E — Explain** | Missing or summary | Connects evidence to answer |

**Total: 3 points** (or tier-adjusted: A-only = 1pt, A+C = 2pt, A+C+E = 3pt)

## Complete RACE Model Response

**Question:** How does the author develop the theme of perseverance in the passage?

**[R — Restate]** The author develops the theme of perseverance throughout the passage in several ways.

**[A — Answer]** Specifically, the author shows that perseverance through repeated failure ultimately leads to transformation and self-discovery.

**[C — Cite]** In paragraph 12, the narrator reflects, "After the third rejection, something shifted — not in the world around her, but in the way she carried herself through it." Additionally, in paragraph 18, the author writes, "The mountain had not grown smaller; she had simply learned to climb."

**[E — Explain]** These passages reveal that the author develops the theme not through a single triumphant moment, but through a gradual internal shift. The first quote demonstrates that the character's growth comes from within — suggesting that perseverance changes the person, not the circumstances. The metaphor in paragraph 18 reinforces this by showing that obstacles remain unchanged; it is the character's capacity that evolves. Together, these details develop the theme that perseverance is not about the world becoming easier but about the individual becoming stronger.

## Common Student Errors and Fixes

| Error | What's Happening | Fix |
|-------|-----------------|-----|
| Skipping Restate | Student jumps straight to answering | Require "R:" label before every response. No credit for R without it. |
| Vague answer | "The author uses literary devices" | Ask: "WHICH device? To do WHAT? About WHAT theme?" |
| Evidence without explanation | Student quotes then stops | Ask: "HOW does this prove your answer? What does it SHOW?" |
| Explanation that summarizes | "This shows the character was sad" | Ask: "So what? Why does this MATTER for the theme/benchmark?" |
| Evidence not relevant | Quote exists in text but doesn't support answer | Ask: "Does this PROVE your answer, or just relate to the topic?" |
| No paragraph citation | Evidence without location | Require format: "In paragraph [#]..." |
| Quote too long | Student copies 3+ sentences | Teach: "Keep quotes to 1-2 sentences. The shorter and more specific, the better." |
| Starting with the quote | "The text says '...' The theme is..." | Teach: "Introduce the quote first. Say WHERE it is, THEN quote." |

## ESOL Modifications

### By Proficiency Level

**ESOL Levels 1-2 (Entering/Beginning):**
- Stay at Lite tier longer (R+A only through most of Semester 1)
- Provide bilingual RACE anchor chart
- Oral rehearsal: student says RACE to partner before writing
- Allow response in L1 with key terms in English
- Sentence frame for ALL components (keep through Day 6)

**ESOL Level 3 (Developing):**
- Follow tier progression but with sentence frames available through Day 6
- Word bank with analytical verbs (demonstrates, reveals, illustrates)
- Reduced evidence requirement: 1 quote instead of 2 for Meets
- Extended time for CR responses

**ESOL Levels 4-5 (Expanding/Bridging):**
- Follow standard tier progression
- Sentence starters (not full frames) available Days 1-4 only
- Standard expectations by Days 5-6

```

### ESOL Sentence Frames for ACE

```

## RACE vs CER — When to Use Which

| Factor | RACE (Default) | CER (Argument Units) |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| **When** | Most units | R.2.4 (Argument), R.2.3 (Purpose & Perspective) |
| **Structure** | Restate → Answer → Cite → Explain | Claim → Evidence → Reasoning |
| **Key difference** | Answer is analytical but not necessarily debatable | Claim must be debatable/interpretive |
| **Student strength** | R builds the restate habit; steps are linear | C requires taking a position students can defend |
| **Student struggle** | E (Explain) — connecting evidence to answer | C (Claim) — students confuse claims with facts |
| **Shortened form** | ACE (A+C+E) | None — CER is already compact |

**Rule:** Choose ONCE per unit. Never mix RACE and CER in the same unit. All CR activities, scaffolds, and assessments use the chosen framework.

For the full CER framework, see `cer-writing-guide`.
