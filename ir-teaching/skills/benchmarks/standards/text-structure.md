# ELA.10.R.2.1 - Text Structure & Features (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.2.1**: Analyze how text structure and text features contribute to meaning and purpose in informational texts.

## Benchmark Clarifications
This benchmark applies to **informational texts** and has two parts:
1. **Text structure**: How the text is organized
2. **Text features**: Visual/design elements (headings, images, captions, etc.)

**Common Text Structures:**
- **Description**: Characteristics or features of a topic
- **Sequence/Chronological**: Events in time order
- **Problem/Solution**: Issue and how to fix it
- **Cause/Effect**: What happened and why
- **Compare/Contrast**: Similarities and differences
- **Classification**: Grouping items by categories

**Common Text Features:**
- Headings/subheadings
- Bold/italicized words
- Graphics (charts, graphs, diagrams, images)
- Captions
- Text boxes/sidebars
- Bulleted/numbered lists
- Table of contents, glossary, index

**Key Focus**: Not just identifying structure/features—analyzing HOW they contribute to meaning and support author's purpose

## Prerequisite Skills & Common Gaps

**Students may struggle with:**
- Confusing structure types (especially cause/effect vs. problem/solution)
- Identifying structure when multiple patterns exist
- Understanding WHY author chose this structure (not just WHAT structure)
- Overlooking text features as "not real reading"

**Built-in Remediation:**
- Create anchor chart with structure types, signal words, and graphic organizers for each
- Practice with short, clear examples of each structure before complex texts
- Model "thinking aloud" about why author chose this structure
- Explicitly teach that text features provide important information

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Text Structure
Determine the organizational pattern:
- How is the information arranged?
- What pattern does the author follow?

**Signal words for each structure:**
- **Description**: "characteristics are," "for example," "such as"
- **Sequence**: "first, next, then, finally," "before, after," dates
- **Problem/Solution**: "the problem is," "the solution," "one way to solve"
- **Cause/Effect**: "because, since, therefore, as a result, consequently"
- **Compare/Contrast**: "similar, different, however, on the other hand, both"

**Organizer prompt**: "What text structure does the author use?"

### Step 2: Identify Text Features
Note visual/design elements:
- What headings, subheadings, or titles appear?
- What graphics or images are included?
- What special formatting is used (bold, italics, bullets)?
- Are there captions, labels, or diagrams?

**Organizer prompt**: "What text features are present?"

### Step 3: Analyze How Structure Supports Purpose
Connect structure to author's purpose:
- **If purpose is to inform about history** → Likely chronological structure
- **If purpose is to persuade about an issue** → Likely problem/solution
- **If purpose is to explain why something happens** → Likely cause/effect
- **If purpose is to help readers understand differences** → Likely compare/contrast

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the structure support the author's purpose?"

### Step 4: Analyze How Features Contribute to Meaning
Determine what each feature adds:
- What information does this graphic provide?
- How does the heading guide understanding?
- Why did the author bold these specific words?
- How do captions clarify or extend the text?

**Organizer prompt**: "How do text features contribute to meaning?"

### Step 5: Evaluate Effectiveness
Assess whether structure/features work:
- Is this structure effective for this purpose?
- Do the features enhance understanding or distract?
- What would be lost without these features?
- Could a different structure be more effective?

**Organizer prompt**: "How effective are the structure and features?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Structure Identification Questions
- What organizational pattern does the author use? (description, problem/solution, chronological, compare/contrast, cause/effect, sequence)
- What signal words reveal the text structure?
- How is the information arranged?

### Structure Analysis Questions
- How does this structure support the author's purpose?
- Why did the author choose this structure?
- How does the structure help readers understand [topic]?

### Text Features Questions
- What text features guide your understanding?
- How does [heading/graphic/caption] contribute to meaning?
- What information do text features provide that the main text doesn't?

### Evaluation Questions
- How effective is this structure for this purpose?
- What would change if the author used a different structure?
- Do the text features enhance or distract from the main text?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Analyzes complex or combined text structures
- Evaluates effectiveness of structure for purpose and audience
- Interprets sophisticated text features and their contributions
- Considers alternative structures and their implications

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Identifies text structure with evidence (signal words)
- Analyzes how structure supports author's purpose
- Explains how text features contribute to meaning
- Uses features to enhance comprehension

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies basic text structure
- Recognizes common text features
- Makes simple connections between structure and purpose
- Notes what features show

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Difficulty identifying structure accurately
- Overlooks or misinterprets text features
- Minimal analysis of how structure/features contribute
- Struggles to connect structure to purpose

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Informational articles, textbook excerpts, manuals
- Texts with clear organizational pattern
- Texts with multiple text features
- Texts where structure choices are purposeful
- Complex texts with combined structures

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Literary fiction (use R.1.x standards)
- Texts with no discernible structure
- Texts without text features (rare in informational texts)
- Purely chronological narratives without informational purpose

## Common Text Structure Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- structure (organization, arrangement)
- feature (element, characteristic)
- contribute (add to, support)
- purpose (reason, goal)
- organize (arrange, order)
- sequence (order, progression)
- contrast (difference)
- compare (similarity and difference)

**Text Structure Types:**
- description (characteristics)
- chronological (time order)
- sequence (steps or stages)
- problem/solution (issue and fix)
- cause/effect (reason and result)
- compare/contrast (similarities/differences)
- classification (categories)

**Text Features:**
- heading/subheading
- caption (text describing image)
- graphic (visual element)
- diagram (labeled illustration)
- sidebar (text box with extra info)
- bold/italics (formatting for emphasis)

**Signal Words by Structure:**
- Cause/Effect: "because, therefore, as a result, consequently, due to"
- Problem/Solution: "problem, solution, resolve, fix, address"
- Compare/Contrast: "similarly, however, on the other hand, both, unlike"
- Sequence: "first, next, then, finally, before, after"

## ESOL Scaffolds for Text Structure

**Sentence frames:**
- "The text structure is [chronological/cause-effect/etc.] because _______."
- "Signal words like '_____' show this structure."
- "This structure helps readers _______ because _______."
- "The [text feature] shows _______ which helps readers _______."
- "This is effective because _______."

**Vocabulary support:**
- Anchor chart with structure types, definitions, signal words, and graphic organizer for each
- Color-code signal words by structure type in texts
- Provide signal words list on student packets

**Graphic organizers:**
- Match structure to graphic organizer (Venn = compare/contrast, flowchart = sequence, etc.)
- Use graphic organizer as note-taking tool

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Preview text features before reading (picture walk)
- Partner identification of structure using signal words
- Discuss how features help understanding

**Text feature scavenger hunt:**
- Find all features before close reading
- Discuss what each might tell us

## Prerequisite Mini-Lessons (Built-In Remediation)

**Day 1 Focus**: What are text structures? Introduce 5 main types with short, obvious examples

**Day 2 Focus**: Signal words - Create anchor chart with signal words for each structure type

**Day 3 Focus**: Text features identification - Use textbook page or infographic to find all features

**Day 4 Focus**: Why structures matter - Compare same information in different structures to show impact

**Day 5+**: Apply to target text with gradual release

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Analyze how text structure and features contribute to meaning and purpose

**Key Process**: Identify structure → Identify features → Analyze how structure supports purpose → Analyze how features contribute → Evaluate effectiveness

**Essential Question**: "How does the author organize information and use text features to help readers understand?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students identify structure/features but don't analyze HOW they contribute to meaning or purpose

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "Why did the author organize it this way?" and "What does this feature add that the main text doesn't?"

## Feedback Checkpoints

### Day 4 Checkpoint: Structure & Features Identification

**What to Check:**
- Has the student identified the organizational pattern (problem-solution, cause-effect, compare-contrast, chronological, description)?
- Are text features noted (headings, graphics, formatting, captions, etc.)?
- Is there analysis of how structure affects meaning/understanding?
- Does the student explain the purpose of the structure choice?

**Quality Criteria:**
- Structure identification is accurate with supporting evidence (signal words, paragraph references)
- Text features are listed with specificity (not just "there's a graph" but "graph showing correlation between X and Y")
- Analysis moves beyond identification to explanation of effect ("This cause/effect structure helps readers understand WHY..." not just "This is cause/effect")
- Connection made between structure and author's purpose

**Red Flags (Needs Intervention):**
- Incorrect structure identification (confusing cause/effect with problem/solution, etc.)
- No signal words or evidence provided
- Text features overlooked or dismissed as unimportant
- Only identifies WHAT structure without explaining WHY or HOW it works

**Feedback Moves:**
- Point to specific signal words: "Look at paragraph 3—what does 'as a result' tell you about the structure?"
- Ask purpose questions: "Why would the author organize it this way instead of chronologically?"
- Highlight features: "What does the sidebar add that the main text doesn't give you?"
- Model thinking: "When I see 'however' and 'on the other hand,' I know the author is comparing..."

### Day 6 Checkpoint: Structure & Features Analysis

**What to Check:**
- Does the answer claim HOW structure/features contribute to meaning or purpose?
- Is there specific analysis (not just labeling)?
- Are text features connected to comprehension (not just listed)?
- Does the response show understanding of WHY the author made these choices?

**Quality Issues to Address:**

**Issue 1: Only Labels Without Effect**
- Student identifies structure ("This is cause/effect") but doesn't explain how it aids understanding
- **Fix**: "You've identified the structure correctly. Now tell me—how does organizing it as cause/effect help readers understand the topic better than if it were chronological?"

**Issue 2: Lists Features Without Purpose**
- Student notes "There's a graph, a sidebar, and bold words" but doesn't explain what each contributes
- **Fix**: "You found all the text features. Now let's dig deeper—what does the graph show that would be hard to explain in words? Why did the author bold those specific terms?"

**Issue 3: Doesn't Show HOW Structure Aids Comprehension**
- Student says "The structure is effective" without explaining mechanism
- **Fix**: "Effective how? Walk me through what the compare/contrast structure helps you see that you might miss if the author just described each topic separately."

**Issue 4: Confuses Structure with Topic**
- Student discusses content instead of organization ("The article is about climate change" vs. "The article uses problem/solution structure")
- **Fix**: "That's what the article is about. But HOW is the information organized? Is it comparing things? Showing causes and effects? Describing problems and solutions?"

**Teacher Focus Targets:**
- Explain EFFECT of structure on meaning (not just identify the structure)
- Show HOW features aid comprehension (not just list features)
- Connect structure to author's purpose (inform, persuade, explain, etc.)
- Distinguish between WHAT (content) and HOW (organization)

**Strong Response Indicators:**
- Uses precise language: "The cause/effect structure reveals the relationship between..." rather than "It's organized well"
- Explains mechanisms: "The graph provides visual evidence that shows the dramatic trend, making the data more compelling than a paragraph of statistics"
- Connects to purpose: "By using problem/solution, the author doesn't just inform about the issue—she positions readers to take action"
- Analyzes choices: "The heading 'The Sleep Disruption Cycle' prepares readers for the circular cause/effect relationship explained in that section"
