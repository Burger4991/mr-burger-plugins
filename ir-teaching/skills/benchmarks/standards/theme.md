# ELA.10.R.1.2 — Theme (Grade 10)

## Standard

**ELA.10.R.1.2**: Analyze and compare universal themes and their development throughout a literary text.

## Benchmark Clarifications (from Planning Guide)

**Clarification 1**: A universal theme is an idea that applies to anyone, anywhere, regardless of cultural differences. Examples include but are not limited to:
- An individual's or a community's confrontation with nature
- An individual's struggle toward understanding, awareness, and/or spiritual enlightenment
- The tension between the ideal and the real
- The conflict between human beings and advancements in technology/science
- The impact of the past on the present
- The inevitability of fate
- The struggle for equality
- The loss of innocence

## ELA Expectations Addressed

- **ELA.K12.EE.1.1**: Cite evidence to explain and justify reasoning
- **ELA.K12.EE.3.1**: Make inferences to support comprehension

## Assessment Limits (from Planning Guide)

**Key limits for assessment design:**
- Items may ask the student to examine how broad universal themes apply to the specifics of the text
- Items should compare or analyze universal themes in a single text
- Items should not ask the student only to identify what the themes are but also ask the student to analyze how those themes are developed

**IMPORTANT LIMITS:**
- Focus must be on theme DEVELOPMENT AND COMPARISON, not theme identification
- Themes must be universal (apply across contexts) rather than character-specific or plot-specific
- Assessment should require students to analyze HOW themes develop and compare themes, not just WHAT themes exist

## Achievement Level Descriptors (from Planning Guide)

### Level 2 (Beginning)
Explains and compares simple or explicitly stated universal themes and their development throughout a grade-level low-to-mid complexity literary text.

### Level 3 (Approaching)
Analyzes and compares simple or explicitly stated universal themes and their development throughout a grade-level low-to-mid complexity literary text.

### Level 4 (Proficient — TARGET)
Analyzes and compares universal themes and their development throughout a grade-level mid-to-high complexity literary text.

### Level 5 (Mastery)
Analyzes thoroughly and compares complex and/or abstract universal themes and their development throughout a grade-level high complexity literary text(s).

### Progression Notes (Beginner → Mastery)

Use ALDs to scaffold instruction across the unit:
- **Days 1-2 (Foundation)**: Target Level 2-3 — students explain simple/explicitly stated themes in accessible text with clear evidence markers
- **Days 3-4 (Analysis)**: Target Level 3-4 — students analyze how themes develop across text sections with textual support
- **Day 5 (Assessment)**: Assessment items should span Levels 2-5 to capture full range of student understanding
- **Day 6 (Revision)**: Use ALD language in feedback to show students exactly where they are and what mastery looks like

## Task Demands and Question Stems (from Planning Guide)

### Task Demand 1: Analyze the development of universal themes in a text

| Question Stem | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| How does the author develop the theme of ___? | Direct development analysis |
| What universal theme is developed through ___ (literary element)? | Element → theme connection |

| Part A: What is the universal theme in the passage / passage ___? / Part B: How is the theme developed? | Two-part development |

### Task Demand 2: Compare the development of universal themes within a text

| Question Stem | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| How are the themes ___ and ___ developed in the passage? | Direct comparison of theme development |
| Which two statements show how the themes of ___ and ___ developed in the passage? | Evidence-based comparison |
| Part A: Which two universal themes are present in the passage? / Part B: How are the two themes developed differently? | Two-part theme identification and comparative analysis |
| Part A: How are the themes ___ and ___ in the passage? / Part B: Which statements from the passage support your response to Part A? | Two-part comparative analysis with evidence |
| Complete the table by selecting the universal themes present in the passage and how they are developed. | Multi-select comparison task |

### Using Question Stems in Units

When building MC questions for this benchmark:
1. Select stems from the task demands above — do NOT create custom stems
2. Fill in the blanks with text-specific details (line numbers, character names, universal themes)
3. Include a mix of single-select and Part A/Part B questions
4. Include questions from BOTH Task Demand 1 (analysis) AND Task Demand 2 (comparison)
5. Include at least one Part A/Part B question per assessment
6. Tag each question with the task demand it addresses

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify Universal Themes
Ask: Does this idea apply to anyone, anywhere, regardless of context?
- NOT character-specific themes ("Harry learns magic")
- NOT plot summaries ("The character overcomes obstacles")
- YES universal themes ("Confrontation with nature reveals inner strength")
- **Organizer prompt**: "What universal ideas does the author explore?"

### Step 2: Identify When/How Theme Is Introduced
Look for theme introduction through:
- Character actions, thoughts, or dialogue
- Setting descriptions and how they create atmosphere
- Conflict establishment or tension
- Symbol or motif introduction
- Narrative voice and tone
- **Organizer prompt**: "When and how is the theme first introduced in the text?"

### Step 3: Gather Evidence of Theme Development
Track theme development by noting:
- How the theme appears in the beginning, middle, and end of the text
- Character growth or change related to theme
- Pivotal moments that intensify or challenge the theme
- Recurring symbols, motifs, or language related to theme
- Shifts in tone, mood, or perspective tied to theme
- **Organizer prompt**: "What evidence shows how the theme develops from beginning to end?"

### Step 4: Analyze Development Trajectory
Trace how the theme:
- Emerges or is introduced (beginning)
- Develops, deepens, or is challenged (middle)
- Resolves, concludes, or is reinforced (end)
- **Organizer prompt**: "How does the theme change or deepen throughout the text?"

### Step 5: Synthesize — Contribution to Overall Meaning
- What message about this universal theme emerges?
- How do textual elements work together to develop the theme?
- **Organizer prompt**: "What does the text ultimately say about this universal theme?"

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For "The Necklace" by Guy de Maupassant:**

| Universal Theme | When/How Introduced | Beginning | Middle | End | Overall Development |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Appearance vs. Reality** | The theme is introduced when Mathilde says she cannot attend the ball without "worthy" jewelry, revealing her obsession with outward appearance (para 2). | "She was one of those pretty and charming girls born, as if by an error of fate, into a family of clerks" (para 1) — She feels her appearance doesn't match her "true" worth; her reality is clerk's daughter, but she perceives herself as worthy of nobility. | She borrows the necklace, believing it will transform how others see her (para 7). The irony deepens: she loses the necklace and spends years in poverty to replace it, her appearance gradually destroyed by hardship (para 10). | The final revelation: the borrowed necklace was fake (para 12). After years of sacrifice for appearance, she learns the distinction between the two meant nothing. | **Universal theme**: The pursuit of external validation and appearance often blinds us to the true value of what we have, and the cost of maintaining false appearances can destroy our actual lives. |

## Common Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words**: develop, analyze, universal, emerge, reveal, convey, reinforce, implicit, abstract, trajectory

**Literary Terms**: theme, universal theme, motif, symbol, tone, mood, irony, foreshadowing, climax, resolution, character arc

**Theme-Specific Vocabulary**:
- **universal** — applying to anyone, anywhere, regardless of context or culture
- **develop** — to show how a theme grows, deepens, or changes over time
- **explicit** — clearly stated or obvious
- **implicit** — suggested or hinted at, not directly stated
- **motif** — a recurring element (image, phrase, symbol) that reinforces theme
- **resolve** — how a theme concludes or is addressed at the end of the text

## ESOL Scaffolds

**Sentence frames:**
- "The universal theme of _______ appears when _______."
- "In [beginning/middle/end] of the text, the theme develops because _______."
- "The author uses _______ (element) to show that _______."
- "The theme changes from _______ to _______ because _______."

**Vocabulary support**:
- Pre-teach universal theme concept with non-text examples (e.g., "This happens to people everywhere")
- Create visual chart of universal vs. character-specific themes
- Provide word bank of universal theme examples for reference during unit

**Think-Pair-Share**: Partner discussion of "How do you see this theme in real life?" before writing

## Spiraling This Benchmark

R.1.2 pairs with other benchmarks and should be introduced as PRIMARY in Unit 2-3, then spiraled into every literary unit thereafter:

**R.1.2 pairs with:**
- **ELA.10.R.1.1 (Literary Elements)**: R.1.1 analyzes HOW elements add meaning; R.1.2 focuses specifically on how elements develop universal themes
- **ELA.10.R.1.3 (Perspective)**: Perspective/point of view can reveal or reinforce theme
- **ELA.10.R.3.1 (Figurative Language)**: Figurative language can reinforce or develop theme — align to R.3.1 when focus is figurative language → mood/meaning specifically

**Spiraling schedule suggestion:**
- Unit 2-3: R.1.2 as PRIMARY (introduce universal theme concept)
- Unit 4-5: R.1.2 as PRIMARY or CO-PRIMARY with R.1.1
- Unit 6+: R.1.2 spiraled into every literary unit as a foundational skill

- [ ] I found evidence from the beginning, middle, and end of the text
- [ ] I included paragraph/line numbers for all evidence
- [ ] I explained HOW the theme develops (not just WHAT the theme is)
- [ ] My work follows the I Do model for quality

Teacher Review:
- [ ] Themes are universal (apply to anyone, anywhere, regardless of context)
- [ ] Evidence spans beginning, middle, and end of text
- [ ] Text evidence cited with paragraph or line numbers
- [ ] Analysis explains HOW themes develop (not just identification)
- [ ] Quality matches Level 3-4 ALD descriptors

### Day 6: CER/ACE Response Checkpoint

- [ ] CLAIM names and explains the universal theme
- [ ] EVIDENCE includes 2+ quotes with paragraph/line numbers showing development
- [ ] REASONING explains HOW the theme develops from beginning to end
- [ ] EVIDENCE shows theme introduced, developed, and resolved/reinforced
- [ ] Quality matches Level 3-4 ALD descriptors
