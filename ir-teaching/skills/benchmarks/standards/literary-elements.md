# ELA.10.R.1.1 — Literary Elements (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.1.1**: Analyze how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a literary text.

## Benchmark Clarifications (from Planning Guide)

**Clarification 1**: Key elements of a literary text are setting, plot, characterization, conflict, point of view, theme, and tone.

**Clarification 2**: For layers of meaning, any methodology or model may be used as long as students understand that text may have multiple layers and that authors use techniques to achieve those layers. A very workable model for looking at layers of meaning is that of I.A. Richards:
- **Layer 1**: The literal level — what the words actually mean
- **Layer 2**: Mood — those feelings that are evoked in the reader
- **Layer 3**: Tone — the author's attitude
- **Layer 4**: Author's purpose — interpretation of author's purpose as it is often inferred

**Clarification 3**: Style is the way in which the writer uses techniques for effect. It is distinct from meaning but can be used to make the author's message more effective. The components of style are diction, syntax, grammar, and use of figurative language. Style helps to create the author's voice.

## ELA Expectations Addressed
- **ELA.K12.EE.1.1**: Cite evidence to explain and justify reasoning
- **ELA.K12.EE.3.1**: Make inferences to support comprehension

## Assessment Limits (from Planning Guide)

Items may ask the student how key elements contribute to meaning and/or style in a literary text. Key elements and components of style are as listed in the benchmark clarifications, and layers of meaning include, but are not limited to, literal meaning, mood, tone, and purpose. Items should not refer to or name any particular model that defines layers of meaning.

**IMPORTANT LIMITS:**
- Items may address the relationship between key elements and style as a whole or its component parts
- Items that meet the specific criteria to be aligned to **ELA.10.R.1.2** (the development of universal themes), **ELA.10.R.1.4** (the creation of meaning and especially ambiguity in poetry), or **ELA.10.R.3.1** (the connection between figurative language and mood) should be aligned to those benchmarks rather than this one
- The focus of questions about layers of meaning should be on the DEVELOPMENT of those layers rather than simply asking the student to identify what the theme, mood, or tone IS

## Achievement Level Descriptors (from Planning Guide)

### Level 2 (Beginning)
Explains how simplistic key elements enhance or add a single layer of meaning and/or style in a grade-level low-to-mid complexity literary text.

### Level 3 (Approaching)
Analyzes how simplistic key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a grade-level low-to-mid complexity literary text.

### Level 4 (Proficient — TARGET)
Analyzes how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a grade-level mid-to-high complexity literary text.

### Level 5 (Mastery)
Analyzes thoroughly how complex key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in grade-level high complexity literary text(s).

### Progression Notes (Beginner → Mastery)
Use ALDs to scaffold instruction across the unit:
- **Days 1-2 (Foundation)**: Target Level 2-3 — students describe/explain simplistic elements in accessible text
- **Days 3-4 (Analysis)**: Target Level 3-4 — students explain how elements add layers of meaning with evidence
- **Day 5 (Assessment)**: Assessment items should span Levels 2-5 to capture full range
- **Day 6 (Revision)**: Use ALD language in feedback to show students exactly where they are and what mastery looks like

## Task Demands and Question Stems (from Planning Guide)

### Task Demand 1: Analyze how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning

| Question Stem | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| Select the sentence from paragraph ____ that shows that ____. | Evidence identification |
| How does the plot in paragraph ____ develop the theme of ____? | Plot → theme connection |
| How does the setting affect the plot? | Setting → plot interaction |
| How does the last paragraph reveal ____? | Element revelation |
| How does paragraph ____ create mood? | Element → mood (Layer 2) |
| What is the purpose of the monologue (dialogue/aside/soliloquy) in the scene (paragraph/excerpt)? | Dialogue function |

### Task Demand 2: Analyze how key elements enhance or add to components of style

| Question Stem | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| How does the use of ____ (key element) enhance the author's style? | Element → style connection |
| How does the author develop the ____ style in the passage? | Overall style analysis |
| Part A: How does the author's style impact the overall meaning of the text? / Part B: How does ____ (key element) enhance that style? | Two-part style/meaning |

### Using Question Stems in Units
When building MC questions for this benchmark:
1. Select stems from the task demands above — do NOT create custom stems
2. Fill in the blanks with text-specific details (paragraph numbers, character names, themes)
3. Include a mix of both task demands (meaning AND style)
4. Include at least one Part A/Part B question per assessment
5. Tag each question with the task demand it addresses

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Key Elements
Key elements (per clarification): setting, plot, characterization, conflict, point of view, theme, tone
- **NOT just identification**: Students must explain HOW elements add meaning
- **Organizer prompt**: "What key elements does the author use in this passage?"

### Step 2: Identify the Layers of Meaning
Use the I.A. Richards model:
- Layer 1: What do the words literally mean?
- Layer 2: What mood (feelings) does this create in the reader?
- Layer 3: What is the author's tone (attitude)?
- Layer 4: What is the author's purpose?
- **Organizer prompt**: "What layers of meaning does this element create?"

### Step 3: Analyze How Elements Enhance Meaning
Look for connections between elements and layers:
- How does SETTING create MOOD (Layer 2)?
- How does CHARACTERIZATION reveal TONE (Layer 3)?
- How does CONFLICT develop PURPOSE (Layer 4)?
- **Organizer prompt**: "How does this element add to the layers of meaning?"

### Step 4: Analyze Style Techniques
Identify diction, syntax, grammar, figurative language:
- What word choices stand out?
- How does sentence structure create effect?
- What is the author's voice?
- **Organizer prompt**: "What techniques does the author use, and what effect do they create?"

### Step 5: Synthesize — Contribution to Overall Meaning/Style
- What message emerges from all layers?
- How do the elements work together?
- **Organizer prompt**: "How do these elements and techniques create the text's meaning and style?"

## Common Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words**: enhance, contribute, layer, reveal, convey, develop, create, establish, reinforce, reflect

**Literary Terms**: setting, plot, characterization, conflict, point of view, theme, tone, mood, diction, syntax, foreshadowing, irony, symbolism, exposition, climax

## ESOL Scaffolds

**Sentence frames:**
- "The author uses _______ (element) to create _______ (mood/tone)."
- "In paragraph ____, the _______ (element) adds a layer of meaning because _______."
- "The _______ (technique) contributes to the author's style by _______."

**Vocabulary support**: Pre-teach layers of meaning model with visual diagram (4 layers stacked)

**Think-Pair-Share**: Partner discussion of "What mood does this create?" before writing

## Spiraling This Benchmark

R.1.1 is a PRIMARY benchmark that should appear in most literary text units. It spirals with:
- **R.1.2 (Theme)**: R.1.1 analyzes HOW elements develop meaning; R.1.2 focuses specifically on universal themes
- **R.1.3 (Coming of Age)**: Literary elements are tools R.1.1 analyzes; R.1.3 focuses specifically on how coming-of-age experiences transform perspective and how the author represents conflicting perspectives
- **R.1.4 (Meaning in Poetry)**: R.1.1 analyzes elements generally; R.1.4 focuses specifically on meaning and ambiguity in poetry
- **R.3.1 (Figurative Language)**: Figurative language contributes to style (R.1.1) AND creates mood (R.3.1) — align to R.3.1 when the focus is figurative language → mood specifically

**Spiraling schedule suggestion:**
- Unit 1-2: R.1.1 as PRIMARY (introduce layers of meaning model)
- Unit 3-4: R.1.1 as SECONDARY with R.1.2, R.1.4, or R.1.3 as primary
- Unit 5+: R.1.1 spiraled into every literary unit as a foundational skill

## Feedback Checkpoints

### Day 4: Organizer Checkpoint
Student Self-Check:
- [ ] I analyzed all key elements (at least 3 different elements)
- [ ] I identified layers of meaning (not just literal)
- [ ] I cited evidence with paragraph numbers
- [ ] I explained HOW the element enhances meaning or style
- [ ] My work follows the I Do model for quality

Teacher Review:
- [ ] Multiple key elements analyzed (not just one)
- [ ] Layers of meaning addressed (mood, tone, purpose — not just literal)
- [ ] Text evidence cited with paragraph numbers
- [ ] Analysis explains HOW (not just WHAT)
- [ ] Quality matches Level 3-4 ALD descriptors

### Day 6: CER/ACE Response Checkpoint
- [ ] CLAIM answers the prompt and names specific elements
- [ ] EVIDENCE includes 2+ quotes with paragraph numbers
- [ ] REASONING explains how elements ENHANCE meaning (uses layers vocabulary)
- [ ] Response connects to overall meaning/theme
- [ ] Quality matches Level 3-4 ALD descriptors
