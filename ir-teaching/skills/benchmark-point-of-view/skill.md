# ELA.10.R.1.3 - Point of View & Perspective (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.1.3**: Analyze and evaluate how the author's choices in point of view create effects such as suspense or humor.

## Benchmark Clarifications
This benchmark focuses on **literary texts** and how author's choices about WHO tells the story affect the reader's experience.

**Point of View Types:**
- **First person**: Narrator is a character ("I")
- **Second person**: Narrator addresses reader as "you" (rare)
- **Third person limited**: Narrator knows one character's thoughts
- **Third person omniscient**: Narrator knows all characters' thoughts
- **Third person objective**: Narrator only reports observable actions

**Perspective vs. Point of View:**
- **Point of view** = Grammatical (I, he/she, they)
- **Perspective** = Whose thoughts/feelings/experiences we access
- A story can be third person but limited to one character's perspective

**Effects to Analyze:**
- **Suspense**: What does narrator hide or reveal?
- **Humor**: What irony or limited understanding creates comedy?
- **Sympathy/empathy**: Whose perspective makes us care?
- **Bias**: How does narrator's knowledge/beliefs color the story?
- **Reliability**: Can we trust this narrator?

**Key Focus**: Not just identifying POV—analyzing how the author's CHOICE creates EFFECTS

## Prerequisite Skills & Common Gaps

**Students may struggle with:**
- Confusing point of view (grammatical) with perspective (whose viewpoint)
- Identifying third person limited vs. omniscient
- Understanding what narrator knows vs. doesn't know
- Analyzing "why this POV?" instead of just "what POV?"

**Built-in Remediation:**
- Use short excerpts showing same scene from different POVs
- Create anchor chart: POV types with examples
- Practice "what does narrator know/not know?" before analyzing effects
- Model "what if this were told by [different character]?" to show author's choice

## 6-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Point of View
Determine the grammatical point of view:
- **First person**: Uses "I," "we" (narrator is character)
- **Third person**: Uses "he," "she," "they" (narrator is outside)
- **Second person**: Uses "you" (rare, often in poetry or experimental fiction)

**Organizer prompt**: "What point of view is used?"

### Step 2: Identify the Perspective (Whose Story?)
Determine whose thoughts/feelings we access:
- Whose experiences are we following?
- Whose thoughts can we read?
- Whose perspective is privileged?
- Are we limited to one character or do we access multiple?

**Organizer prompt**: "From whose perspective is the story told?"

### Step 3: Analyze What Narrator Knows vs. Doesn't Know
Map the narrator's knowledge:
- What information does the narrator have access to?
- What is hidden from the narrator?
- What do other characters know that narrator doesn't?
- Are there information gaps?

**Organizer prompt**: "What does the narrator know? What is hidden?"

### Step 4: Determine Narrator Reliability
Evaluate if we can trust this narrator:
- **Reliable**: Trustworthy, objective, knowledgeable
- **Unreliable**: Biased, limited understanding, deceiving, mentally unstable

**Clues for unreliable narrator:**
- Contradictions in their account
- Other characters dispute their version
- Obvious bias or limited understanding
- Mental/emotional instability

**Organizer prompt**: "Is the narrator reliable? How do you know?"

### Step 5: Analyze the Effect of POV Choice
Determine WHY the author chose this POV:
- **Suspense**: Does limited knowledge create mystery?
- **Humor**: Does misunderstanding create comedy?
- **Sympathy**: Does intimate access create emotional connection?
- **Bias**: Does perspective shape how we judge characters/events?
- **Surprise**: Does limited POV enable plot twists?

**Organizer prompt**: "What effect does this POV create?"

### Step 6: Evaluate Alternative POV Choices
Consider what would change:
- How would the story change if told from [different character]'s POV?
- What would we gain or lose?
- Why did the author choose THIS perspective over others?

**Organizer prompt**: "How would the story change from a different POV?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Identification Questions
- From whose perspective is this told? How do you know?
- What point of view does the author use?
- Whose thoughts and feelings can we access?

### Knowledge Questions
- What does the narrator know or not know?
- How does this affect our understanding of events?
- What information is withheld from the narrator?

### Effect Questions
- How would the story change if told from [different character]'s perspective?
- What effect does the author create through this POV choice?
- How does the point of view create [suspense/humor/sympathy]?

### Evaluation Questions
- Is the narrator reliable? How do you know?
- Why did the author choose this particular POV?
- How does perspective affect our judgment of characters?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Evaluates complex POV choices and their sophisticated effects
- Analyzes unreliable narration with nuanced understanding
- Explains how perspective shapes theme or meaning
- Considers multiple alternative POVs and their implications

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Analyzes how POV creates specific effects (suspense, humor, etc.)
- Distinguishes between point of view and perspective
- Evaluates narrator reliability with evidence
- Explains author's reasoning for POV choice

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies point of view (first/third person)
- Recognizes whose perspective is presented
- Notes basic effects (we know/don't know certain information)
- Provides some textual support

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Confusion between POV and perspective
- Difficulty analyzing narrator's knowledge/limitations
- Minimal analysis of effects
- Struggles to evaluate alternative POVs

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Literary fiction with clear POV
- Texts where POV creates measurable effects
- Stories with unreliable or limited narrators
- Multiple excerpts for POV comparison
- Texts where perspective significantly affects interpretation

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Informational texts (use R.2.3 for author's perspective)
- Texts with generic or neutral omniscient narrator
- Plays/drama (no narrator)
- Poetry without clear narrative voice

## Common Point of View Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- perspective (viewpoint, whose story)
- narrator (voice telling the story)
- reliable (trustworthy)
- unreliable (biased, deceptive, or limited)
- omniscient (all-knowing)
- limited (restricted knowledge)
- objective (factual, no access to thoughts)
- bias (prejudice or preference)

**POV Terms:**
- first person (I/we)
- third person (he/she/they)
- point of view (grammatical narrator type)
- perspective (whose experiences we access)
- narrative voice (how story is told)

**Effect Terms:**
- suspense (tension, uncertainty)
- dramatic irony (reader knows more than character)
- sympathy (care about character)
- empathy (share character's feelings)

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For "The Tell-Tale Heart" by Edgar Allan Poe:**

| Point of View | Perspective (Whose?) | What Narrator Knows | What's Hidden | Effect Created |
|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------|----------------|
| **First person**: The narrator uses "I" throughout: "TRUE!—nervous—very, very dreadfully nervous I had been and am; but why will you say that I am mad?" | We experience the story entirely from the **murderer's perspective**. We are inside his head, accessing his thoughts, feelings, and distorted perceptions. We don't access the old man's thoughts or the police officers' suspicions. | The narrator knows (or thinks he knows): his own motivations ("It was not the old man...it was his Evil Eye"), his actions (the murder, concealment), his internal state (hearing the heartbeat). He believes he's perfectly sane and clever. | **Hidden from narrator**: How others perceive him (they likely see madness, not cleverness). Whether the heartbeat is real or imagined. The police officers' actual thoughts—he assumes they hear the heartbeat and are mocking him, but they may just be calm. | **Suspense and unreliability**: We don't know if we can trust this narrator who insists he's sane while describing insane actions. **Dramatic irony**: We recognize his madness even as he denies it. **Claustrophobia**: Being trapped in his deteriorating mind is terrifying. If told by police POV, it would be a simple crime story. Poe's choice makes this a psychological horror where the murderer is both perpetrator and victim of his own mind. |

## ESOL Scaffolds for Point of View

**Sentence frames:**
- "The story is told from [first/third] person point of view."
- "We experience the story from [character]'s perspective."
- "The narrator knows _______ but doesn't know _______."
- "This creates [suspense/humor/sympathy] because _______."
- "If told from [other character]'s POV, we would _______."

**Vocabulary support:**
- Create anchor chart with POV types and visual examples (comic strips work well)
- Color-code: I/we = blue (first person), he/she/they = green (third person)

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Read same scene from two different POVs (provide both versions)
- Discuss what changes before writing analysis

**Graphic organizers:**
- "What narrator knows" vs. "What narrator doesn't know" T-chart
- Venn diagram comparing two possible POVs

## Prerequisite Mini-Lessons (Built-In Remediation)

**Day 1 Focus**: What is POV? First vs. third person identification practice with short excerpts

**Day 2 Focus**: POV vs. Perspective - Use examples showing third person limited to different characters

**Day 3 Focus**: Narrator knowledge - Practice "What does narrator know? What is hidden?" with simple stories

**Day 4 Focus**: Reliable vs. unreliable narrators - Examples from familiar stories (movie villains who justify actions)

**Day 5+**: Apply to target text with gradual release

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Analyze and evaluate how author's POV choices create effects like suspense or humor

**Key Process**: Identify POV → Identify perspective → Analyze narrator knowledge → Determine reliability → Analyze effects → Evaluate alternative POVs

**Essential Question**: "From whose perspective is this told, and how does the author's choice create specific effects?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students identify POV (first/third person) but don't analyze the EFFECT of the author's choice

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "Why did the author choose THIS narrator?" and "What would change if told by [different character]?"

### Day 6: RACE Response Checkpoint

===================================
DAY 6 CHECKPOINT: Point of View RACE Response
===================================

**STUDENT SELF-CHECK:**
Before submitting, check your RACE response:
□ R - I restated the question in my answer
□ A - I answered the question with a clear claim about how POV affects the reader
□ C - I cited specific evidence showing POV techniques with paragraph numbers
□ E - I explained HOW the POV choice creates specific effects (not just what POV is used)

---

**TEACHER REVIEW:**

**✓ R - RESTATE**
□ Restated the prompt clearly
□ _Quality issue:_ Restatement is vague or unclear

**✓ A - ANSWER (POV Claim)**
□ Claim states how POV affects reader's experience or shapes meaning
□ Claim goes beyond just identifying POV type
□ _Quality issue:_ Only identifies POV type without stating effect (e.g., "The story uses first person" instead of "The first-person POV creates suspense by limiting what readers know")
□ _Quality issue:_ Confuses narrator with author (e.g., "The author tells the story" instead of "The narrator tells the story")
□ _Quality issue:_ Claim is unclear or generic

**✓ C - CITE (Evidence)**
□ 2+ pieces of evidence included
□ Evidence has paragraph numbers
□ Evidence shows POV techniques and their effects
□ _Quality issue:_ Evidence present but NOT RELEVANT to POV claim
□ _Quality issue:_ Evidence quoted but not explained
□ _Quality issue:_ Evidence only shows POV exists, not how it creates effects

**✓ E - EXPLAIN (POV Reasoning)**
□ Shows HOW POV choice creates specific effects (suspense, humor, sympathy, bias, etc.)
□ Explains what the narrator reveals or hides and why that matters
□ Connects POV to theme or purpose
□ _Quality issue:_ Only identifies POV without explaining effect on reader
□ _Quality issue:_ Doesn't show HOW POV shapes meaning or understanding
□ _Quality issue:_ Missing connection to theme/purpose
□ _Quality issue:_ Confuses what happens in plot with how POV affects reader's experience
