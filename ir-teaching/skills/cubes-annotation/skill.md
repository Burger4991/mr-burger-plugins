# CUBES Annotation Protocol — 10th Grade Intensive Reading

## Overview

CUBES is Mr. Burger's go-to annotation strategy for 10th grade IR students. It works for **two contexts**, each using a **3-step layered approach**:

1. **Annotating TEXT passages** — 3-step close reading (General Read → CUBES Annotation → Question-Driven Rereading)
2. **Annotating QUESTIONS** — 3-step test attack (CUBES the Question → Find Key Details in Text → STOP to Eliminate and Prove)

The same CUBES acronym adapts to both contexts. Students learn ONE framework and apply it everywhere.

---

## CUBES for PASSAGE Annotation (3-Step Layered Approach)

Passage annotation uses three steps. Step 1 is a general close reading with CUBES. Step 2 is a question-driven return to the text. Step 3 is synthesis — pulling it all together.

### STEP 1: First Read — CUBES Close Reading

On the first read-through, students use CUBES for general comprehension and close reading. This happens BEFORE students look at any questions.

| Letter | Action | What Students Do | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **C** | **Circle** key vocabulary | Circle unfamiliar words, Tier 2/3 academic words, and any words that seem important to meaning | Builds vocabulary awareness; flags words to use context clues on |
| **U** | **Underline** the claim/central idea/purpose | Underline sentences that state or strongly suggest the author's main point, argument, or purpose | Identifies the "so what" of the passage |
| **B** | **Box** text evidence | Draw a box around quotes, facts, statistics, examples, or details that support the claim/central idea | Prepares students for citing evidence in responses |
| **E** | **Evaluate** author's craft | Write margin notes about HOW the author achieves their purpose (devices, structure, appeals, figurative language) | Moves beyond WHAT to HOW — the skill tested on BEST assessments |
| **S** | **Summarize** each section | At the end of each section or paragraph, write a 1-sentence summary or reaction in the margin | Checks comprehension; prevents passive reading |

#### Step 1 Symbols (Quick Reference)

```
C = ○ Circle vocabulary words
U = ___ Underline claim/central idea/purpose
B = [ ] Box key evidence
E = ✎ Write margin notes on author's craft
S = ★ Summarize each section (1 sentence)
```

### STEP 2: Question-Driven Rereading — Return to the Text

After reading the questions (using CUBES for Questions — see below), students return to the passage with specific targets. This is the second layer.

| Checklist Item | What Students Do | Why It Matters |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Identify target paragraphs** | Locate which paragraphs the question refers to (using boxed references from question annotation) | Narrows the search area; prevents aimless rereading |
| **Underline or highlight relevant details** | Add a second layer of underlining/highlighting focused on what the question asks | Builds the habit of rereading with purpose |
| **Write margin notes connecting details to the question** | Annotate HOW a detail connects to what the question is asking | Bridges the gap between finding evidence and using it |
| **Find specific evidence that could answer the question** | Locate and mark the exact quote, detail, or example that answers the question | Prepares students for selecting or writing evidence-based answers |
| **Note details that need analysis (not just literal facts)** | Flag details that require interpretation, inference, or evaluation — not just surface-level facts | Trains analytical thinking; aligns to BEST assessment rigor |

#### Step 2 Symbols (Quick Reference)

```
Target paragraphs = ¶ Mark paragraph numbers that match the question
Relevant details  = HL Highlight or double-underline
Margin connections = → Write arrows connecting details to question focus
Evidence          = ✦ Star the best evidence for each question
Analysis needed   = ? Flag details requiring deeper thinking
```

### STEP 3: Synthesis — Pull It Together

After both annotation passes, students pause to synthesize before answering.

| Checklist Item | What Students Do | Why It Matters |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Review CUBES annotations from Step 1** | Scan circled vocabulary, underlined central idea, boxed evidence, and margin notes | Reconnects general comprehension to specific question demands |
| **Review targeted annotations from Step 2** | Check highlighted details and margin connections for each question | Ensures evidence is question-specific, not generic |
| **Confirm evidence is sufficient** | Ask: "Do I have enough text evidence to answer this question fully?" | Prevents thin or unsupported responses |
| **Identify the analytical move required** | Ask: "Am I being asked to identify, analyze, compare, or evaluate?" | Ensures the response matches the question's cognitive demand |

## CUBES for QUESTION Annotation (3-Step Approach)

Question annotation uses three sequential steps: CUBES to break down the question, return to the text (Step 2 of Passage Annotation above), and STOP to eliminate and select.

### STEP 1: CUBES the Question

| Letter | Action | What Students Do | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **C** | **Circle** key words | Circle important words in the question stem — names, concepts, qualifiers (most, best, primarily) | Focuses attention on what the question actually asks |
| **U** | **Underline** what the question is asking | Underline the core demand — what you need to find, explain, or analyze | Prevents answering a different question than the one asked |
| **B** | **Box** paragraph references or text clues | Box any paragraph numbers, line numbers, excerpt references, or signal phrases that point to the text | Tells you WHERE to look; connects to Step 2 rereading |
| **E** | **Evaluate** what type of question it is | Determine the question type: literal recall, inference, analysis, evaluation, comparison | Activates the right thinking strategy for the question type |
| **S** | **STOP** — use the STOP strategy to eliminate and prove | Move to the STOP strategy (Step 3) to systematically evaluate answer choices | Prevents impulsive answering; builds a proving habit |

#### Step 1 Symbols (Quick Reference)

```
C = ○ Circle key words in the question
U = ___ Underline what the question is asking
B = [ ] Box paragraph references or text clues
E = ? Evaluate the question type (write it in margin)
S = STOP → Move to STOP strategy
```

### STEP 2: Return to the Text — Find Key Details

*(This is the same as Step 2 of Passage Annotation above. After CUBES-ing the question, students go back to the passage with targeted annotation.)*

Checklist:
- ☐ Identified which paragraphs the question refers to
- ☐ Underlined or highlighted relevant details in the text
- ☐ Wrote margin notes connecting details to the question
- ☐ Found specific evidence that could answer the question
- ☐ Noted details that need analysis (not just literal facts)

### STEP 3: Eliminate Answer Choices — STOP Strategy

After annotating both the question and the text, students use STOP to evaluate each answer choice. **For the full STOP teaching guide, anchor charts, practice templates, and ESOL modifications, see `stop-strategy`.**

| Letter | Action | What Students Do | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **S** | **Silly** | Identify the obviously wrong choice — off-topic, absurd, or contradicted by the text | Quick elimination; builds confidence |
| **T** | **Tricky** | Identify the partially correct choice — contains some truth but is incomplete, overgeneralized, or distorts the text | Targets the most common trap answer on BEST assessments |
| **O** | **Opposite** | Identify the choice that reverses the correct answer — says the opposite of what the text supports | Catches misreads and reversal traps |
| **P** | **Proven** | Select the choice supported by text evidence — the one you can PROVE with a specific quote or detail | Forces evidence-based selection; eliminates guessing |

#### Step 3 Requirements

- ☐ Labeled EACH answer choice (S, T, O, or P) before selecting a final answer
- ☐ Wrote a one-sentence justification for the Proven response

#### STOP Symbols (Quick Reference)

```
S = ✗ Silly — cross it out (obviously wrong)
T = ~ Tricky — wavy line (partially correct, watch out)
O = ⟲ Opposite — reversal symbol (says the reverse)
P = ✓ Proven — checkmark (supported by text evidence)
```

## How the Two Strategies Connect

The passage and question strategies are designed to work together in a loop:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PASSAGE ANNOTATION                                  │
│                                                      │
│  Step 1: First Read — CUBES Close Reading             │
│          (general comprehension, before questions)    │
│                              │                       │
│                              ▼                       │
│  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐    │
│  │  QUESTION ANNOTATION                         │    │
│  │                                              │    │
│  │  Step 1: CUBES the Question                  │    │
│  │          (break down the question)            │    │
│  └──────────────────┬───────────────────────────┘    │
│                     │                                │
│                     ▼                                │
│  Step 2: Return to Text — Find Key Details            │
│          (question-driven rereading)                  │
│                              │                       │
│                              ▼                       │
│  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐    │
│  │  Step 3: STOP Strategy                       │    │
│  │          (eliminate and prove)                │    │
│  └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘    │
│                                                      │
│  Step 3 (Passage): Synthesis — Pull It Together       │
│          (confirm evidence, identify analytical move) │
│          (used for constructed responses / ACE-CER)   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Key insight**: Step 2 of Passage Annotation and Step 2 of Question Annotation are the **same step**. Students return to the text with question-driven targets. This is where the two strategies intersect.

## Benchmark-Specific CUBES Adaptations

The core CUBES strategy stays the same, but the **"U" and "E" steps adapt** based on which benchmark is the unit focus.

### Passage Annotation Adaptations (Step 1 Focus)

These adaptations customize what students focus on during their first-read CUBES annotation.

#### R.1.1 — Literary Elements (Analyze how key elements contribute)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle character names, setting details, key plot events |
| **U** | Underline how elements CONTRIBUTE to meaning/style |
| **B** | Box dialogue, descriptions, or actions that reveal character/conflict |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW does this element develop the story/style? |
| **S** | Summarize: What is the author doing with this element? |

#### R.1.2 — Theme (Analyze and compare universal themes)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle repeated ideas, symbols, character realizations |
| **U** | Underline passages that reveal the theme (not topic — the MESSAGE) |
| **B** | Box evidence that supports the theme statement |
| **E** | Margin note: What UNIVERSAL message does the author develop here? |
| **S** | Summarize the theme as a complete sentence (not one word) |

#### R.1.3 — Coming of Age / Conflicting Perspectives

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle moments of growth, realization, conflict between perspectives |
| **U** | Underline the character's transformation or the conflicting viewpoints |
| **B** | Box before/after evidence showing change or opposing views |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW does the author show growth or represent conflict? |
| **S** | Summarize: What changed? What perspectives conflict? |

#### R.1.4 — Poetry (Layers of meaning / Ambiguity)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle figurative language, imagery, ambiguous words/phrases |
| **U** | Underline lines with multiple possible meanings |
| **B** | Box contrasts, paradoxes, or shifts in the poem |
| **E** | Margin note: What are the possible meanings? How do they coexist? |
| **S** | Summarize: What does this poem say on the surface vs. deeper? |

#### R.2.1 — Text Structure & Features (Analyze impact)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle signal words (because, however, as a result, similarly) |
| **U** | Underline the author's purpose/central idea |
| **B** | Box headings, bold terms, graphs, captions |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW does this structure/feature support meaning? |
| **S** | Summarize: Name the structure + explain WHY the author chose it |

#### R.2.2 — Central Idea (Historical American speeches/essays)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle repeated concepts, key terms the author returns to |
| **U** | Underline the central idea statement (explicit or implicit) |
| **B** | Box supporting details, examples, evidence for the central idea |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW is this idea developed and supported? |
| **S** | Summarize central idea as a complete sentence |

#### R.2.3 — Purpose (Historical American speeches/essays)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle rhetorical devices, appeals (ethos/pathos/logos), figurative language |
| **U** | Underline the author's purpose (explicit or implicit) |
| **B** | Box specific authorial choices with paragraph numbers |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW does this choice establish/achieve the purpose? |
| **S** | Summarize: State purpose + name 2 choices that achieve it |

#### R.2.4 — Argument (Compare opposing arguments)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle claims, counterclaims, evidence types |
| **U** | Underline each author's main claim |
| **B** | Box shared evidence that both authors reference |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW does each author use this evidence differently? |
| **S** | Summarize: Author 1 claims ___ using ___; Author 2 claims ___ using ___ |

#### R.3.1 — Figurative Language & Mood (Analyze how fig lang creates mood)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle all figurative language (metaphor, simile, personification, etc.) |
| **U** | Underline the mood the passage creates (use precise mood words) |
| **B** | Box the most powerful figurative language examples with para numbers |
| **E** | Margin note: HOW does this device create or reinforce the mood? |
| **S** | Summarize: The ___ (device) creates a ___ mood by ___ |

#### R.3.4 — Rhetoric (Analyze use of rhetoric)

| Step | Passage Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **C** | Circle rhetorical devices + appeals (ethos/pathos/logos) |
| **U** | Underline the author's purpose and audience |
| **B** | Box specific rhetoric examples with paragraph numbers |
| **E** | Margin note: Is this rhetoric APPROPRIATE for the audience? Is it EFFECTIVE? |
| **S** | Summarize: The author uses ___ to ___ (purpose) for ___ (audience) |

### Question Annotation Adaptations (CUBES Step 1 + STOP Step 3)

These adaptations customize what students focus on when breaking down questions and evaluating answers.

#### R.1.1 — Literary Elements

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word (analyze, explain) |
| **U** | Underline which element the question asks about |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking about WHAT the element is or HOW it contributes? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that only IDENTIFY elements without explaining contribution |

#### R.1.2 — Theme

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline which theme or comparison the question targets |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking for theme (message) or topic (subject)? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that state topic instead of theme |

#### R.1.3 — Coming of Age / Conflicting Perspectives

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline whether question asks about coming-of-age OR conflicting perspectives |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking about events or about transformation/conflict? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that describe events without connecting to growth/conflict |

#### R.1.4 — Poetry

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline which layer or ambiguity the question targets |
| **B** | Box line/stanza references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking about one meaning or multiple layers? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that offer only ONE meaning when the question asks about layers |

#### R.2.1 — Text Structure & Features

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline whether question asks about STRUCTURE or FEATURES |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking what the feature IS or what it DOES (impact)? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices about what the feature IS vs. what it DOES |

#### R.2.2 — Central Idea

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline which aspect of central idea the question targets |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking for the central idea or a supporting detail? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that are too broad, too narrow, or off-topic |

#### R.2.3 — Purpose

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline whether question asks about purpose, authorial choices, or both |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking about WHAT the purpose is or HOW choices achieve it? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that identify purpose without analyzing HOW |

#### R.2.4 — Argument

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline which task demand: compare development, same info/different ends, or evaluate validity |
| **B** | Box paragraph references from BOTH passages |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking you to compare or just describe one side? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that describe WHAT authors argue vs. HOW they develop arguments |

#### R.3.1 — Figurative Language & Mood

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline whether question asks about HOW fig lang creates mood |
| **B** | Box paragraph/line references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking you to identify a device or explain its mood effect? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that identify device type instead of explaining mood creation |

#### R.3.4 — Rhetoric

| Step | Question Focus |
|------|---------------|
| **C** | Circle command word |
| **U** | Underline whether question asks about appropriateness or effectiveness |
| **B** | Box paragraph references |
| **E** | Evaluate: Is this asking you to identify rhetoric or analyze its use? |
| **STOP** | Eliminate choices that identify device type instead of analyzing use |

## Teaching CUBES (Days 1-2 of Any Unit)

### Day 1: Introduce CUBES for Passages (3-Step Model)

1. **Display the CUBES poster** (anchor chart — passage version)
2. **Explain the 3-step approach**: "We read TWICE. First, we CUBES the passage for general understanding. Later, we come back with specific questions."
3. **I Do**: Teacher reads paragraph 1 aloud, modeling each CUBES step on the document camera
4. **We Do**: Class annotates paragraph 2 together using CUBES
5. **Check**: Teacher circulates to verify annotations match the model
6. **Transition**: "Tomorrow you'll learn how to CUBES the questions and come BACK to the text."

### Day 2: Introduce CUBES for Questions + STOP Strategy

1. **Display the Question CUBES poster** AND the **STOP poster** (two anchor charts)
2. **I Do — CUBES**: Teacher shows a sample MC question, models each CUBES step
3. **I Do — Return to Text**: Teacher models going back to the passage to find key details
4. **I Do — STOP**: Teacher models labeling each answer choice as S, T, O, or P
5. **We Do**: Class works through a second question together (all 3 steps)
6. **You Do**: Students practice on 2-3 questions independently
7. **Debrief**: "Show me your STOP labels. What was the Tricky choice? What made the Proven choice provable?"

### Ongoing Reinforcement (Days 3-6)

- **Verbal prompt**: "Did you CUBES the passage before looking at the questions?" (said during circulation)
- **Verbal prompt**: "Show me your STOP labels — where's the Tricky one?" (said during independent work)
- **Packet checkpoint**: Annotation boxes include micro-step checkboxes tied to CUBES and STOP
- **Feedback**: Reference specific steps when giving feedback ("You found the Proven answer, but you didn't label the Tricky one — that's what usually catches students on the real test")

## ESOL Connection

CUBES is especially powerful for ESOL students because:
- **Visual**: Each step has a distinct symbol (○, ___, [ ], ✎/✗, ★/✓)
- **Kinesthetic**: Students physically mark the text
- **Structured**: Provides a repeatable process that reduces cognitive load
- **Vocabulary-first**: The "C" step ensures unknown words are flagged before comprehension breaks down
- **Layered**: The 3-step approach gives multiple passes, reducing the pressure to "get it" on the first read

**ESOL adaptation**: For newcomers or Level 1-2 ESOL students, simplify:
- **Passage**: Start with **CUB** only (Circle, Underline, Box). Add E and S once the first three steps are automatic.
- **Questions**: Start with **CUB** only. Add E (evaluate question type) and S (STOP strategy) once students are comfortable breaking down questions.
- **STOP**: Introduce with just **S and P** first (Silly and Proven). Add T and O once students can consistently find the obvious wrong and the provable right.
