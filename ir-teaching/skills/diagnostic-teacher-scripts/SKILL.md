# Diagnostic Teacher Scripts with Gap Identification & Remediation

## PURPOSE

These scripts transform traditional "I Do/We Do/You Do" into **diagnostic teaching moments** where teachers:
1. **Anticipate** common student misconceptions
2. **Check for understanding** at critical junctures
3. **Identify gaps** in real-time
4. **Remediate on the spot** with targeted scaffolds

**Core Principle:** Every teaching script includes "If students struggle with X, then do Y" guidance.

---

## SCRIPT STRUCTURE

Each gradual release script includes:

### I Do (Teacher Modeling) - 8-12 minutes
- **Model** the thinking process (think-aloud)
- **Highlight** potential confusion points
- **Name** the strategy/skill being used
- **Predict** where students might struggle

### We Do (Guided Practice) - 8-12 minutes
- **Pose** a collaborative task
- **Check for understanding** (CFU) - specific questions to ask
- **Listen for** common errors/gaps
- **Remediate** if gaps detected (built-in fix-up strategies)

### You Do w/ Partner (Structured Practice) - 5-8 minutes
- **Preview** task for pairs
- **Circulate** with monitoring checklist (what to look/listen for)
- **Intervene** if patterns emerge (whole-class pause vs. 1:1)

### You Do (Independent Preview) - 2-3 minutes
- **Connect** to Independent rotation work
- **Set expectations** for quality
- **Reminder** of resources available

## I DO SCRIPT TEMPLATE (THEME EXAMPLE)

### Context
- **Benchmark:** ELA.10.R.1.2 (Theme)
- **Skill:** Identifying universal theme vs topic
- **Text:** "The Gift of the Magi" by O. Henry
- **Time:** 8-10 minutes

### Script

**[Display completed I Do row of organizer]**

**Teacher:** "I'm going to show you how to identify a universal theme - one that applies to anyone, anywhere, at any time. Watch how I think through this.

**[PREDICTION OF PITFALL #1: Students confuse theme with topic]**

First, I need to remember: **theme is NOT the same as topic**. Let me explain the difference.
- Topic = what the story is about (love, sacrifice, Christmas)
- Theme = the message or lesson about that topic (Love requires sacrifice)

So if I just say 'This story is about love,' that's a TOPIC, not a theme. I need to say something about love - a message that could apply to anyone.

**[MODELING]**

Let me look at the ending of 'Gift of the Magi.' [Read aloud paragraph 10-11]

'The magi brought valuable gifts, but Della and Jim gave the greatest gifts of all - they sacrificed what they valued most for each other.'

Okay, so what's happening here? Both characters gave up something precious. Della cut her hair to buy Jim a chain. Jim sold his watch to buy Della combs.

**[THINK-ALOUD: Naming the thinking process]**

I'm going to use a sentence frame to help me: **'The text shows that...'**

The text shows that... true love means putting someone else's happiness before your own.

Let me check: Is this UNIVERSAL? Could this apply to anyone, anywhere, at any time? Yes! This isn't just about Jim and Della - this is a message about love in general.

**[PREDICTION OF PITFALL #2: Students make theme too specific]**

I also need to make sure I'm not too specific. If I said 'The text shows that married couples should buy each other gifts,' that's too narrow. My theme needs to be broader: **Love requires sacrifice.**

**[CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING - Built into I Do]**

Turn to your partner for 10 seconds: What's the difference between topic and theme? [Wait, listen]

Good! Topic = what it's about. Theme = message about the topic.

**[COMPLETE I DO ROW]**

So my I Do row says:
- Evidence: Jim sold his watch, Della cut her hair (¶10-11)
- What it shows about theme: Both characters sacrificed what they valued most
- Theme statement: True love requires personal sacrifice

That's a complete I Do row. Notice I:
1. Found evidence (specific paragraphs)
2. Explained what it shows
3. Stated a UNIVERSAL theme (not topic, not too specific)

Now let's try one together."

---

### Gap Identification Points (Teacher Notes)

**STOP and check if students:**
- Say topic instead of theme → **Remediate:** "Is that universal or specific to this story?"
- Make theme too specific → **Remediate:** "Could this apply to anyone, or just these characters?"
- Can't find evidence → **Remediate:** "Look at the ending - what did characters do?"

**Listen for these phrases (indicate confusion):**
- "The theme is Christmas" (topic, not theme)
- "Jim and Della love each other" (too specific, not universal)
- "The story is about gifts" (topic again)

## WE DO SCRIPT TEMPLATE (RHETORIC EXAMPLE)

### Context
- **Benchmark:** ELA.10.R.3.4 (Rhetoric)
- **Skill:** Analyzing rhetorical device + effect
- **Text:** "I Have a Dream" speech by MLK
- **Time:** 10 minutes

### Script

**[Display partially complete We Do row]**

**Teacher:** "Now we're going to analyze rhetorical devices together. I've started the We Do row - let's finish it as a class.

**[PREDICTION OF PITFALL #1: Students identify device but can't explain effect]**

Remember: It's not enough to just NAME the device (repetition, parallelism, etc.). We have to explain the EFFECT - what it makes the audience think, feel, or do.

**[COLLABORATIVE TASK]**

Turn to paragraph 12 in the speech. Read it with your partner. [30 seconds]

Now, what do you notice about how Dr. King structures his sentences? What pattern do you see?

**[CFU #1: Listen for device identification]**

[Call on student] 'He repeats 'I have a dream' over and over.'

**GAP CHECK:** If students just say "repetition" without specifics:
**REMEDIATE:** "Good start! How many times? Where in the paragraph? Let's count together."

Exactly! He uses ANAPHORA - repetition at the beginning of clauses. Let's count: 'I have a dream' appears 8 times.

**[TRANSITION TO EFFECT ANALYSIS]**

Now here's the harder question: WHY does he repeat this phrase 8 times? What effect does it create?

**[CFU #2: Listen for effect analysis]**

Think-Pair-Share: Talk to your partner for 1 minute. What's the effect of all this repetition?

**[CIRCULATE: Listen for common responses]**

**GAP CHECK:** If students say "It sounds good" or "It's memorable":
**REMEDIATE:** "Yes, but dig deeper. What EMOTION does it create? What does he want the audience to DO?"

[Call on pair] 'It makes the audience feel hopeful. It emphasizes his vision for the future.'

**[SCAFFOLDING LANGUAGE]**

Perfect! Let's use this sentence frame: **'This device makes the audience feel _____ because _____.'**

So we'd write: 'The repetition of 'I have a dream' makes the audience feel hopeful and inspired because it emphasizes the possibility of change and creates a sense of urgency.'

**[GAP CHECK #3: Are we connecting device to PURPOSE?]**

One more question: How does this connect to Dr. King's PURPOSE? Why use THIS device for THIS speech?

**COMMON ERROR:** Students might say "To make it sound good."

**REMEDIATE:** "Okay, but he's trying to PERSUADE people. How does repetition help persuade?"

[Student response] 'It makes people remember his dream and want to make it come true.'

YES! That's the connection to purpose: **Repetition reinforces the central message and motivates action.**

**[COMPLETE WE DO ROW]**

Let's write that in our We Do row:
- Device: Anaphora (repetition of 'I have a dream')
- Quote: 'I have a dream that one day...' (¶12, repeated 8x)
- Effect: Creates hope and urgency; emphasizes vision for future
- Purpose: Persuades audience to believe in and work toward change

Great work! Now you're ready to try one with a partner."

---

### Gap Identification Points (Teacher Notes)

**STOP and check if students:**
- Can't name the device → **Remediate:** Provide anchor chart, narrow options ("Is this repetition or parallelism?")
- Name device but can't explain effect → **Remediate:** Use sentence frame ("This makes the audience feel _____")
- Explain effect but don't connect to purpose → **Remediate:** "How does this help the speaker persuade?"

**Common Errors to Listen For:**
- "It sounds nice" (surface-level, no analysis)
- "It makes ME feel sad" (personal response, not audience analysis)
- Confusing device names (repetition vs anaphora vs parallelism)

**Quick Fixes:**
- Provide anchor chart with device examples
- Model with simpler example first
- Use "This makes the audience feel/think/do" frame
- Ask: "How does this help achieve the speaker's purpose?"

## YOU DO W/ PARTNER SCRIPT TEMPLATE (ARGUMENT EXAMPLE)

### Context
- **Benchmark:** ELA.10.R.2.4 (Argument)
- **Skill:** Identifying claim, evidence, and reasoning
- **Text:** Editorial on school uniforms
- **Time:** 6-8 minutes

### Script

**[Display You Do w/ Partner row with sentence frames]**

**Teacher:** "Now you and your partner are going to analyze the author's argument together. You'll have 6 minutes.

**[SET UP TASK]**

Your job:
1. Find the author's CLAIM (main argument)
2. Find one piece of EVIDENCE
3. Explain the REASONING (how evidence supports claim)

I've given you sentence frames to help:
- Claim: 'The author argues that _____.'
- Evidence: 'The author supports this by stating _____ (¶___).'
- Reasoning: 'This evidence shows _____ because _____.'

**[PREDICTION OF PITFALLS]**

Watch out for these traps:
- **Trap #1:** Confusing claim with evidence. The claim is the OPINION. The evidence is the FACTS that support it.
- **Trap #2:** Stopping at evidence without explaining reasoning. You must explain HOW the evidence proves the claim.

**[MONITORING CHECKLIST - What to listen/look for]**

As you work, I'll be walking around listening for:
✓ Are you finding the main claim (not a supporting detail)?
✓ Are you choosing strong evidence (facts, statistics, expert quotes)?
✓ Are you explaining reasoning (not just restating evidence)?

**[INTERVENTION POINTS - Built into circulating]**

**IF I hear:** 'The claim is that uniforms are expensive.'
**THEN I say:** 'Wait - is that the main argument, or is that a reason against uniforms? What's the author's overall position?'

**IF I hear:** Students copying evidence but no reasoning
**THEN I say:** 'Good evidence! Now tell me: HOW does this fact support the claim? What does it prove?'

**IF I see:** Partners stuck, not writing anything
**THEN I say:** 'Start with the claim. What is the author trying to convince you of? Find the sentence where they state their position.'

**[WHOLE-CLASS PAUSE if needed - Use if >50% stuck]**

[After 3 min, if many pairs struggle]

'Okay everyone, pause for a moment. I'm noticing some confusion. Let me clarify:

The CLAIM is in paragraph 2: 'School uniforms should be required because they reduce bullying and improve focus.'

That's the author's main argument. Everything else is EVIDENCE to support that claim.

Now, back to your partners - find one piece of evidence that supports this claim.'

**[CLOSING - Set quality expectations]**

You have 2 minutes left. Make sure your reasoning is COMPLETE. Don't just say 'This supports the claim.' Explain WHY or HOW.

**[QUICK SHARE-OUT]**

Who wants to share their reasoning sentence? [Call on 1-2 pairs]

'This evidence shows that uniforms reduce distractions because students focus on learning instead of clothing.'

Perfect! That's complete reasoning - you explained HOW the evidence connects to the claim.

In Independent rotation, you'll finish the rest of the organizer on your own. Use these same steps."

---

### Gap Identification Points (Teacher Notes)

**Common Partner Work Gaps:**
1. **One student dominates** → Intervene: "Partner A, what's your idea? Partner B, do you agree?"
2. **Confusing claim with evidence** → Pause whole class, clarify
3. **Weak reasoning** → Push: "How does that fact prove the claim? Be specific."
4. **Off-task** → Proximity, refocus: "What's your claim?"

**Monitoring Checklist (What to Look/Listen For):**
- [ ] Both partners contributing?
- [ ] Claim is main argument (not detail)?
- [ ] Evidence comes from text (not opinion)?
- [ ] Reasoning explains HOW (not just restates evidence)?
- [ ] Using sentence frames or writing fluently?

**Intervention Decision Tree:**
- **1-2 pairs stuck** → Individual intervention (whisper support)
- **3-5 pairs stuck** → Small group pull-aside (quick re-teach)
- **50%+ stuck** → Whole-class pause (re-model skill)

## FLEXIBLE PACING GUIDANCE

### Pacing Philosophy
**Don't force rigid timing.** Instead, use **checkpoints and flex time**.

**Guideline:** "I Do takes 8-12 min, but watch for understanding, not the clock."

### Pacing with Built-In Flex

**I Do (8-12 min):**
- **Minimum** (8 min): One complete example, quick CFU
- **Standard** (10 min): One example, 2 CFUs, brief Q&A
- **Extended** (12 min): Example + address major confusion

**Decision point:** "If students look confused during CFU, add 2 min for clarification."

**We Do (8-12 min):**
- **Minimum** (8 min): One collaborative example, quick share
- **Standard** (10 min): Collaborative example, multiple CFUs, share-out
- **Extended** (12 min): Example + remediation if gaps detected

**Decision point:** "If We Do reveals major gaps, pause and re-model before moving to You Do."

**You Do w/ Partner (5-8 min):**
- **Minimum** (5 min): Quick partner practice, 1-2 min check
- **Standard** (6 min): Partner work with circulating, share-out
- **Extended** (8 min): Partner work + intervention + whole-class debrief

**You Do Preview (2-3 min):**
- Can be shortened if running over
- Can be skipped if students are ready

### Time-Saving Strategies

**If running behind:**
1. **Shorten You Do preview** - students will get it in Independent
2. **Skip second CFU** in We Do - prioritize first check
3. **Reduce share-outs** - call on 1 pair instead of 3

**If ahead of schedule:**
1. **Add second example** in I Do
2. **Extend We Do** for deeper practice
3. **Add metacognitive reflection** - "What strategy helped you?"

### I Do Slide Requirements

**Slide 1: Completed Example**
- Show fully complete I Do row
- Include all components (evidence, analysis, citation)
- Use color-coding to highlight parts (evidence = yellow, reasoning = green)

**Slide 2: Think-Aloud Prompts (in notes section for teacher)**
- "Notice how I... [strategy]"
- "I'm checking: Is this universal/text-based/specific enough?"

### We Do Slide Requirements

**Slide 1: Partially Complete Example**
- Some boxes filled (model), some blank (class fills)
- Guiding questions visible: "What device is used? What's the effect?"

**Slide 2: Sentence Frames**
- Display frames for student use
- "This shows _____ because _____"

**Slide 3: Gap Check Reminders**
- "Remember: Theme ≠ Topic"

- "Ask yourself: Did we find the claim? Did we explain reasoning?"

## BENCHMARK-SPECIFIC PITFALLS & REMEDIATIONS

### Theme (ELA.10.R.1.2)

**Common Pitfalls:**
1. **Confusing theme with topic**
   - **Listen for:** "The theme is love" or "The theme is war"
   - **Remediate:** "That's the TOPIC. What MESSAGE does the text show about love/war?"

2. **Making theme too specific**
   - **Listen for:** "Della should have kept her hair"
   - **Remediate:** "Is that universal? Could this apply to anyone, or just Della?"

3. **Stating plot summary instead of theme**
   - **Listen for:** "Jim and Della gave each other gifts"
   - **Remediate:** "That's WHAT happened. What's the LESSON or message?"

**Quick Fix Script:**
```
"Let's test if this is a theme. Ask yourself 3 questions:
1. Is it universal? (Applies to anyone, anywhere, anytime?)
2. Is it a message or lesson? (Not just topic or plot?)
3. Can I prove it with text evidence?

If yes to all 3, you have a theme!"
```

### Rhetoric (ELA.10.R.3.4)

**Common Pitfalls:**
1. **Identifying device but not explaining effect**
   - **Listen for:** "The speaker uses repetition"
   - **Remediate:** "Good! Now tell me: What effect does that repetition create?"

2. **Personal response instead of audience analysis**
   - **Listen for:** "It makes ME feel sad"
   - **Remediate:** "Remember, we're analyzing the audience, not just you. What does the speaker WANT the audience to feel?"

3. **Confusing device names**
   - **Listen for:** "Is this a metaphor or parallelism?"
   - **Remediate:** Provide anchor chart, use process of elimination

**Quick Fix Script:**
```
"For every rhetorical device, answer 3 questions:
1. WHAT is the device? (Name it: repetition, parallelism, etc.)
2. WHAT is the effect? (Makes audience feel/think/do...)
3. WHY use it? (How does it help achieve purpose?)"
```

### Argument (ELA.10.R.2.4)

**Common Pitfalls:**
1. **Confusing claim with evidence**
   - **Listen for:** "The claim is that 75% of students prefer uniforms"
   - **Remediate:** "That's EVIDENCE (a fact). The claim is the OPINION the author is arguing."

2. **Weak or missing reasoning**
   - **Listen for:** "This supports the claim" (no explanation)
   - **Remediate:** "HOW does it support? Explain the connection."

3. **Not addressing counterclaim**
   - **Listen for:** Ignoring paragraph with opposing view
   - **Remediate:** "The author also discusses the OTHER side. How do they address it?"

**Quick Fix Script:**
```
"Argument = C.E.R.
- Claim: Opinion/position (debatable)
- Evidence: Facts/stats/quotes (provable)
- Reasoning: How evidence proves claim (your explanation)

Check: Is your claim an OPINION? Is your evidence a FACT? Did you explain HOW they connect?"
```

## INTEGRATION WITH LESSON PLANS

### When Writing I Do Script:
1. Start with completed organizer row
2. Write think-aloud that predicts 2-3 pitfalls
3. Include 1-2 CFUs (embedded checks)
4. End with summary of process

### When Writing We Do Script:
1. Start with collaborative task (partially complete row)
2. Include 2-3 CFU checkpoints
3. For each CFU, add "If students say X, remediate with Y"
4. Build in whole-class pause option if major gaps detected

### When Writing You Do w/ Partner Script:
1. Provide sentence frames/scaffolds
2. Create monitoring checklist (what to look/listen for)
3. Include intervention scripts (individual, small group, whole-class)
4. Set quality expectations before release
