## R.1.1 Literary Elements

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.1.1 — Explain how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a literary text.
**10th Grade:** ELA.10.R.1.1 — Analyze how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a literary text.

### Clarifications
- Key elements: setting, plot, characterization, conflict, point of view, theme, and tone.
- Layers of meaning model (I.A. Richards): Layer 1) literal level, Layer 2) mood, Layer 3) tone, Layer 4) author's purpose.
- Style = diction, syntax, grammar, use of figurative language. Style creates the author's voice.

### Assessment Limits
- Items may ask how key elements contribute to meaning and/or style.
- Layers of meaning should be explicitly named (don't assume student knows a particular model).
- Items about theme development → align to R.1.2.
- Items about figurative language + mood → align to R.3.1.
- Focus on DEVELOPMENT of layers, not just identification.

### Achievement Level Descriptors
| Level 2 | Level 3 | Level 4 | Level 5 |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| describes how simplistic key elements enhance or add a single layer of meaning and/or style in a grade-level low-to-mid complexity literary text | explains/analyzes how simplistic key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a grade-level low-to-mid complexity literary text | explains/analyzes how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a grade-level mid-to-high complexity literary text | explains/analyzes thoroughly how complex key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a grade-level high complexity literary text |

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Analyze how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning**
- Select the sentence from paragraph ____ that shows that ____.
- How does the plot in paragraph ____ develop the theme of ____?
- How does the setting affect the plot?
- How does the last paragraph reveal ____?
- How does paragraph ____ create mood?
- What is the purpose of the monologue (dialogue/aside/soliloquy) in the scene (paragraph/excerpt)?

**Task Demand: Analyze how key elements enhance or add to components of style**
- How does the use of ____ (key element) enhance the author's style?
- How does the author develop the ____ style in the passage?
- This question has two parts. First, answer Part A. Then, answer Part B.
  Part A: How does the author's style impact the overall meaning of the text?
  Part B: How does ____ (key element) enhance that style?

## R.1.2 Theme

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.1.2 — Analyze universal themes and their development throughout a literary text.
**10th Grade:** ELA.10.R.1.2 — Analyze and compare universal themes and their development throughout a literary text.

### Clarifications
- A universal theme applies to anyone, anywhere, regardless of cultural differences.
- Examples: individual's struggle toward understanding/awareness/spiritual enlightenment; confrontation with nature; tension between ideal and real; conflict between human beings and technology; impact of past on present; inevitability of fate; struggle for equality; loss of innocence.

### Assessment Limits
- Items may ask how broad universal themes apply to the specifics of the text.
- 10th grade: Items should compare or analyze universal themes in a single text.
- Items should NOT ask the student only to identify themes — must also analyze HOW those themes are developed.
- Items should NOT ask the student to define what the theme is.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Analyze the development of universal theme(s) in a text**
- How does the author develop the theme of ____?
- What universal theme is developed through ____ (literary element)?
- Select the stanza that develops the universal theme of ____?
- Select two lines from the poem that develop the theme?
- Part A/B: What is the universal theme in the passage/passage ____? / How is the theme developed?
- Part A/B: What is the universal theme in the passage/passage ____? / Which excerpt supports your answer to Part A?
- Part A/B: How is the theme of ____ developed throughout the text? / Which statement from the text supports your answer to Part A?

**Task Demand: Compare the development of universal themes within a text (10th grade)**
- How are the themes ____ and ____ developed in the passage?
- Which two statements show how the themes of ____ and ____ developed in the passage?
- Part A/B: Which two universal themes are present in the passage? / How are the two themes developed differently?
- Part A/B: How are the themes ____ and ____ in the passage? / Which statements from the passage support your response to Part A?
- Complete the table by selecting the universal themes present in the passage and how they are developed.

## R.1.3 Perspective & Point of View

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.1.3 — Analyze the influence of narrator perspective on a text, explaining how the author creates irony or satire.
**10th Grade:** ELA.10.R.1.3 — Analyze coming of age experiences reflected in a text and how the author represents conflicting perspectives.

### Clarifications (9th Grade)
- See Rhetorical Devices for irony (p177).

### Assessment Limits (9th Grade)
- Items may ask how irony or satire is created by narrator's perspective.
- Focus on perspective or particular attitude, NOT point of view.
- Forms of irony: situational, dramatic, verbal.
- Items should avoid using specific literary terms beyond "irony."
- Do NOT ask the student what is ironic about the situation.

### 9th Grade Question Stems
**Task Demand: Analyze how the influence of the narrator's perspective creates irony or satire**
- How does using an omniscient narrator develop irony in the passage?
- In passage ____, how does the author use the narrator's perspective to create irony?
- How does the narrator's perspective help create a satirical situation in the passage?
- Which two statements show how the author uses the narrator's perspective to create irony/satire?
- Select a sentence from paragraph ____ that shows how the author creates irony/satire through the narrator's perspective.
- Part A/B: What is the narrator's perspective about ____? / Why does that perspective create irony/satire?
- Part A/B: How does using an omniscient narrator develop irony in the passage? / Which detail from the passage supports your answer to Part A?

### Assessment Limits (10th Grade)
- Items may ask how coming-of-age experiences are reflected.
- Conflicting perspectives: between character's thoughts/feelings before and after coming of age; between characters coming of age and society/other characters; among secondary characters/society that influence the protagonist.
- Focus on AUTHOR'S CRAFT, not just the difference in perspective between two characters.
- Items may ask about events, experiences, or literary elements (setting, plot, conflict) that affect characters as they come of age.

### 10th Grade Question Stems
**Task Demand: Analyze coming-of-age experiences reflected in a text**
- How does ____ (literary element) affect the perspective of the main character?
- How does ____ (character's) perspective change by the end of the passage?
- How does ____ (event) affect ____ (character's) perspective?
- Which two statements best describe how ____ (literary element) affect the perspective of the main character?
- Select three events that lead to ____ (character's) change of perspective.
- Which two statements best describe how ____ (character's) perspective changes by the end of the passage?
- Part A/B: How does ____ (character's) perspective changes in the text? / Which statement from the text supports your response to Part A?
- Part A/B: How does ____ (literary element) affect the perspective of the main character? / Which statement from the text supports your response to Part A?

**Task Demand: Analyze how the author represents conflicting perspectives in a coming-of-age text**
- How is ____ (character's) perspective presented differently from ____ (character's)?
- How does ____ (event) affect ____ (character) and ____ (character) differently?
- How does the text present the difference in perspectives between ____ (character) and ____ (character)?
- Select two statements that show how ____ (character's) perspective is presented differently from ____ (character's)?
- Which three statements show how the text presents the difference in perspectives between ____ (character) and ____ (character)?
- Part A/B: How is ____ (character's) perspective presented differently from ____ (character's)? / Which statement from the text supports your response to Part A?
- Complete the chart by selecting how each character's perspective is presented.

## R.1.4 Poetry

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.1.4 — Analyze the characters, structures, and themes of epic poetry.

### Clarifications
- See Literary Periods (p165).

### Assessment Limits
- Items about how characters, structures, and themes are developed in epic poetry.
- Structural elements: form, line length, white space, indentation, line breaks, stanza breaks, narrative structure.
- Items about theme should connect to the poetic form specifically.
- Characters: ask for analysis of how characters are DEVELOPED, not character traits.
- Do NOT assume student has prior knowledge of the entire epic poem.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Analyze the character(s) of epic poetry**
- How does the author develop the characterization in passage ____?
- How does ____ (character's actions) create tension?
- Part A/B: Which best describes the structure used in ____? / Which detail from the passage supports your answer to Part A?

**Task Demand: Analyze the structure(s) of epic poetry**
- Which statement best describes the epic structure used in ____?
- Part A/B: Which best describes the structure used in ____? / Which detail from the text supports your answer to Part A?

**Task Demand: Analyze the theme(s) of epic poetry**
- How is the theme of ____ conveyed in the passage?
- How does the characterization of ____ help convey the theme of ____?
- Part A/B: How is the theme of ____ conveyed in the poem? / Which evidence from the text supports the answer to Part A?

## R.2.1 Text Structure

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.2.1 — Analyze how multiple text structures and/or features convey a purpose and/or meaning in texts.

### Clarifications
- Structures: description, problem/solution, chronological, compare and contrast, cause and effect, and sequence.
- Features: table of contents, headings, captions, photographs, graphs, charts, illustrations, glossary, footnotes, annotations, appendix.

### Assessment Limits
- Items should address MORE THAN ONE text structure or feature.
- Items may ask student to compare structure of a section to structure of two different sections, or how one structure works with another to develop meaning.
- Text features: section may or may not be marked by text features.
- Items addressing text features should ask student to analyze the cumulative effect of TWO OR MORE text features.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Analyze how text structures and/or text features convey purpose or meaning**
- How does the structure of paragraph ____ convey the author's purpose?
- How does the structure of paragraph ____ relate to the rest of the passage?
- How does the difference(s) in text structure convey ____?
- How does the shift in structure in paragraphs ____ and ____ support the author's purpose?
- Why does the author include ____ and ____ (two different text features) in the passage?
- How does the use of ____ and ____ (two different text features) convey the author's purpose?
- Which two statements show how the text structure of passage 1 reveals ____?
- Part A/B: What is the structure of paragraph(s) ____? / How does the structure in Part A affect the overall meaning of the passage?
- Complete the table by selecting how each text feature contributes to the purpose of the passage.

## R.2.2 Central Idea

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.2.2 — Evaluate the support an author uses to develop the central idea(s) throughout a text.

### Clarifications
- Students are using and responsible for the appeals of logos, ethos, and pathos.
- See Rhetorical Appeals (p178) and Rhetorical Devices (p177).

### Assessment Limits
- Items may ask to evaluate the SUPPORT, the rhetorical appeals, or the rhetorical devices as they relate to central ideas.
- Do NOT simply ask student to identify the central idea — focus on the SUPPORT for that central idea.
- "Support" = information and evidence used to prove or corroborate something.
- Items should evaluate the CONTENT of the argument, not the methods to deliver it (methods → R.2.3 or R.3.4).
- Items should NOT require background knowledge of the topic.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Evaluate the support that develops the central idea(s)**
- How does the author support the idea that ____?
- How does the author of passage ____ support her claim that ____?
- Read the following excerpt from the text. Which of the following central ideas does the excerpt support?
- Which two statements support the central idea of the text?
- Which two statements from the text best support the idea that ____?
- Part A/B: What is the claim in passage ____? / How is the claim from Part A supported?
- Part A/B: Which statement best supports the idea that ____? / Why is the statement from Part A effective in supporting the central idea?
- Part A/B: How does the author support the claim that ____? / Which sentence from the passage supports the answer in Part A?

## R.2.3 Purpose & Perspective

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.2.3 — Analyze how an author establishes and achieves purpose(s) through rhetorical appeals and/or figurative language.

### Clarifications
- Figurative language: metaphor, simile, alliteration, onomatopoeia, personification, hyperbole, meiosis (understatement), allusion, and idiom.
- Students will explain the appropriateness of appeals in achieving a purpose (logos, ethos, pathos).

### Assessment Limits
- Items may ask how purpose is conveyed through figurative language types listed above.
- Items may analyze how purpose is conveyed through appeals of logos, ethos, and pathos.
- Do NOT use specific terms or assume student knowledge of those terms.
- Items must be directly related to the author's purpose.
- Purpose should be conveyed as a STATEMENT, not a one- or two-word description.
- Focus on development of purpose or how purpose is conveyed through rhetorical appeals, NOT asking student to restate the purpose.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Analyze how an author establishes and achieves purpose(s) through figurative language**
- How does ____ (figurative language) in paragraph ____ relate to the overall purpose of the text?
- Read the following excerpt. How does the use of ____ (figurative language) contribute to the purpose ____ (purpose) of the text?
- How does ____ (figurative language) in paragraphs ____ and ____ contribute to ____ (purpose)?
- Which two statements show the relationship between the use of ____ (figurative language) and ____ (purpose)?
- Select the ____ (figurative language) that contributes to ____ (purpose).
- Part A/B: What is the purpose of the text? / How does the use of ____ (figurative language) contribute to the purpose in Part A?
- Part A/B: What is the overall purpose of the text? / Which figurative language from the text contributes to the purpose in Part A?

**Task Demand: Analyze how an author establishes and achieves purpose(s) through rhetorical appeals**
- How does ____ (rhetorical appeal) in paragraph ____ relate to the overall purpose of the text?
- Read the following excerpt. How does the use of ____ (rhetorical appeal) contribute to the purpose of the text?
- The purpose of passage ____ is to ____. Which two rhetorical appeals from the text develop that purpose.
- Which rhetorical appeals from paragraphs ____ and ____ contribute to ____ (author's purpose)?
- Select the rhetorical appeal in paragraph ____ that contributes to the author's purpose.
- Part A/B: What is the purpose of the passage? / How does the use of rhetorical appeals contribute to that purpose?
- Part A/B: What is the main purpose of the text? / Which rhetorical appeal from the text contributes to that purpose?
- Complete the table by selecting how each rhetorical appeal conveys the given purpose.

## R.2.4 Argument

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.2.4 — Compare the development of two opposing arguments on the same topic, evaluating the effectiveness and validity of the claims.

### Clarifications
- Validity = the soundness of the arguments.

### Assessment Limits
- Items ask how two opposing arguments about the same topic are developed.
- Focus on differences in AUTHORS' CRAFT, not just identifying the disagreement.
- Items addressing effectiveness/validity should NOT contain subjective value judgments.
- Items should NOT require outside knowledge.
- Sources of additional information: excerpt from authoritative source, new text feature (R.2.1).

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Compare the development of two opposing arguments on the same topic**
- How does the author of passage 1 address the claim differently than the author of passage 2?
- How is the claim in passage 1 developed differently to the claim in passage 2?
- Select two statements that demonstrate the differences in the development of the claims in passages 1 and 2.
- Which two statements reveal how the two authors develop opposing arguments on ____?
- Part A/B: How do the two authors develop opposing arguments? / Select evidence from both texts that supports your answer to Part A.
- Part A/B: How does the author of passage 1 develop the argument that ____? / How does the author of passage 2 develops a counterclaim to passage 1?
- Complete the table by selecting how the argument in passages 1 and 2 is developed.

**Task Demand: Evaluate the effectiveness or validity of claims**
- Read the following excerpt. How effective is that evidence in supporting the claim that ____?
- Which of the following statements would be a credible piece of evidence to support the claim that ____?
- Why is the claim that ____ effective?
- Which statement from passage 1, if true, invalidates the claim in passage 2?
- Which excerpt is a relevant support of the claim that ____?
- Read the following excerpt from ____. Which claim from the passage would be supported by this source?
- Which two statements from the text best support the idea that ____?
- Which two pieces of evidence would serve as relevant support for the idea that ____?
- Select three statements that explain why the idea that ____ is an effective claim.
- Part A/B: Which statement is a claim from the passage? / Which statement from the passage best support your answer to Part A?
- Part A/B: What is the claim of the passage? / Select an excerpt from another source that supports the claim in Part A.
- Complete the chart by selecting how each claim is supported in the text.

## R.3.1 Figurative Language & Mood

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.3.1 — Explain how figurative language creates mood in text(s).

### Clarifications
- Figurative language: metaphor, simile, alliteration, onomatopoeia, personification, hyperbole, meiosis (understatement), allusion, and idiom.

### Assessment Limits
- Items ask how figurative language creates mood regardless of text type.
- Do NOT ask student to identify the TYPE of figurative language used.
- Do NOT ask student to identify an instance of a type of figurative language.
- Do NOT ask student to identify the mood in isolation.
- Focus on author's CRAFT — how figurative language creates mood.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Explain how figurative language creates mood**
- How does ____ (figurative language) create the ____ mood?
- How does the ____ (figurative language) in paragraph ____ develop the ____ mood?
- How does the use of ____ (figurative language) contribute to the overall mood of the text?
- Read the following excerpt. How does the ____ (figurative language) in the sentence(s) contribute to the ____ mood?
- How does the ____ (figurative language) convey a ____ mood?
- How does the author's description of ____ in paragraph ____ create a(n) ____ mood?
- How does the ____ (figurative language) in lines ____ contribute to the mood?
- Select two details from the text that contribute to the ____ mood.
- Part A/B: What is the overall mood of the passage? / Which detail(s) from the text develop(s) that mood?
- Part A/B: How does the author develop the ____ mood? / Which detail(s) from the text support(s) the answer to Part A?

## R.3.3 Comparative Reading

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.3.3 — Compare and contrast the ways in which authors have adapted mythical, classical, or religious literary texts.

### Clarifications
- Classical source texts: ancient Greece or Rome's Classical period (1200 BCE–455 CE).
- Mythical texts: any civilization's early history.
- Religious texts: include works such as the Bible.

### Assessment Limits
- Items ask to compare key elements of original texts with adaptations (setting, plot, characterization, conflict, theme, tone).
- Focus on the MODERN AUTHOR'S CRAFT, not just identifying similarities/differences.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Compare and contrast adaptations of mythical, classical, or religious literary texts**
- How do passages 2 and 3 adapt the ____ (literary element) found in passage 1?
- How does passage 2 transform the ____ (literary element) from passage 1?
- Which statement(s) best describe(s) how Passage 2 draws on and transforms some of the material in Passage 1?
- Which statement best describes how ____ adapts ____?

## R.3.4 Rhetoric

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.R.3.4 — Explain an author's use of rhetoric in a text.

### Clarifications
- Rhetorical devices = figurative language devices from R.3.1 PLUS: irony, rhetorical question, antithesis, zeugma, metonymy, and synecdoche.

### Assessment Limits
- Items ask HOW an author applies rhetoric, NOT identification of type.
- Do NOT ask student to identify type of figurative language or rhetoric.
- Do NOT ask student to identify an example of a type.
- Focus on AUTHOR'S CRAFT.
- If figurative language is establishing mood → align to R.3.1.
- If related to purpose → align to R.2.3.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Explain the use of rhetoric**
- How does the author use ____ (ethos/pathos/logos) in the text?
- How does the author use ____ (ethos/pathos/logos) to develop the idea that ____?
- Read the following excerpt. How does the use of ____ in the excerpt develop the author's purpose?
- How does the author use rhetoric to develop his/her purpose?
- Why does the author use ____ (rhetorical device) in paragraph ____?
- How does the use of ____ (rhetorical device) develops the author's purpose?
- Select two statements that explain how the use of rhetoric develops the author's purpose.
- Part A/B: How does the author use ____ (ethos/pathos/logos) in the text? / Which statement(s) from the text supports the answer to Part A?
- Part A/B: How does the author use ____ (rhetorical device) in the text? / Which statement(s) from the text supports the answer to Part A?

## V.1.2 Morphology

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.V.1.2 — Apply knowledge of etymology and derivations to determine meanings of words and phrases in grade-level content.

### Clarifications
- Etymology = study of word origins and how they changed over time.
- Derivation = making new words from existing words by adding affixes.

### Assessment Limits
- Items should ask to determine meaning using knowledge of etymology, derivations, roots, and affixes.
- Distractors should include common misunderstandings including application of the wrong root or affix meaning.
- Items may ask about differences between two or more words that share a similar origin.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Determine the meaning of words or phrases by applying knowledge of etymology and derivations**
- Read the sentence from the text. The Latin word ____ means ____. Based on that, what is the meaning of the underlined word?
- Read the sentence from the text. The Greek affix ____ means ____. Based on that, what does the word ____ mean in the sentence.
- Select a word in paragraph ____ that comes from the root meaning ____.

## V.1.3 Context & Connotation

**9th Grade:** ELA.9.V.1.3 — Apply knowledge of context clues, figurative language, word relationships, reference materials, and/or background knowledge to determine the connotative and denotative meaning of words and phrases.

### Clarifications
- Review of words learned is critical to building background knowledge and related vocabulary.
- See Context Clues (p205) and Word Relationships (p205).
- See ELA.9.R.3.1 and Secondary Figurative Language (p176).

### Assessment Limits
- Words should have clear and sufficient context for determining meaning.
- Items should NOT focus on dictionary word meanings but on how words function within text context.
- Items may focus on words with figurative or allusive meanings central to the text meaning (not isolated, incidental vocabulary).
- Items may ask about words with discrete context clues in close proximity OR words whose meaning is conveyed more implicitly throughout the text.
- Items may require student to make connections between words and to delve into figurative or connotative meanings.

### Task Demands & Question Stems

**Task Demand: Determine the meaning of a word or phrase using appropriate strategies**
- Read the following excerpt sentences. What is the meaning of ____ as used in the sentence?
- Read the following sentences. What is the meaning of the word (or phrase) ____, based on the context of the passage.
- Select the phrase that reveals the meaning of the word ____?
- Part A/B: What is the meaning of ____ as used in paragraph ____? / Which of the following statements from paragraph ____ supports the answer to Part A?

**Task Demand: Distinguish between the connotative and denotative meanings of a word**
- Read the following sentence from the passage. In which sentence does the underlined word have a connotative meaning similar to the word ____ in Passage 1?
- How does the phrase "____" impact the meaning of paragraph ____?
- How do the metaphors in lines ____ help the reader understand ____?

## How to Use This Reference

### When Building MC Questions (any skill or agent):
1. Identify the target benchmark
2. Find the matching section in this reference
3. Select a question stem from the Task Demands list
4. Adapt the stem by filling in the blanks with text-specific details
5. Check the Assessment Limits to make sure your item doesn't violate any constraints
6. Use Achievement Level Descriptors to calibrate difficulty (Level 2 = DOK 1, Level 3 = DOK 2, Level 4-5 = DOK 3)

### When Building Assessments:
1. Include a MIX of single-part and Part A/B two-part questions
2. Part A/B format: "This question has two parts. First, answer Part A. Then, answer Part B."
3. Part A typically asks for analysis/identification; Part B asks for text evidence supporting Part A
4. Assessment items should reflect Achievement Level 3-4 (not just Level 2 recall)

### When Building Bellringers:
- Context clue bellringers use V.1.3 stems: "What is the meaning of ____ as used in the sentence?"
- Morphology bellringers use V.1.2 stems: "The Latin/Greek affix ____ means ____. Based on that, what is the meaning of ____?"
- Benchmark vocab bellringers adapt the relevant benchmark's stems to test understanding of analytical terms

### Assessment Limit Guardrails (Common Violations to Avoid):
- R.1.2: Do NOT ask students to define what the theme is → ask HOW it's developed
- R.1.3 (9th): Do NOT ask "what is ironic?" → ask how perspective CREATES irony
- R.2.2: Do NOT ask students to identify the central idea → ask about the SUPPORT for it
- R.3.1: Do NOT ask to identify type of figurative language → ask how it creates MOOD
- R.3.4: Do NOT ask to identify type of rhetoric → ask how author USES it
- R.2.4: Do NOT include subjective value judgments about effectiveness

## Planning Card Analytical Processes (10th Grade)

Source: ELA Planning Cards, Office of Academics and Transformation, Division of Academics, Department of English Language Arts

**PURPOSE:** These step-by-step processes show HOW students should analyze text for each benchmark. Use these to design organizer scaffolding, model thinking during I Do/We Do, and ensure MC question stems align with the analytical process students are taught.

---

### R.1.1 Literary Elements — Analytical Process (Grade 10)

**ELA.10.R.1.1 Analyze how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning and/or style in a literary text.**

**Task Demand 1: Analyze how key elements enhance or add layers of meaning in a literary text.**

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| Step 1 | Summarize what is happening in the story (key elements): What are the setting and characters? How do the characters develop? What is the conflict? How is the conflict resolved? What is the point of view of the text? What are the themes of the text? |
| Step 2 | Analyze how the key elements develop the mood of the text: What feelings are evoked by the reader? (Look at setting, imagery, word choice, sentence structure/syntax; What is the overall feel/atmosphere?) Identify interesting or important word choices or syntax (nuanced words, unusual or strong phrasing, figurative language). Change the wording to analyze the impact (remove figurative language, replace specifics with generalities, replace nuanced/unusual words with ordinary ones). What mood is suggested? What is the impact on the text? |
| Step 3 | Analyze how the key elements develop the tone of the text: What is the author's attitude? (Look at word choice, dialogue, narration, sentence structure/syntax, imagery, rhetorical devices, and point of view.) Identify interesting or important word choices or syntax throughout the development of the text. Change the wording to analyze the impact. Analyze the effects of having different points of view — do they create humor, suspense, or another mood? Do they advance the story? What tone is suggested throughout? What is the impact on the text? |
| Step 4 | Analyze how key elements develop the author's purpose: What effect is the author attempting to achieve? How is this effect achieved throughout the text? (Look at word choice, dialogue, narration, sentence structure/syntax, imagery, figurative language, mood, tone, and point of view.) |
| Step 5 | Analyze the relationship between these key elements: What meaning is created with these layers? Does the author lead with one layer over others? Does it provide context, depth, and/or structure? What is the impact on the text? |

**Task Demand 2: Analyze the techniques the author uses to develop style in a literary text.**

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| Step 1 | What techniques does the writer use for effect? Look at the writer's use of: diction (word choice), syntax (arrangement of words and phrases), grammar, figurative language. |
| Step 2 | How do these techniques help to create the writer's voice? What makes this author unique? What lens does the author use to view the world? What does the writer value? What does the writer challenge/oppose? Is there bias? |
| Step 3 | Analyze the way in which the writer uses specific techniques for effect: How are these techniques used to communicate the writer's message more effectively? Do these techniques help to engage the reader with the text? What is the impact on the text? |

### R.1.2 Theme — Analytical Process (Grade 10)

**ELA.10.R.1.2 Analyze and compare universal themes and their development throughout a literary text.**

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| Step 1 | Choose a universal theme in the text. Make sure the theme applies to anyone, anytime, anywhere regardless of cultural differences. |
| Step 2 | When and how is the universal theme introduced? Look for clues in: dialogue, description, setting, events, character development, conflict, symbols or repeated language. |
| Step 3 | Gather evidence of the universal theme throughout the text including: surprising developments, changes or added detail, when and how the message becomes clear, how does the conflict become more complicated? |
| Step 4 | Analyze how the universal theme develops from the beginning to the end: When is the theme introduced? Which key details are added as the story moves forward? How is the conflict resolved? |
| Step 5 | Repeat the above steps for additional universal themes (at least 2-3). |
| Step 6 | Look for similarities and differences: How are the messages connected? What do they have in common? Are the messages bleak or uplifting? Do the themes address an individual struggle or those that affect society, a group of people, family, etc.? |

### R.1.3 Perspective & Point of View — Analytical Process (Grade 10)

**ELA.10.R.1.3 Analyze coming of age experiences reflected in a text and how the author represents conflicting perspectives.**

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| Step 1 | What do you know about the narrator(s) and/or character(s)? Is it in first person, second person, or third person limited/omniscient? Are there multiple narrators/characters in the text? How are they introduced? How do they grow or change? When are they similar or different? |
| Step 2 | When do you first notice the narrator/character's perspective? |
| Step 3 | What do the narrator(s)/character(s) think, feel, and do? Look for details that show perspective: strong feelings, descriptive language, tone of voice, what is shared/not shared, dialogue, actions and responses. |
| Step 4 | Analyze how the narrator/characters' perspective is developed/established: What details show each character's perspective? Are there any changes in perspective throughout the text? What are they? What knowledge or ignorance is evident? |
| Step 5 | Analyze the coming-of-age experiences in a text: What events do the character(s) go through as they age from childhood to adulthood or internal maturation/degradation? Look for: trials, obstacles, revelations, internal struggles, external forces, adversaries, social pressures/expectations, relationships, and self-discovery. What emotional changes are influenced by these events? How does an experience, event, or literary element affect the perspective of the character(s)? How does the perspective of character(s) change as a result? |
| Step 6 | Analyze how the author represents conflicting perspectives in a coming-of-age text: How is one character's perspective represented differently from another's? How are characters affected differently by the same literary element? How are the differences in perspective between characters represented in the text? |

### R.2.1 Text Structure — Analytical Process (Grade 9, applicable to Grade 10)

**ELA.9.R.2.1 Analyze how multiple text structures and/or features convey a purpose and/or meaning in texts.**
*(Note: The Planning Cards do not include a separate Grade 10 R.2.1 card. The Grade 9 process below applies, with 10th grade expecting deeper analysis of how MULTIPLE structures work together.)*

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| Step 1 | What are the important ideas in this text? What is the central idea of the text? |
| Step 2 | Study clues to the overall structures of the text (text features: chapters/sections, headings, table of contents, indices, glossaries, bolded words, sidebars, pictures, photographs, illustrations, captions, labeled diagrams, subheadings, footnotes, annotations, appendices). Study the organizational structures: CHRONOLOGICAL (order of events), PROCESS/SEQUENCE (steps in a process), CAUSE AND EFFECT (explains why or how), PROBLEM/SOLUTION, COMPARE/CONTRAST, DESCRIPTION. |
| Step 3 | Study how a specific text section/feature fits into the overall structure: Where in the text does the section and/or text feature appear? What purpose does it fulfill? |
| Step 4 | Analyze how multiple text sections/features convey the author's ideas/meaning: How do they clarify the author's ideas? What information do they provide? Why did the author include these? How do they impact the meaning? How does a particular section/feature support the meaning of a developed idea? Compare overall structure to the structure of a section — how does this develop meaning? Compare the structure of two different sections. Analyze how one text structure works in conjunction with another. Analyze the cumulative effect of two or more text features on meaning. |
| Step 5 | Identify the author's purpose (persuading/arguing for or against something, or teaching/providing information?). How do multiple text sections/features help to communicate the author's purpose? How do multiple sections/features support the purpose? |
| Step 6 | Analyze how multiple text sections/features affect the author's purpose: How do they clarify the author's ideas? What information do the text sections/text features provide? Why did the author include this part of the text? |
