# CER Writing Guide - Claim, Evidence, Reasoning

## Purpose

CER is the primary analytical writing framework for 10th grade Intensive Reading. Students use CER for constructed responses on Days 5-6 assessments, organizer analysis rows, and any task requiring text-based argumentation.

**Related frameworks:**
- **RACE** (Restate, Answer, Cite, Explain) — used for shorter, more structured responses. See `assessment-design` skill.
- **CER** — used for deeper analytical and argumentative tasks where students must build and defend a claim.

## CER Components

### C — Claim
The student's interpretation or position that answers the prompt.

**Requirements:**
- Directly answers the question asked
- Debatable — not just restating a fact
- Uses benchmark language (theme, purpose, rhetoric, etc.)
- Specific enough to be provable with text evidence

**Quality Progression:**
| Level | Example (Theme prompt) |
|-------|----------------------|
| Below | "The story has a theme." |
| Approaching | "The theme is about hard work." |
| Meets | "The author develops the theme that perseverance through adversity leads to personal growth." |
| Exceeds | "Through the protagonist's repeated failures and eventual transformation, the author develops the universal theme that perseverance through adversity is the catalyst for personal growth." |

**Common Errors:**
- Restating the question instead of answering it
- Making a factual statement instead of an interpretive claim
- Being too vague ("The author uses literary devices")
- Not addressing the specific benchmark skill

### E — Evidence
Specific textual support proving the claim.

**Requirements:**
- Direct quotes from the text in quotation marks
- Paragraph number citations
- Relevant — directly supports the claim (not just interesting)
- Sufficient — 1-2 pieces minimum for Meets, 2+ for Exceeds

**How to Teach Evidence Selection:**

**Step 1: Identify potential evidence**
- Scan text for sections related to claim
- Mark 3-4 possible quotes

**Step 2: Evaluate evidence quality**
Ask: "Does this quote PROVE my claim, or just relate to the topic?"

| Strong Evidence | Weak Evidence |
|----------------|---------------|
| Directly proves the claim | Only relates to the topic |
| Specific quote with details | Vague or general statement |
| Shows the skill in action | Just tells what happened |

**Step 3: Cite properly**
- Format: In paragraph [#], the author states, "[exact quote]."
- Keep quotes to 1-2 sentences max
- Don't start sentences with the quote

**Sentence Frames for Evidence (scaffold progression):**
- Days 1-2: "In paragraph ___, the text states, '___________.' "
- Days 3-4: "In paragraph ___, the author ___________."
- Days 5-6: Remove frames — students cite independently

### R — Reasoning
Explains HOW the evidence supports the claim. This is where most students struggle.

**Requirements:**
- Explicitly connects evidence back to claim
- Explains the "so what?" — why this evidence matters
- Uses analytical language, not summary
- Demonstrates benchmark-level thinking

**The Reasoning Test:**
If you can remove the evidence and the reasoning still makes sense on its own, the reasoning is too general. Good reasoning is anchored to the specific evidence cited.

**Teaching the Difference:**

| Summary (Wrong) | Analysis (Right) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| "This shows that the character worked hard." | "This demonstrates that the author uses the character's repeated failures to develop the theme that growth requires struggle — the failures are not obstacles but necessary steps." |
| "The author uses figurative language." | "The metaphor comparing her journey to 'climbing a mountain with no summit' reveals the author's message that perseverance is valuable not for its destination but for the strength gained during the ascent." |

**Reasoning Sentence Starters (scaffold progression):**
- Days 1-2: "This shows that _____ because _____."
- Days 3-4: "This demonstrates/reveals/illustrates that _____."
- Days 5-6: Remove starters — students explain independently

**Key Reasoning Verbs:**
demonstrates, reveals, illustrates, suggests, emphasizes, reinforces, highlights, underscores, conveys, develops

## Complete CER Model

**Prompt:** How does the author develop the theme of perseverance in the passage?

**Model Response:**

**[Claim]** The author develops the theme that perseverance through repeated failure ultimately leads to transformation and self-discovery.

**[Evidence]** In paragraph 12, the narrator reflects, "After the third rejection, something shifted — not in the world around her, but in the way she carried herself through it." Additionally, in paragraph 18, the author writes, "The mountain had not grown smaller; she had simply learned to climb."

**[Reasoning]** These passages reveal that the author develops the theme not through a single triumphant moment, but through a gradual internal shift. The first quote demonstrates that the character's growth comes from within — "something shifted" inside her — suggesting that perseverance changes the person, not the circumstances. The metaphor in paragraph 18 reinforces this by showing that obstacles remain unchanged; it is the character's capacity that evolves. Together, these details develop the theme that perseverance is not about the world becoming easier but about the individual becoming stronger.

## Scoring Rubric

### CER Rubric (8-point scale)

| Component | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----------|---|---|---|---|
| **Claim** (0-2) | Missing or off-topic | Present but vague, doesn't fully address prompt | Clear, specific, addresses prompt with benchmark language | — |
| **Evidence** (0-3) | Missing | 1 piece, may lack citation or relevance | 1-2 relevant quotes with paragraph citations | 2+ strong, relevant quotes with accurate citations |
| **Reasoning** (0-3) | Missing | Present but summarizes instead of analyzes | Connects evidence to claim, explains HOW | Deep analysis showing benchmark-level thinking, explains significance |

**Score Interpretation:**
- 7-8: Exceeds — ready for grade-level analytical writing
- 5-6: Meets — demonstrates benchmark understanding
- 3-4: Approaching — needs targeted support on weak component
- 0-2: Below — needs intensive modeling and scaffolding

## Scaffolding Across 6-Day Cycle

### Days 1-2: Heavy Scaffolding
- Provide complete CER model for students to annotate
- Label each component (highlight Claim, Evidence, Reasoning)
- Students identify components in sample responses
- Practice: Given claim + evidence, students write reasoning only

### Days 3-4: Partial Scaffolding
- Provide sentence frames for each component
- Students write full CER with frames available
- Peer review: Partners check for all 3 components
- Teacher models thinking process during organizer work

### Days 5-6: Independent
- Remove all scaffolds (except for IEP/ESOL modifications)
- Students write full CER independently
- STOP analysis for MC + CER for constructed response
- Teacher uses CER rubric for feedback

## Common Student Errors and Fixes

| Error | Example | Fix |
|-------|---------|-----|
| Evidence without reasoning | "In paragraph 5, it says '...' This proves the theme." | Ask: "HOW does it prove it? What does this evidence show?" |
| Reasoning that summarizes | "This shows the character was sad." | Ask: "So what? Why does this matter for the theme/benchmark?" |
| Claim too vague | "The author uses literary devices." | Ask: "WHICH device? To do WHAT? About WHAT theme?" |
| Evidence not relevant | Quote exists in text but doesn't support claim | Ask: "Does this PROVE your claim, or just relate to the topic?" |
| No paragraph citation | Student writes evidence without location | Require format: "In paragraph [#]..." |

## ESOL Modifications for CER

- **Sentence frames** for all 3 components (keep through Day 6 for ESOL Levels 1-3)
- **Word bank** with reasoning verbs (demonstrates, reveals, illustrates)
- **Bilingual glossary** for benchmark terms
- **Reduced evidence requirement** (1 quote instead of 2 for Meets)
- **Oral rehearsal** before writing: student says CER to partner first
