# ELA.10.R.2.2 - Central Idea (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.2.2**: Analyze and evaluate central ideas in informational texts, including how they are developed and refined through supporting details.

## Benchmark Clarifications
This benchmark applies to **informational texts** (not literary). The central idea is the **most important point** the author wants readers to understand.

**Central Idea vs. Topic:**
- **Topic**: What the text is about (one word or phrase: "climate change," "social media")
- **Central Idea**: What the author is saying about the topic (full sentence: "Climate change requires immediate global action")

**Central Idea vs. Theme:**
- **Central idea**: Used for informational texts
- **Theme**: Used for literary texts (use R.1.2)

**Key Components:**
1. **Identify** the central idea
2. **Analyze** how it's developed through supporting details
3. **Trace** how it's refined or evolves throughout the text
4. **Evaluate** how effectively it's developed

**NOT just topic or summary:**
- "This text is about bees" ❌ (topic, not central idea)
- "The text discusses bee populations" ❌ (still just topic)
- "Bee populations are declining due to pesticides, habitat loss, and disease" ✓ (central idea)

## Prerequisite Skills & Common Gaps

**Students may struggle with:**
- Confusing topic with central idea
- Writing summary instead of central idea statement
- Identifying supporting details but not connecting to central idea
- Believing central idea must appear explicitly in text

**Built-in Remediation:**
- Practice topic vs. central idea with short paragraphs
- Use formula: "The author wants us to understand that [central idea]"
- Model finding implicit central ideas (not directly stated)
- Teach that supporting details are evidence FOR the central idea

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Topic
Determine what the text is about:
- What is the subject? (one word or short phrase)
- What keeps appearing throughout?

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the topic of this text?"

### Step 2: Identify the Central Idea
Determine the main point about the topic:
- What is the author's most important point?
- What does the author want readers to understand?
- Can you state it in one complete sentence?

**Key move**: Turn topic into statement
- Topic: "Online learning"
- Central idea: "Online learning offers flexibility but requires strong self-discipline"

**Look for:**
- Thesis statement (often in introduction)
- Repeated points
- What conclusion or main point emerges

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the central idea?"

### Step 3: Identify Supporting Details
Find evidence that develops the central idea:
- What facts, examples, statistics support it?
- What explanations or descriptions develop it?
- How does the author prove or illustrate the point?

**Types of supporting details:**
- Facts and statistics
- Examples and anecdotes
- Expert quotations
- Definitions and explanations
- Comparisons and contrasts

**Organizer prompt**: "What details support and develop the central idea?"

### Step 4: Analyze How Central Idea Is Developed and Refined
Trace how the idea evolves:
- How is the idea introduced?
- How does it develop throughout (beginning, middle, end)?
- Does the idea become more specific or complex?
- How do sections/paragraphs build on each other?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the central idea develop from beginning to end?"

### Step 5: Evaluate Effectiveness
Assess how well the central idea is developed:
- Is the central idea clearly conveyed?
- Are supporting details relevant and sufficient?
- Is the development logical and coherent?
- What would strengthen the development?

**Organizer prompt**: "How effectively is the central idea developed?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Identification Questions
- What is the central idea of this text/section?
- What is the author's main point about [topic]?
- What does the author want readers to understand?

### Supporting Detail Questions
- What details support the central idea?
- How does the author develop the central idea through [structure/evidence]?
- What examples/facts/explanations support the main point?

### Development Questions
- How is the central idea introduced?
- How does the central idea develop from beginning to end?
- How is the central idea refined or made more specific?
- How do different sections contribute to the central idea?

### Evaluation Questions
- How effectively does the author develop the central idea?
- Are the supporting details relevant and sufficient?
- What would strengthen the development of the central idea?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Identifies implicit or complex central ideas
- Analyzes sophisticated development and refinement
- Evaluates effectiveness with specific criteria
- Distinguishes between central and supporting ideas

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Accurately identifies central idea (explicit or implicit)
- Analyzes how supporting details develop central idea
- Traces refinement or development across text
- Provides relevant textual evidence

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies basic central idea (often explicit)
- Recognizes some supporting details
- Notes where central idea appears
- Provides limited analysis of development

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Confuses topic with central idea
- Difficulty distinguishing main ideas from details
- Limited identification of supporting evidence
- Minimal analysis of how idea develops

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Informational articles, essays, speeches
- Texts with clear central idea (explicit or implicit)
- Texts with multiple supporting details
- Texts where central idea develops or refines
- Passages from textbooks, magazines, newspapers

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Literary fiction (use R.1.2 Theme instead)
- Texts with multiple unrelated central ideas
- Very short texts without development
- Purely procedural texts (recipes, instructions)

## Common Central Idea Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- central idea (main point)
- topic (subject)
- support (back up, provide evidence for)
- develop (expand, elaborate)
- refine (make more specific or clear)
- detail (specific fact or example)
- evidence (proof, support)
- convey (communicate)
- implicit (suggested, not stated directly)
- explicit (clearly stated)

**Analysis Terms:**
- supporting detail (evidence)
- main idea (primary point)
- thesis (central claim)
- summary (brief overview)
- introduction/body/conclusion
- relevant (connected, appropriate)
- sufficient (enough)

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For article: "Why Finland's Schools Are So Successful"**

| Central Idea | Supporting Detail 1 | Supporting Detail 2 | Supporting Detail 3 | How Idea Is Developed |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Finland's educational success stems from prioritizing teacher quality, minimizing standardized testing, and emphasizing equity over competition.** (Note: This is never stated in one sentence—it's implicit, synthesized from the entire text.) | **Teacher quality**: "Finland accepts only the top 10% of university graduates into teaching programs" (para 3). Teachers earn respect and autonomy similar to doctors. Detail shows how Finland invests in skilled teachers rather than testing systems. | **Minimal testing**: "Finnish students take only one standardized test—at age 16" (para 7). Less test prep means more time for deep learning, creativity, and critical thinking. This contrasts with test-heavy systems and supports the "why they succeed" claim. | **Equity over competition**: "There are no 'gifted' tracks or ability grouping" (para 10). All students get same quality education. Extra support for struggling students ensures no one falls behind. This explains how Finland achieves high results for ALL students. | The central idea develops across three major sections. The introduction establishes Finland's success (high test scores, graduation rates). The body explains three causes (teacher quality, less testing, equity). Each section provides evidence. The conclusion synthesizes: "When you trust teachers, reduce testing pressure, and ensure equity, students thrive." The idea is **refined** from "Finland does well" (beginning) to "here are three specific reasons why" (middle) to "these principles work together" (end). |

## ESOL Scaffolds for Central Idea

**Sentence frames:**
- "The topic of this text is _______."
- "The central idea is that _______." (complete sentence)
- "The author wants us to understand that _______."
- "This is supported by _______ (detail from paragraph ___)."
- "The central idea develops by _______."
- "At first, the author _______. Then, _______. Finally, _______."

**Vocabulary support:**
- Anchor chart: Topic (word/phrase) vs. Central Idea (complete sentence)
- Formula: Topic + What author says about it = Central Idea
- Provide "supporting detail transition words" list (for example, in addition, furthermore, specifically)

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Partner identification of topic first
- Then discuss: "What is the author saying about this topic?"
- Share central idea statement with class before writing

**Graphic organizers:**
- Main idea/supporting details web (center = central idea, branches = details)
- Beginning/Middle/End chart for tracing development

## Prerequisite Mini-Lessons (Built-In Remediation)

**Day 1 Focus**: Topic vs. Central Idea - Use short paragraphs, practice distinguishing

**Day 2 Focus**: Explicit vs. Implicit - Some texts state central idea directly; others require inference

**Day 3 Focus**: Supporting details - Practice identifying which details support main point vs. which are minor

**Day 4 Focus**: Formula practice - Topic + what author says about it = Central idea

**Day 5+**: Apply to target text with gradual release

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Analyze and evaluate central ideas, including how they're developed through supporting details

**Key Process**: Identify topic → State central idea → Find supporting details → Analyze development → Evaluate effectiveness

**Essential Question**: "What is the author's main point, and how is it developed throughout the text?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students state the topic instead of central idea OR write summary instead of central idea statement

## Feedback Checkpoints

### Day 4 Checkpoint: Core Skills Development

**Purpose**: Verify students can identify central ideas, cite supporting details, and explain development.

**Check for:**
1. **Central idea stated** (author's main point, not topic)
   - Complete sentence (not just topic word/phrase)
   - Shows what author says about the topic
   - Example: "Climate change requires immediate global action" ✓ vs. "Climate change" ❌

2. **Supporting details cited** (2-3 key details)
   - Specific evidence from text (facts, examples, quotations)
   - Evidence includes paragraph numbers for reference
   - Details directly support the central idea

3. **How idea is developed shown** (introduction → development → conclusion)
   - Traces central idea through beginning, middle, end
   - Shows how idea is introduced, expanded, refined
   - Explains how each section builds on the central idea

4. **Evidence includes paragraph numbers**
   - Citations reference specific locations in text
   - Format: "Paragraph 3" or "(para 3)"

**Teacher Focus Targets** (if checkpoint reveals gaps):
- Clarify central idea vs. topic (use formula: topic + what author says = central idea)
- Show HOW details develop the idea (not just list details)
- Use specific supporting details with paragraph numbers
- Trace development through text sections

**Action if students struggle:**
- Reteach topic vs. central idea distinction
- Model finding implicit central ideas (not always directly stated)
- Practice citing evidence with paragraph references
- Use Beginning/Middle/End chart to trace development

### Day 6 Checkpoint: Quality & Precision

**Purpose**: Ensure responses demonstrate proficient understanding of central idea analysis.

**Quality Indicators** (What proficient answers include):
- **Central idea**: States main point (not topic)
- **Development**: Shows HOW details support and develop the idea
- **Specific evidence**: Cites relevant details with paragraph numbers
- **Traced progression**: Explains how idea evolves through text

**Common Quality Issues to Address:**

1. **States topic instead of central idea**
   - Student writes: "This text is about online learning" ❌
   - Should be: "Online learning offers flexibility but requires strong self-discipline" ✓
   - Fix: Use formula "The author wants us to understand that [central idea]"

2. **Lists details without showing development**
   - Student lists: "Detail 1, detail 2, detail 3"
   - Missing: HOW each detail develops the central idea
   - Fix: After each detail, add "This supports the central idea by..."

3. **Doesn't explain HOW details support idea**
   - Student cites evidence but doesn't connect to central idea
   - Fix: Require explicit connection ("This detail shows that...")

4. **Vague central idea statement**
   - Student writes: "The author talks about problems"
   - Fix: Require specific, complete sentence stating author's main point

**Teacher Focus Targets** (targeted re-teaching):
- Clarify central idea (not topic) - use sentence frames
- Show HOW details develop idea - model explicit connections
- Use specific supporting details - require paragraph numbers
- Trace development through text - use progression chart

**Action for common patterns:**
- If 50%+ state topic: Reteach with topic vs. central idea anchor chart
- If development unclear: Model with "Beginning/Middle/End" organizer
- If vague statements: Use formula "The author's main point is that..."
- If missing connections: Require sentence frame "This supports the central idea by..."
