# ELA.10.R.2.3 - Purpose & Perspective (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.2.3**: Analyze author's purpose and perspective in historical and informational texts.

## Benchmark Clarifications
This benchmark applies to **informational texts** (not literary). Students must identify both **purpose** (why the author wrote it) and **perspective** (the author's viewpoint, bias, or beliefs).

**Author's Purpose:**
- **Inform**: Provide facts, explain concepts
- **Persuade**: Convince readers of a position
- **Entertain**: Engage or amuse (less common in informational texts)
- **Multiple purposes**: Texts often have primary + secondary purposes

**Author's Perspective:**
- Point of view or bias
- Background, experiences, beliefs shaping the writing
- What the author emphasizes or omits
- Tone revealing attitude toward subject

**Historical Context Matters:**
- When was this written?
- What events/movements were happening?
- How does time period shape perspective?

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Author's Purpose
Determine why the author wrote the text:
- What is the author trying to accomplish?
- Is the primary purpose to inform, persuade, or entertain?
- Are there secondary purposes?

**Look for:**
- Direct statements of purpose
- Text structure clues (argument structure = persuade, chronological = inform)
- Tone and word choice
- Call to action (signals persuasion)

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the author's purpose? How do you know?"

### Step 2: Identify the Author's Perspective
Determine the author's viewpoint or bias:
- What does the author believe about this topic?
- What is the author's background or position?
- What perspective does the author represent?
- Is there evidence of bias?

**Look for:**
- Explicit statements of belief
- Repeated emphasis on certain ideas
- What's included vs. omitted
- Tone (neutral, passionate, critical)
- Loaded language or connotation

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the author's perspective or bias?"

### Step 3: Analyze How Purpose Shapes Content
Connect purpose to author's choices:
- What evidence/examples does the author include?
- How does purpose affect organization?
- What language choices support the purpose?
- What techniques does the author use?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the author's purpose shape the content?"

### Step 4: Analyze How Perspective Shapes Content
Connect perspective to author's choices:
- What does the author emphasize or downplay?
- How does perspective affect evidence selection?
- What assumptions does the author make?
- How does historical context influence perspective?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the author's perspective shape the content?"

### Step 5: Evaluate Effectiveness
Determine how well the author achieves purpose:
- Does the author accomplish their purpose?
- How does perspective strengthen or weaken the text?
- How might different readers respond?
- What questions or concerns arise?

**Organizer prompt**: "How effective is the author in achieving their purpose?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Purpose Questions
- What is the author's purpose? (inform, persuade, entertain)
- How does the author's purpose shape the content?
- What does the author want readers to understand or believe?
- Is there more than one purpose? Explain.

### Perspective Questions
- What perspective or bias is evident?
- What does the author believe about [topic]?
- How does the author's background influence the text?
- What does the author emphasize or omit?

### Integration Questions
- How do purpose and perspective work together?
- How does historical context shape the author's perspective?
- What evidence reveals both purpose and perspective?

### Evaluation Questions
- How effective is the author in achieving their purpose?
- How might perspective limit or strengthen the argument?
- How might different audiences respond to this perspective?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Analyzes complex relationships between purpose, perspective, and historical context
- Evaluates effectiveness of author's choices
- Identifies subtle bias or unstated assumptions
- Considers multiple perspectives and audience responses

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Analyzes author's purpose and perspective with evidence
- Explains how purpose and perspective shape content
- Identifies explicit and some implicit bias
- Connects to historical context

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies basic purpose (inform/persuade)
- Recognizes obvious perspective or bias
- Provides some textual support
- Makes simple connections to content

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Difficulty distinguishing purpose types
- Limited identification of perspective
- Minimal textual evidence
- Struggles to connect purpose/perspective to content

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Historical texts (speeches, documents, letters)
- Informational articles with clear perspective
- Texts where author's background is relevant
- Texts with identifiable bias or viewpoint
- Multiple texts with contrasting perspectives

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Literary fiction (use R.1.3 for point of view)
- Neutral encyclopedia entries with no perspective
- Texts requiring extensive historical knowledge not provided
- Technical/procedural texts without perspective

## Common Purpose & Perspective Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- purpose (reason for writing)
- perspective (viewpoint or position)
- bias (prejudice or preference)
- persuade (convince)
- inform (teach or explain)
- emphasize (stress or highlight)
- objective (neutral, fact-based)
- subjective (opinion-based)
- context (circumstances or setting)
- influence (affect or shape)

**Analysis Terms:**
- tone (attitude toward subject)
- connotation (emotional association)
- assumption (unstated belief)
- omission (what's left out)
- loaded language (emotionally charged words)
- credibility (trustworthiness)
- rhetoric (persuasive techniques)

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For excerpt from "Letter from Birmingham Jail" by Martin Luther King Jr. (1963):**

| Author's Purpose | Evidence of Purpose | Author's Perspective | Evidence of Perspective | How They Shape Content |
|------------------|---------------------|----------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| King's primary purpose is to **persuade** white clergy to support the civil rights movement. His secondary purpose is to **inform** them about the urgency and justice of the cause. | He directly addresses their criticism: "You deplore the demonstrations taking place in Birmingham." He uses logical arguments and appeals to morality. His concluding call to action shows persuasive intent: the letter demands understanding and action, not just awareness. | King writes from the perspective of a civil rights leader who is imprisoned for peaceful protest. His perspective reflects frustration with "white moderates" who prefer "order" to justice. He believes direct action is necessary because "justice too long delayed is justice denied." | King's tone shifts from respectful ("My Dear Fellow Clergymen") to increasingly urgent and frustrated ("shallow understanding from people of good will is more frustrating than absolute misunderstanding"). He emphasizes the suffering of Black Americans and critiques those who urge patience. His historical references (Socrates, early Christians) position civil rights as part of a moral tradition. | King's persuasive purpose leads him to use multiple rhetorical strategies (ethos, pathos, logos) and Biblical/historical allusions his audience would respect. His perspective as someone unjustly imprisoned gives him moral authority but also explains the emotional intensity. Writing during active struggle (1963) shapes his urgent tone—he cannot wait for gradual change while people suffer. His emphasis on morality over legality reflects his perspective that unjust laws must be resisted. |

## ESOL Scaffolds for Purpose & Perspective

**Sentence frames:**
- "The author's purpose is to _______ because _______."
- "The text structure shows the purpose is to _______ because it uses _______."
- "The author's perspective is _______, which is evident when _______."
- "The author emphasizes _______ and omits _______, which reveals _______."
- "The historical context of _______ influences the author's perspective by _______."

**Vocabulary support:**
- Create anchor chart distinguishing inform/persuade/entertain
- Provide "perspective signal words" list (I believe, in my view, clearly, obviously)
- Post "bias indicator" checklist (loaded language, what's omitted, tone)

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Partner identification of purpose before individual analysis
- Discuss possible bias before writing claims

**Graphic organizers:**
- Two-column T-chart: Purpose | Evidence
- Triangle diagram: Historical Context (top) → Perspective → Content Choices (bottom)

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Analyze author's purpose and perspective in historical and informational texts

**Key Process**: Identify purpose → Identify perspective → Analyze how each shapes content → Evaluate effectiveness

**Essential Question**: "Why did the author write this, what do they believe, and how do those factors shape the content?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students confuse purpose (inform/persuade) with topic (what it's about) OR they identify perspective but don't analyze how it shapes the text

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "How does knowing the purpose/perspective help us understand the author's choices?" and "What did the author include/exclude because of their perspective?"

## Feedback Checkpoints

### Day 4 Checkpoint (Mid-Cycle Formative)

**What to assess:**
- Author's purpose identified (inform, persuade, entertain, explain)
- Author's perspective/bias analyzed
- Evidence showing purpose/perspective cited
- Effect on audience considered

**Sample checkpoint prompt:**
"Based on the text we've analyzed, identify the author's purpose and perspective. Cite evidence showing how each shapes the content."

**Look for in student responses:**
- Correct identification of purpose (not just topic)
- Recognition of author's viewpoint or bias
- Specific textual evidence for both purpose and perspective
- Connection between purpose/perspective and author's choices
- Consideration of how these affect readers

**Red flags (address immediately):**
- Confusing purpose with topic ("The purpose is discrimination" vs. "The purpose is to persuade readers to end discrimination")
- Identifying only purpose OR perspective (both required)
- Generic statements without evidence
- Listing facts without showing how purpose/perspective shapes them
- Missing the connection between historical context and perspective

**Day 4 feedback focus:**
- "You identified the topic, but what is the author trying to accomplish? Inform? Persuade?"
- "What does the author believe about this topic? What's their viewpoint?"
- "Show me where in the text you see evidence of this purpose/perspective."
- "How does knowing the author's purpose help explain their choices?"

### Day 6 Checkpoint (Pre-Assessment Check)

**What quality responses include:**
- Claims about BOTH author's purpose AND perspective (not just one)
- Purpose specified beyond generic label (not just "to inform" but "to inform readers about the urgent need for...")
- Author's perspective/bias clearly identified with evidence
- Analysis of HOW purpose and perspective shape content choices
- Evidence showing influence on message, not just facts listed
- Effect on audience considered

**Common quality issues to address:**

**Issue 1: Generic or missing purpose**
- Student writes: "The author's purpose is to inform."
- Why it's weak: Too vague; doesn't specify what or why
- Revision focus: "Inform readers about WHAT specifically? Why did they write this?"

**Issue 2: Doesn't identify perspective/bias**
- Student writes: "The author's purpose is to persuade people to support civil rights."
- Why it's weak: Identifies purpose but not perspective
- Revision focus: "What is the author's viewpoint or bias? What do THEY believe about this issue?"

**Issue 3: Lists evidence without showing influence**
- Student writes: "The author uses statistics. The author includes personal stories. The author uses emotional language."
- Why it's weak: Describes what's there but doesn't analyze WHY or how it connects to purpose/perspective
- Revision focus: "How does the author's purpose EXPLAIN why they chose these techniques? What does this evidence reveal about their perspective?"

**Issue 4: Confuses purpose with topic**
- Student writes: "The author's purpose is immigration" or "The author's purpose is the Civil War."
- Why it's weak: States subject, not intent (inform/persuade/entertain)
- Revision focus: "That's the topic. Why did the author write about it? To inform? To persuade? What did they want readers to think or do?"

**Issue 5: No analysis of how purpose/perspective shape content**
- Student writes: "The author's purpose is to persuade and their perspective is that voting rights are important. They use quotes and facts."
- Why it's weak: Identifies purpose and perspective but doesn't connect them to author's choices
- Revision focus: "HOW does wanting to persuade shape what the author includes? What does their perspective about voting rights explain about their emphasis or tone?"

**Teacher focus targets for Day 6:**
- Specify purpose beyond generic label (what specifically? why?)
- Identify author's perspective/bias (what do they believe?)
- Show HOW purpose shapes message (not just what's included, but why)
- Analyze effect on audience (how would readers respond?)
- Connect historical context to perspective (how does when/where they lived influence their viewpoint?)

**Revision sentence stems:**
- "The author's purpose is to [inform/persuade] readers that _______ because _______."
- "The author's perspective is _______, which is evident when _______."
- "Because the author wants to _______, they choose to _______."
- "The author emphasizes _______ and omits _______, which reveals their bias toward _______."
- "Writing during _______, the author's perspective reflects _______."
