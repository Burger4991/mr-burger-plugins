# ELA.10.R.1.1 - Literary Elements (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.1.1**: Analyze how character development, setting, and plot interact and contribute to the overall meaning.

## Benchmark Clarifications
Literary elements do not exist in isolation. This benchmark requires students to analyze the **interaction** between elements and their **contribution to meaning**.

**Key Focus:**
- **Character development**: How characters change or grow
- **Setting**: Time, place, social context (not just backdrop)
- **Plot**: Events and their sequence
- **Interaction**: How these elements affect each other
- **Meaning**: Theme, message, or central idea

**NOT just identification:**
- "The character is brave" ❌
- "The setting is a forest" ❌
- "The plot has conflict" ❌

**Analysis with interaction:**
- "The hostile setting forces the character to develop courage" ✓
- "The character's decision creates the central conflict" ✓
- "The historical setting shapes the character's limited choices" ✓

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

Use this process to guide students through literary element analysis:

### Step 1: Identify the Literary Elements
**Character**: Who are the main characters? What do we learn about them?
- Physical traits, personality, beliefs
- Relationships with other characters
- Internal conflicts or motivations

**Setting**: When and where does this take place?
- Historical period or time frame
- Geographic location
- Social/cultural context
- Mood or atmosphere created

**Plot**: What are the key events?
- Exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution
- Major conflicts (internal/external)
- Turning points

**Organizer prompt**: "What are the key character traits, setting details, and plot events?"

### Step 2: Analyze How Elements Interact
Look for connections:
- **Character → Plot**: How do character traits/decisions cause events?
- **Setting → Character**: How does setting shape character development?
- **Plot → Character**: How do events change the character?
- **Setting → Plot**: How does setting create or limit conflict?

**Organizer prompt**: "How do the character, setting, and plot affect each other?"

### Step 3: Trace Character Development
Track changes throughout the text:
- What does the character believe/value at the beginning?
- What challenges or events occur?
- How does the character respond?
- What has changed by the end?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the character change from beginning to end?"

### Step 4: Connect Setting to Meaning
Analyze setting's role beyond backdrop:
- Does setting create obstacles or opportunities?
- Does historical context limit choices?
- Does setting reflect character's internal state?
- Does setting symbolize larger themes?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the setting contribute to the text's meaning?"

### Step 5: Determine Contribution to Overall Meaning
Synthesize the analysis:
- What message or theme emerges?
- How do character, setting, and plot work together to convey this?
- What would change if any element were different?

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the overall meaning, and how do the literary elements create it?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Identification Questions
- What are the key character traits of [character]?
- Describe the setting. When and where does this take place?
- What are the main events in the plot?

### Interaction Questions
- How does [character]'s development reveal [theme/meaning]?
- Analyze the relationship between setting and [conflict/character/plot].
- How do [character's] actions drive the plot forward?
- What role does setting play in the conflict?

### Meaning Questions
- Explain how [plot event] contributes to the text's overall meaning.
- How do character, setting, and plot work together to convey [theme]?
- What would change if [element] were different?

### Development Questions
- How does [character] change throughout the text?
- What causes this change?
- How does the setting influence character development?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Analyzes complex interactions between multiple elements
- Evaluates how changes to one element would affect meaning
- Makes sophisticated connections between elements and theme
- Provides nuanced textual evidence

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Analyzes how character, setting, and plot interact
- Explains contribution to overall meaning with evidence
- Traces character development with specific support
- Connects interactions to theme or message

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies character traits, setting details, plot events
- Makes simple connections between elements
- Notes character changes with limited analysis
- Provides some evidence

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Lists elements without analysis
- Difficulty explaining interactions
- Minimal or no connection to meaning
- Limited textual support

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Literary texts (fiction, drama, narrative poetry)
- Texts with clear character development
- Meaningful setting (not generic backdrop)
- Plot events that interact with character/setting
- Complex enough for analysis of interaction

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Informational texts (use R.2.x standards instead)
- Static characters with no development
- Generic settings with no meaningful role
- Plot-only focus without character/setting interaction

## Common Literary Elements Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- interact (affect each other)
- contribute (add to, help create)
- develop (change or grow)
- reveal (show or make clear)
- influence (affect or shape)
- motivate (cause to act)
- reflect (show or mirror)
- shape (form or determine)
- catalyst (something that causes change)
- dynamic (changing)

**Literary Terms:**
- protagonist (main character)
- antagonist (opposing character/force)
- conflict (struggle: internal/external)
- climax (turning point)
- resolution (outcome)
- atmosphere (mood created by setting)
- exposition (introduction/background)
- foreshadowing (hint at future events)

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For "The Most Dangerous Game" by Richard Connell:**

| Literary Element | Key Details | How It Interacts with Other Elements | Contribution to Meaning |
|------------------|-------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Character** | Rainsford begins as a confident hunter who believes "the world is made up of two classes—the hunters and the huntees" (paragraph 1). | His arrogance makes him dismissive of prey's fear, which sets up irony when he becomes hunted. The island setting forces him to experience what his victims felt. | His transformation from hunter to hunted (and back) reveals the theme: experiencing another's perspective changes understanding. His survival depends on using both hunter skills and prey instincts. |
| **Setting** | Ship-Trap Island is isolated, with jungle terrain that provides both danger and hiding places. Zaroff's mansion represents civilization, but houses barbarism. | The jungle setting creates life-or-death stakes that force Rainsford's development. The isolation means no escape—he must face this challenge. The contrast between mansion (civilized) and hunt (savage) emphasizes moral conflict. | The setting serves as a physical manifestation of moral wilderness. The island's isolation makes the ethical question unavoidable: what separates civilized humans from animals? |
| **Plot** | Rainsford falls off the yacht (inciting incident), meets Zaroff, discovers the hunt, becomes prey, survives three days, confronts Zaroff in final battle. | Each plot event escalates stakes and forces character growth. The hunt reverses Rainsford's role, making him experience his prey's terror. His survival requires adapting to the setting's dangers. | The plot structure mirrors the hunter/hunted reversal. The circular ending (Rainsford in Zaroff's bed) suggests he's become what he fought against, complicating the "victory." |

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Analyze how character development, setting, and plot interact and contribute to overall meaning

**Key Process**: Identify elements → Analyze interactions → Trace development → Connect to meaning

**Essential Question**: "How do character, setting, and plot work together to create the text's meaning?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students identify elements but don't analyze how they **interact** or **contribute to meaning**

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "How does this element affect the others?" and "So what? What does this reveal?"
