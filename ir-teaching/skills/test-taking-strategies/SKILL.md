# Test-Taking Strategies for Intensive Reading

## PURPOSE

Test-taking is a **learnable skill**, not innate ability. This document provides systematic instruction in strategies that directly improve test performance on Florida BEST ELA assessments.

**Core Principle:** Students need explicit instruction in HOW to approach tests, not just WHAT content to know.

---

## THE 4 CORE TEST-TAKING STRATEGIES

| Strategy | Use For | Skill Level | When Taught |
|----------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **STOP Protocol** | Multiple Choice Questions | DOK 1-3 | Daily (bellringers) + Explicit (Day 5) |
| **CER Framework** | Constructed Response (analytical) | DOK 3-4 | Days 3-6 (writing instruction) |
| **RACE Framework** | Constructed Response (short answer) | DOK 2-3 | Days 3-6 (alternative to CER) |
| **Prompt Analysis** | All question types | DOK 2-4 | Days 5-6 (test prep) |

## STRATEGY 1: STOP PROTOCOL (Multiple Choice)

### What Is STOP?

**S**illy - Obviously incorrect, easy to eliminate
**T**ricky - Sounds partially correct but has a fatal flaw
**O**pposite - States the reverse of the correct answer
**P**roven - Correct answer that can be proven with text evidence

**Purpose:** Systematize the process of elimination to improve MC accuracy

---

### Year-Long Progression

**Units 1-2 (September-October): Exposure**
- Students see STOP format in bellringers daily
- Teacher models STOP thinking occasionally
- No formal assessment of STOP yet
- Goal: Familiarity with S/T/O/P labels

**Units 3-4 (November-December): Explicit Instruction**
- Day 5 of each unit: 15-minute STOP lesson
- Teacher models full think-aloud for 3-5 questions
- Students practice with justification sheets
- Goal: Can identify all 4 types and justify choices

**Units 5-6 (January-February): Independent Practice**
- Students use STOP independently on assessments
- Bellringer review includes "Which type was choice B?"
- Track accuracy data: Are students improving?
- Goal: STOP becomes automatic habit

---

### Day-by-Day Integration

**Days 1-4: Informal Exposure**
- Bellringers use STOP-formatted answer choices daily
- Review mentions STOP occasionally: "Choice A was Silly because..."
- No formal instruction yet
- Students build pattern recognition

**Day 5: Explicit Instruction (15 minutes Teacher-Led)**

**Step 1: Teach STOP (5 min)**
```
Teacher: "Today I'm going to teach you a strategy that will help you eliminate wrong answers faster. It's called STOP."

[Project poster with definitions]

S = Silly - Obviously wrong, contradicts the text
T = Tricky - Sounds good but has a mistake or misses key info
O = Opposite - Says the reverse of what's true
P = Proven - Correct! You can prove it with evidence from the text

"Every MC question has these 4 types. Your job is to find the P and eliminate S, T, O."
```

**Step 2: Model Think-Aloud (5 min)**
```
[Project MC question on board]

Teacher: "Watch me think through this question out loud.

First, I read the question carefully. It asks [restate question].

Now I look at choice A: [read aloud]. Hmm, this says [paraphrase]. But in paragraph 3, the text says [quote evidence]. So A contradicts the text. I'm labeling this S for Silly. [Cross out A]

Choice B: [read aloud]. This sounds partially true because [explain]. But wait - it says [identify flaw]. The text actually shows [correct info]. This is close but wrong. That's T for Tricky. [Cross out B]

Choice C: [read aloud]. This says [paraphrase]. But the text says the opposite: [quote]. This is O for Opposite. [Cross out C]

Choice D: [read aloud]. Let me check - does the text support this? Yes! In paragraph [#], it says [quote evidence]. This matches choice D perfectly. This is P for Proven. [Circle D]

My answer is D, and I can prove it with paragraph [#]."
```

**Step 3: Guided Practice (5 min)**
```
[Project second MC question]

Teacher: "Now let's try one together. Turn to your partner. For each choice, label it S, T, O, or P. You have 2 minutes."

[Students work in pairs]

Teacher: "Let's share. Who thinks choice A is Silly? Why?"
[Call on students, confirm/correct reasoning]

"Who thinks B is Tricky? What's the flaw?"
[Discuss]

"Which is Proven? How do you know?"
[Confirm answer with evidence]
```

**Day 5: Independent Practice (20 min Independent Rotation)**
- Students complete 5-10 MC questions with STOP justification sheets
- Format:
  ```
  Question 1: [Question text]
  My answer: ____

  A) [S/T/O/P] - Why: _______________________
  B) [S/T/O/P] - Why: _______________________
  C) [S/T/O/P] - Why: _______________________
  D) [S/T/O/P] - Why: _______________________
  ```
- Collect for formative data: Are students accurately identifying types?

**Day 6: Application**
- Students use STOP on mini-assessment (no justification sheet needed)
- STOP is now internalized strategy

---

### STOP Justification Sheet Template

```
Name: _________________ Date: ________ Period: ___

STOP PRACTICE - [Unit Name]

Directions: Answer each question. Then label each choice as S (Silly), T (Tricky), O (Opposite), or P (Proven). Explain WHY in 1 sentence.

---

QUESTION 1: [MC Question Text]

My Answer: _____

A) [Choice A text]
   Label: S / T / O / P (circle one)
   Why: _________________________________________________

B) [Choice B text]
   Label: S / T / O / P (circle one)
   Why: _________________________________________________

C) [Choice C text]
   Label: S / T / O / P (circle one)
   Why: _________________________________________________

D) [Choice D text]
   Label: S / T / O / P (circle one)
   Why: _________________________________________________

Text Evidence for Proven Answer:
"_______________________________________________________" (¶___)

---

[Repeat for questions 2-5]
```

---

### Writing STOP-Formatted MC Questions

When creating assessments, intentionally design each question with S/T/O/P choices:

**Example (Theme Question):**
```
What is a theme of this passage?

A) New York City is a dangerous place. [S - Too literal, topic not theme]
B) People should never trust others. [O - Opposite of text's message]
C) Difficult experiences can lead to personal growth. [P - PROVEN - supported throughout]
D) The main character learns to cook. [T - Tricky - character does cook, but that's not the theme]
```

**Anatomy:**
- **P (Correct Answer):** Clearly supported by text, universal theme statement
- **S (Silly):** Confuses theme with topic, too literal
- **T (Tricky):** References something in the text but misses the deeper meaning
- **O (Opposite):** States reverse of actual theme

## STRATEGY 2: CER FRAMEWORK (Constructed Response - Analytical)

### What Is CER?

**C**laim - Your position or interpretation (answers the prompt)
**E**vidence - Specific facts, quotes, or data from text with citation
**R**easoning - Explains HOW evidence supports claim

**Purpose:** Structure analytical writing to ensure complete, logical responses

**Best For:** DOK 3-4 questions (analyze, evaluate, compare)

---

### CER Paragraph Structure

```
CLAIM (Sentence 1):
[Topic sentence that directly answers the prompt using key words from question]

EVIDENCE (Sentences 2-3):
[Introduce evidence + quote with paragraph citation]

REASONING (Sentences 4-5):
[Explain HOW/WHY evidence proves your claim]
[Make the connection explicit - don't assume reader sees it]

Optional EVIDENCE 2 + REASONING 2 (Sentences 6-8):
[Second piece of evidence + explanation if needed for complete answer]

CONCLUSION (Final Sentence):
[Restate claim in new words OR connect to bigger idea]
```

---

### CER Instruction Progression

**Days 1-2: Introduce CER (Teacher-Led)**
```
Teacher: "When you write about texts, you need 3 things: a Claim, Evidence, and Reasoning. Let me show you what each one looks like."

[Project CER poster]

C = Claim: Your answer to the question (What do you think?)
E = Evidence: Proof from the text (quotes, paragraph numbers)
R = Reasoning: Explain WHY the evidence supports your claim (Connect the dots!)

"Here's an example..."
[Show complete CER paragraph, label each part with C/E/R]
```

**Day 3: Model CER (Teacher-Led I Do)**
```
Teacher: "Watch me write a CER paragraph for this prompt."

Prompt: How does the author's use of imagery affect the reader?

[Think-aloud while writing on board]

Claim: "The author's use of vivid imagery helps readers visualize the setting and feel immersed in the story."

[Teacher: "Notice I answered the question directly and used the key words 'imagery' and 'affect the reader.' That's my claim."]

Evidence: "For example, the author describes the forest as 'a thick tangle of emerald vines and moss-covered stones, where sunlight barely penetrated the canopy' (paragraph 4)."

[Teacher: "I used a quote and cited the paragraph. That's my evidence."]

Reasoning: "This imagery creates a vivid mental picture for readers and establishes a mysterious, isolated mood. By describing the forest as dark and tangled, the author makes readers feel the same sense of being lost that the character experiences."

[Teacher: "I explained HOW the imagery affects readers - it creates a picture and a mood. That's my reasoning. I connected the evidence back to my claim."]
```

**Day 3-4: Guided Practice (We Do / You Do w/ Partner)**
- Students write CER paragraphs with sentence frames
- Sentence frames (Day 3 only, remove Day 4):
  ```
  C: The author uses _______ to show _______.
  E: For example, in paragraph ___, the text states "_______."
  R: This shows _______ because _______.
  ```

**Day 5: Timed CER (Teacher-Led modeling)**
```
Teacher: "On tests, you have limited time. Let me show you how to write a CER paragraph in 8-10 minutes."

[Set timer, write in real-time, narrate process]

Minutes 1-2: Read prompt, underline key words, plan claim
Minutes 3-5: Write claim + evidence (find quote, cite paragraph)
Minutes 6-8: Write reasoning (explain connection)
Minutes 9-10: Reread, check for errors, add conclusion if time
```

**Day 5: Independent Timed Practice (Independent Rotation)**
- Students write CER paragraph in 10 minutes (authentic test conditions)
- Use Self-Assessment Checklist after:
  ```
  ☐ Claim directly answers prompt
  ☐ Evidence includes quote + paragraph number
  ☐ Reasoning explains HOW evidence supports claim
  ☐ Complete sentences, correct punctuation
  ```

**Day 6: Assessment**
- CER paragraph on mini-assessment (summative)

---

### CER Sentence Starters (Scaffolds)

**For Claim:**
- "The author uses _______ to demonstrate _______."
- "A central theme in this text is _______."
- "The author's purpose is to _______ by _______."
- "[Character name]'s actions reveal _______."

**For Evidence:**
- "For example, in paragraph ___, the text states '_______'."
- "The author writes, '_______' (¶___)."
- "This is evident when the text says '_______' (¶___)."

**For Reasoning:**
- "This shows _______ because _______."
- "This demonstrates _______ by _______."
- "This reveals _______, which proves _______."
- "The evidence supports the claim that _______ because _______."

**Remove scaffolds by Day 5** (assessment conditions)

## STRATEGY 3: RACE FRAMEWORK (Constructed Response - Short Answer)

### What Is RACE?

**R**estate - Turn question into a statement
**A**nswer - Provide clear, direct answer
**C**ite - Use quotation marks + paragraph number
**E**xplain - Connect evidence back to answer

**Purpose:** Simpler structure for shorter CR questions (DOK 2-3)

**Difference from CER:**
- RACE: More formulaic, works for simpler questions
- CER: More flexible, works for complex analysis

**Use RACE when:** Question asks for definition, identification, or straightforward analysis
**Use CER when:** Question asks for deeper analysis, evaluation, or comparison

---

### RACE Paragraph Structure

```
RESTATE + ANSWER (Sentence 1):
[Turn question into statement + answer it]

CITE (Sentence 2):
[Quote from text with paragraph number]

EXPLAIN (Sentences 3-4):
[Connect evidence to answer - "This shows..." or "This demonstrates..."]
```

---

### RACE Example

**Prompt:** What is the author's purpose in this passage?

**RACE Response:**
```
R+A: The author's purpose in this passage is to persuade readers that education should be a national priority.

C: The author states, "Education is the key to our future, and we must invest in it now" (paragraph 3).

E: This shows the author's persuasive intent because the phrase "we must invest" is a call to action. By emphasizing the urgency ("now"), the author tries to convince readers to support education funding.
```

---

### When to Use CER vs RACE

| Question Type | Framework | Why? |
|---------------|-----------|------|
| "What is the theme?" | RACE | Straightforward identification + support |
| "How does theme develop across the text?" | CER | Requires analysis of development (more complex) |
| "What is the author's purpose?" | RACE | Identification question |
| "How does the author's use of rhetoric achieve the purpose?" | CER | Requires analytical reasoning about technique |
| "Describe the character" | RACE | Description task |
| "How do two characters interact and what does it reveal?" | CER | Requires analysis of interaction + significance |

**Rule of Thumb:**
- Simple question (identify, describe) = RACE
- Complex question (analyze how, evaluate, compare) = CER

## STRATEGY 4: PROMPT ANALYSIS

### What Is Prompt Analysis?

**Purpose:** Break down test questions to understand EXACTLY what's being asked before answering

**Skill:** Identifying key verbs, question focus, and answer requirements

---

### Prompt Analysis Protocol

**Step 1: Underline the Question Verb**
- Identify what the question is asking you to DO
- Common verbs: analyze, explain, compare, describe, evaluate, identify, support

**Step 2: Circle the Question Focus**
- What specifically should you write about?
- Example: "How does the author use **imagery** to create **mood**?"
- Focus: imagery + mood (must address BOTH)

**Step 3: Box the Text Reference**
- Which part of the text? Whole passage? Specific paragraphs?
- Example: "...in **paragraphs 3-5**" or "**throughout the passage**"

**Step 4: Check Answer Requirements**
- How many examples needed? ("at least two pieces of evidence")
- Format? (paragraph, list, chart)

---

### Prompt Analysis Examples

**Example 1:**
"**Analyze** how the **author's use of figurative language** contributes to the **tone** of **the passage**."

- Verb: Analyze (explain HOW something works)
- Focus: figurative language + tone (must connect these two things)
- Text reference: the passage (whole text)
- Requirements: Explain connection between device and tone

**Example 2:**
"**Identify** a **theme** in the story and **explain** how it **develops** from **beginning to end**."

- Verb: Identify + Explain (two-part question)
- Focus: theme + its development (must show change over time)
- Text reference: beginning to end (need evidence from multiple points)
- Requirements: Name theme + trace development arc

---

### Teaching Prompt Analysis (Day 5 Teacher-Led)

**Activity: Prompt Surgery (10 minutes)**

```
Teacher: "Before you answer a question, you need to dissect it like a surgeon. Let's practice."

[Project prompt on board]

"First, underline the verb. What is this question asking you to DO?"
[Students identify: analyze, explain, compare, etc.]

"Next, circle the focus. What should you write about?"
[Students identify topic/concept]

"Box any text references. Which part of the text?"
[Students identify: whole passage, specific paragraphs, etc.]

"Finally, check requirements. How much evidence? What format?"
[Students note requirements]

"Now you know EXACTLY what to write. If your answer doesn't hit all these points, it's incomplete."
```

**Practice:** Students analyze 3-5 prompts, dissect each one, compare with partner

## TIME MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES

### The Test-Taking Clock

**For 90-Minute Assessment:**
- Multiple Choice (20-25 questions): 30-40 minutes (1.5 min per question)
- Constructed Response (2-3 questions): 30-40 minutes (10-15 min per question)
- Review & Check: 10-15 minutes

**Teach Students:**
- Look at total questions first
- Calculate time per question
- Skip hard questions, return later
- Save time for review

---

### Pacing Practice (Day 5 Independent Rotation)

**Activity: Timed Sections**
```
Round 1: MC Sprint (10 minutes)
- Answer 7 MC questions in 10 minutes
- Goal: 1.5 minutes per question

Round 2: CR Timed Write (10 minutes)
- Write one CER paragraph in 10 minutes
- Goal: Complete C+E+R in time limit

Round 3: Review (5 minutes)
- Go back, check answers
- Fix any incomplete responses
```

**Debrief:**
- "Did you finish in time?"
- "What slowed you down?"
- "What will you do differently next time?"

## YEAR-LONG TEST-TAKING PROGRESSION

### September-October (Units 1-2): Build Foundations
- **Bellringers:** Daily STOP exposure (no formal instruction yet)
- **Writing:** Introduce CER framework (model + guided practice)
- **Focus:** Content mastery, not test-taking yet

### November-December (Units 3-4): Explicit Strategy Instruction
- **Day 5 each unit:** 15 min STOP lesson (explicit instruction + practice)
- **Days 3-4:** CER with sentence frames (scaffolded)
- **Day 5:** Timed writing practice (build stamina)
- **Focus:** Learn strategies with support

### January-March (Units 5-7): Independent Application
- **Day 5:** STOP review (quick reminder, not full lesson)
- **Days 3-4:** CER without sentence frames (remove scaffolds)
- **Day 5-6:** Timed practice under test conditions
- **Focus:** Use strategies independently

### April-May (Units 8-9 + Test Prep): Assessment Conditions
- **Full practice tests:** 90 minutes, authentic format
- **Data analysis:** Which strategies are students using? Which need reteaching?
- **Small group intervention:** Pull students struggling with specific strategies
- **Focus:** Stamina, confidence, strategy application

## INTEGRATION WITH DAILY STRUCTURE

### Bellringers (Every Day): STOP Exposure
- 5 MC context clue questions
- Answer choices formatted using STOP
- Review mentions STOP types occasionally
- Builds pattern recognition over time

### Teacher-Led (Days 1-4): Writing Strategies
- Days 1-2: Model CER examples (I Do rows of organizer)
- Days 3-4: Guided CER practice (We Do, You Do w/ Partner)
- Students see CER structure repeatedly before Day 5

### Teacher-Led (Day 5): Explicit Test-Taking Instruction
- 15 min: STOP protocol lesson (Units 3-4 only, then review)
- 10 min: Timed CER modeling (every unit)
- Students see test-taking AS A SKILL, not a mystery

### Independent (Day 5): Strategy Practice
- 10 min: STOP practice with justification sheets
- 10 min: Timed CER writing
- 5 min: Self-assessment with checklist

### Independent (Day 6): Assessment
- Use all strategies under test conditions
- Formative data: Track strategy use and accuracy

## TROUBLESHOOTING

### "Students aren't using STOP even though we taught it"

**Solutions:**
- Increase accountability: Day 5 justification sheets (must label every choice)
- Review in bellringers: "Who labeled choice A as Silly? Why?"
- Model more often: Day 1-4 bellringer reviews, teacher thinks aloud
- Check understanding: Can students define S/T/O/P? Reteach if needed

### "CER paragraphs are missing Reasoning"

**Solutions:**
- Model reasoning explicitly: "The evidence shows _______ because _______"
- Sentence frames: "This demonstrates _______, which proves _______"
- Check for understanding: "What's the difference between Evidence and Reasoning?" (E = quote, R = explanation of quote)
- Highlight exemplars: Show strong reasoning examples, annotate what makes it good

### "Students run out of time on assessments"

**Solutions:**
- Timed practice earlier in unit (not just Day 5)
- Teach skipping strategy: "If stuck, move on and return later"
- Set mini-goals: "Finish MC in 30 minutes" (check clock)
- Reduce perfectionism: "Good enough is better than incomplete"
