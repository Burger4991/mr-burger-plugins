# ENGAGEMENT PROTOCOLS
**Collaborative, Active Learning Strategies for IR Classrooms**

---

## PURPOSE

These protocols replace passive worksheet activities with collaborative, discussion-based learning that builds literacy skills while increasing engagement. Each protocol:
- Requires active participation (can't fake it)
- Builds specific literacy skills (speaking, listening, collaboration, analysis)
- Fits within 20-minute rotation blocks
- Works with 3-rotation structure (Teacher-Led, Independent, Technology)
- Includes scaffolds for struggling students

**Key Principle:** Learning should be social and active, not silent and isolated.

---

## PROTOCOL SELECTION GUIDE

### When to Use Each Protocol

| Protocol | Duration | Best For | Day in Cycle |
|----------|:--------:|----------|:------------:|
| **Think-Pair-Share** | 5-10 min | Quick discussions, activating knowledge | Any day |
| **Socratic Seminar** | 20-30 min | Deep text analysis, critical thinking | Days 2, 4, 6 |
| **Gallery Walk** | 20-25 min | Visual learning, peer feedback | Days 3-4 |
| **Annotation Stations** | 20-25 min | Close reading, multiple text passes | Days 1-2 |
| **Speed Dating Review** | 15-20 min | Revision, peer feedback | Days 4-5 |
| **Kahoot/Quizizz** | 10-15 min | Formative assessment, competitive review | Days 5-6 |

## PROTOCOL 1: THINK-PAIR-SHARE

### Overview
**Time:** 5-10 minutes
**Grouping:** Partners
**Literacy Skills:** Speaking, listening, textual evidence
**Best Used:** Daily, any rotation

### Structure

**STEP 1: THINK (2 minutes) - Silent Individual Work**

**Teacher Actions:**
- Pose complex question (write on board)
- Set visible timer for 2 minutes
- Play soft music (optional)
- Monitor: All students writing

**Student Actions:**
- Write individual response in notebook
- Must write 3-4 sentences minimum
- Use text evidence if applicable
- Work silently (no talking)

**Question Examples by Benchmark:**
- Theme: "What theme is revealed in this scene? How do you know?"
- Purpose: "What is Obama's main purpose in this speech? Find evidence."
- Rhetoric: "How does the author use rhetoric to persuade the audience?"
- Argument: "Do you agree with the author's argument? Why or why not?"

---

**STEP 2: PAIR (3-5 minutes) - Partner Discussion**

**Teacher Actions:**
- "Time! Turn to your partner."
- Assign roles: Partner A speaks first (1-2 min), then Partner B
- Set timer
- Circulate, listen to conversations
- Note misconceptions for whole-class discussion

**Student Actions:**

*Partner A (1-2 minutes):*
- Share response with Partner B
- Read from notebook or explain thinking
- Partner B listens actively (no interrupting)

*Partner B (1-2 minutes):*
- Share response with Partner A
- Partner A listens actively

*Both Partners (1 minute):*
- Discuss together:
  - "What did we agree on?"
  - "What did we disagree on?"
  - "What evidence supports our thinking?"

**Sentence Frames for Support:**
- "I agree with you because..."
- "I disagree because..."
- "My evidence is... What's yours?"
- "Can you explain what you meant by...?"

---

**STEP 3: SHARE (2-3 minutes) - Whole Class Discussion**

**Teacher Actions:**
- Call on 3-5 pairs randomly
- Ask: "What did you and your partner conclude?"
- Chart responses on board
- Push thinking: "Why?" "How do you know?" "What evidence?"
- Address misconceptions

**Student Actions:**
- Raise hands to share
- Report on BOTH perspectives: "My partner said... and I said..."
- Listen to other pairs
- Add new ideas to notes

---

### Modifications

**For Struggling Students:**
- Provide sentence frames to complete
- Give word bank of key terms
- Allow drawing instead of writing
- Pair with stronger partner

**For Advanced Students:**
- Add complexity (DOK 3-4 questions)
- Require multiple evidence pieces
- Make cross-text connections
- Find counterclaims

**For ELL Students:**
- Preview question day before
- Allow native language discussion first
- Provide vocabulary support
- Extended think time (3-4 min)

**For Behavior Issues:**
- Assign partners strategically
- Use timer strictly
- Proximity during PAIR phase
- Reinforce: "Partner A talks, Partner B listens"

---

### Materials Needed
- Question prompt (on board)
- Timer (visible to students)
- Sentence frames poster (optional)
- Student notebooks

## PROTOCOL 2: SOCRATIC SEMINAR CIRCLES

### Overview
**Time:** 20-30 minutes
**Grouping:** Inner circle (8-10 discussants) + Outer circle (observers)
**Literacy Skills:** Speaking, listening, critical thinking, citing evidence
**Best Used:** Days 2, 4, or 6 (after text reading)

### Structure

**Setup (2 minutes):**
- Arrange desks in two circles (inner and outer)
- Inner circle: 8-10 students (discussants)
- Outer circle: Rest of class (observers/note-takers)
- Teacher: Facilitator (not lecturer)

---

**Phase 1: Opening Question (5 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- Pose complex, open-ended question
- Examples:
  - "Is Obama's rhetoric effective? Why or why not?"
  - "Does the author's purpose change throughout the text?"
  - "What is the deeper meaning of this theme?"
- Step back, let students lead

**Inner Circle Actions:**
- Respond to question (not to teacher - to each other)
- MUST cite text evidence
- MUST respond to previous speaker
- Build on each other's ideas

**Outer Circle Actions:**
- Take notes on discussion
- Track who speaks
- Note strong evidence cited
- Prepare to share observations

**Rules:**
- Cite text for every claim
- Respond to previous speaker before adding new idea
- Respectful disagreement encouraged
- No hand-raising (natural flow)

---

**Phase 2: Student-Led Discussion (15-20 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- Minimal intervention (only if discussion stalls)
- Prompt cards if needed:
  - "Can someone provide evidence for that claim?"
  - "Does anyone have a different perspective?"
  - "How does this connect to what [name] said?"
- Take notes on student thinking

**Inner Circle: Discussion Roles**

Assign roles for structured participation:

1. **Discussion Leader** (1 student)
   - Keeps conversation moving
   - Calls on quiet students
   - Bridges ideas together

2. **Text Tracker** (1 student)
   - Writes all evidence cited on board
   - Tracks paragraph numbers
   - Notes gaps in coverage

3. **Devil's Advocate** (1-2 students)
   - Challenges ideas respectfully
   - Asks "What about...?" questions
   - Pushes deeper thinking

4. **Connector** (1-2 students)
   - Links ideas from different students
   - Synthesizes themes
   - Makes cross-text connections

5. **Participants** (Rest of inner circle)
   - Share ideas
   - Respond to each other
   - Cite evidence

**Outer Circle: Observation Tasks**
- Track discussion on graphic organizer
- Count speaking turns (is participation equal?)
- Note strongest evidence cited
- Identify unanswered questions

---

**Phase 3: Debrief (5 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- "Outer circle, what did you observe?"
- "What strong evidence did you hear?"
- "What questions are still unanswered?"
- Summarize key ideas from discussion

**Outer Circle Shares:**
- Observations about discussion quality
- Strong moments
- Areas for improvement

**Inner Circle Reflects:**
- "What went well?"
- "What was challenging?"

**Optional: Switch Circles**
- Halfway through, inner and outer switch
- Everyone gets chance to discuss

---

### Scaffolds for Struggling Students

**Talking Chips System:**
- Each student gets 2 chips (poker chips, colored paper)
- Must use both chips during discussion (place in center when speaking)
- Ensures equal participation
- Once chips are gone, can't speak again until everyone uses theirs

**Sentence Frames:**
- "I agree with [name] because..."
- "I disagree with [name] because..."
- "The text says [quote], which shows..."
- "I want to build on what [name] said..."
- "I have a different perspective..."

**Pre-Discussion Prep:**
- Give questions in advance
- Students write responses first
- Bring notes to discussion
- Can read from notes if needed

---

### Materials Needed
- Desks arranged in circles (or sit on floor)
- Discussion questions (3-5 open-ended)
- Observation sheets for outer circle
- Sentence frame posters
- Talking chips (optional)
- Text (students need access during discussion)

**Assessment:**
- Participation rubric (quality of contributions)
- Outer circle observation notes (formative data)

## PROTOCOL 3: GALLERY WALK

### Overview
**Time:** 20-25 minutes
**Grouping:** Small groups (3-4 students)
**Literacy Skills:** Collaboration, visual literacy, peer feedback
**Best Used:** Days 3-4 (organizer building, visual analysis)

### Structure

**Phase 1: Create (10-12 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- Divide class into groups of 3-4
- Assign each group a task (see examples below)
- Distribute chart paper + markers
- Set timer: "12 minutes to create your visual"
- Circulate, support groups

**Student Actions:**
- Work collaboratively to create visual on chart paper
- Must include:
  - Text evidence (quotes, paragraph numbers)
  - Labels and explanations
  - Visuals (thinking maps, diagrams, illustrations)
  - Group member names
- Assign roles:
  - Recorder (writes on chart)
  - Reader (finds evidence in text)
  - Designer (organizes visual layout)
  - Timekeeper (watches clock)

**Task Examples:**

*Days 1-2 (Theme/Purpose):*
- "Create thinking map showing author's purpose with 5 evidence examples"
- "Design poster analyzing how theme develops across the text"

*Days 3-4 (Rhetoric/Argument):*
- "Find 5 rhetorical devices - create visual explaining each"
- "Map the author's argument structure (claim, evidence, reasoning)"

*Days 5-6 (Synthesis):*
- "Compare two texts - Venn diagram with evidence"
- "Create visual timeline of how author's ideas develop"

---

**Phase 2: Gallery Walk (8-10 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- "Post your charts around the room"
- Give each student sticky notes (3 colors)
- Explain feedback system
- Set timer: "8 minutes to view all charts"
- Play music (optional)

**Student Actions:**
- Rotate around room, view all charts
- Give feedback using sticky notes:
  - Green = "Strong evidence" or "Great analysis"
  - Yellow = "Question: Why did you...?" or "Can you explain...?"
  - Red = "This could be stronger if..." or "Consider adding..."
- Leave 2-3 sticky notes per chart
- Read feedback on other charts

**Feedback Sentence Frames:**
- Green: "Your evidence from paragraph ___ is really strong because..."
- Yellow: "I'm confused about... Can you explain?"
- Red: "You could make this stronger by adding..."

---

**Phase 3: Debrief (3-5 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- "Return to your chart"
- "Read all the feedback you received"
- "Discuss with your group: What patterns do you notice?"
- Optional: "Make quick revisions based on feedback"

**Student Actions:**
- Read all sticky notes
- Discuss with group:
  - "What did people appreciate?"
  - "What questions do we need to answer?"
  - "What should we revise?"
- Optional: Make quick revisions on chart

**Optional Extension:**
- Groups present charts to class (1 min each)
- Class votes on strongest examples
- Display best charts on classroom walls

---

### Thinking Map Options for Gallery Walk

**Circle Map:** Context (background info about topic)
**Tree Map:** Categorizing (types of evidence, themes, devices)
**Bubble Map:** Describing (author's tone, character traits)
**Flow Map:** Sequence (plot events, argument structure)
**Multi-Flow Map:** Cause/Effect (why author made choices, effects on audience)
**Bridge Map:** Analogy (relating concepts)
**Brace Map:** Part-to-whole (text structure)

---

### Modifications

**For Struggling Students:**
- Provide template for chart (boxes to fill in)
- Give sentence frames for labels
- Assign to group with stronger students
- Reduce requirements (3 examples instead of 5)

**For Advanced Students:**
- Require deeper analysis (explain WHY, not just WHAT)
- Make cross-text connections
- Evaluate quality of evidence (which is strongest?)
- Add synthesizing question

**For Behavior Management:**
- Assign groups strategically (not student choice)
- Give clear roles within groups
- Time limits strictly enforced
- Walking protocol: Museum voices, respectful viewing

---

### Materials Needed
- Chart paper (one per group)
- Markers (assorted colors)
- Sticky notes (3 colors)
- Tape (for posting charts)
- Task cards (one per group)
- Thinking map templates (reference)
- Text (students need access)

**Assessment:**
- Completed chart (group grade)
- Quality of feedback given (individual)
- Participation rubric

## PROTOCOL 4: ANNOTATION STATIONS

### Overview
**Time:** 20-25 minutes
**Grouping:** Individual (rotating through stations)
**Literacy Skills:** Close reading, annotation, analysis
**Best Used:** Days 1-2 (first read, text analysis)

### Structure

**Setup:**
- Create 4-5 stations around classroom
- Each station has different annotation focus
- Students rotate every 4-5 minutes
- By end, text is annotated with multiple layers

---

**Station Designs**

**Station 1: Vocabulary Hunt** (DOK 1-2)
- Task: Highlight 5 unknown words, define using context clues
- Materials: Highlighter (yellow), vocabulary worksheet
- Instructions:
  1. Find 5 words you don't know
  2. Highlight them
  3. Read sentence carefully (context clues)
  4. Write predicted meaning
  5. Check definition (if time)

**Station 2: Purpose Tracker** (DOK 2-3)
- Task: Find 3 sentences showing author's purpose, annotate WHY
- Materials: Highlighter (orange), sticky notes
- Instructions:
  1. Find 3 places where author's purpose is clear
  2. Highlight the sentence
  3. Write on sticky note: "This shows purpose of [persuade/inform/entertain] because..."
  4. Place sticky note in margin

**Station 3: Rhetoric Analyzer** (DOK 3)
- Task: Identify 3 rhetorical devices, label and explain effect
- Materials: Colored pencils, annotation symbols
- Instructions:
  1. Find repetition, metaphor, rhetorical questions, or other devices
  2. Label in margin (R = repetition, M = metaphor, RQ = rhetorical question)
  3. Draw arrow to example
  4. Write effect: "This makes the reader feel..."

**Station 4: Evidence Collector** (DOK 2-3)
- Task: Find strongest evidence for author's claim, explain WHY it's strong
- Materials: Highlighter (green), evidence sheet
- Instructions:
  1. Identify author's main claim
  2. Find 3 pieces of evidence supporting claim
  3. Highlight in green
  4. Rank: Which is strongest? Why?
  5. Write explanation

**Station 5: Structure Mapper** (DOK 2-3)
- Task: Label text structure (intro, claim, evidence, counterclaim, conclusion)
- Materials: Brackets, labels
- Instructions:
  1. Draw bracket [ ] around introduction
  2. Draw bracket [ ] around each evidence paragraph
  3. Draw bracket [ ] around conclusion
  4. Label each section in margin

---

**Rotation Protocol**

**Timing:**
- 5 minutes per station
- Timer rings, students rotate clockwise
- 25 minutes total (5 stations x 5 min)

**Teacher Role:**
- Circulate between stations
- Check work quality
- Prompt struggling students
- Keep time

**Student Workflow:**
1. Start at assigned station (teacher assigns based on seating)
2. Complete task for that station (5 min)
3. When timer rings, rotate to next station clockwise
4. Continue until all 5 stations complete
5. End result: Fully annotated text with 5 different lenses

---

**Debrief (5 minutes):**
- "Look at your annotated text - it's color-coded and layered!"
- "Which station was most helpful for understanding?"
- "What did you learn that you didn't know before?"
- "How will these annotations help you complete your organizer?"

---

### Differentiation

**For Struggling Students:**
- Start at Station 1 (easiest)
- May only complete Stations 1-3 (skip advanced stations)
- Provide sentence frames at each station
- Pair with partner for support

**For Advanced Students:**
- Start at Station 5 (hardest)
- Add complexity: "Evaluate which evidence is strongest and why"
- Create additional station tasks if they finish early

**For ELL Students:**
- Station 1 extended time (vocabulary is priority)
- Bilingual dictionary available
- Sentence frames in native language

---

### Materials Needed
- Copies of text (one per student)
- Highlighters (multiple colors)
- Station task cards (laminated, reusable)
- Sticky notes
- Timer (visible/audible)
- Annotation code reference (posted at each station)

**Assessment:**
- Completed annotated text (collection grade)
- Quality of annotations (formative check)

## PROTOCOL 5: SPEED DATING PEER REVIEW

### Overview
**Time:** 15-20 minutes
**Grouping:** Rotating pairs
**Literacy Skills:** Revision, peer feedback, speaking/listening
**Best Used:** Days 4-5 (revision phase)

### Structure

**Setup (2 minutes):**
- Arrange desks in two rows facing each other (or standing at tables)
- One row stays seated (Row A)
- Other row rotates (Row B)
- Each student has draft (organizer, CR, etc.)

---

**Round 1 (3-4 minutes)**

**Partner A (1-2 min):**
- Reads work aloud to Partner B
- Partner B listens actively (no interrupting)

**Partner B (1-2 min):**
- Gives feedback using sentence frames:
  - "One strength is..."
  - "One area to improve is..."
  - "Have you considered...?"

**Partner A:**
- Takes notes on feedback received
- Asks clarifying questions

---

**Rotation:** Row B shifts down one seat, Row A stays put

---

**Rounds 2-4 (Repeat 3-4 times)**
- Same structure
- New partners each round
- 3-4 minutes per round
- Students receive feedback from 4 different peers

---

**Final Reflection (3 minutes)**

**Teacher Actions:**
- "Look at all the feedback you received"
- "What patterns do you notice?"
- "Choose ONE thing to revise right now"

**Student Actions:**
- Review all feedback
- Identify common suggestions
- Choose one revision to make
- Revise immediately (3 min)

---

### Feedback Structures

**Two Stars and a Wish:**
- Star 1: "One thing you did really well is..."
- Star 2: "Another strength is..."
- Wish: "One way to make this even better is..."

**Glow and Grow:**
- Glow: "What's glowing (working well)..."
- Grow: "What could grow (improve)..."

**TAG Feedback:**
- T = Tell what you like
- A = Ask a question
- G = Give a suggestion

---

### Scaffolds

**For Struggling Students:**
- Provide feedback frames printed on cards
- Model feedback first (you + student volunteer)
- Allow reading from notes instead of aloud
- Partner with stronger student first (build confidence)

**For Advanced Students:**
- Give content-specific feedback (not just "good job")
- Reference rubric when giving feedback
- Explain WHY suggestion would improve work

**Sentence Frames:**
- "Your evidence from paragraph ___ is strong because..."
- "You could strengthen this by adding..."
- "I'm confused about... Can you clarify?"
- "Your reasoning would be clearer if you..."

---

### Materials Needed
- Student drafts (printed or on device)
- Feedback frames (printed at each seat)
- Rubric (reference for quality)
- Timer (visible)
- Revision tools (pens, highlighters)

**Assessment:**
- Revised draft (shows incorporation of feedback)
- Participation (giving quality feedback)

## PROTOCOL 6: KAHOOT/QUIZIZZ COMPETITIONS

### Overview
**Time:** 10-15 minutes
**Grouping:** Individual or teams
**Literacy Skills:** Test-taking, content recall, analysis
**Best Used:** Days 5-6 (assessment prep, review)

### Structure

**Pre-Class Setup:**
- Create Kahoot/Quizizz with 10-15 MC questions
- Questions range DOK 1 (recall) to DOK 3 (analysis)
- Include: Text comprehension, vocabulary, benchmark skills, test-taking strategies

**Launch (1 minute):**
- Project game code on board
- Students join on phones/devices
- Or: Low-tech option (students use whiteboards, you project questions)

---

**Gameplay (10-12 minutes):**

**Individual Mode:**
- Students answer independently
- Timed questions (20-30 seconds each)
- Leaderboard shows top 5 scores
- Points for speed + accuracy

**Team Mode:**
- Partners/groups discuss before answering
- Must reach consensus
- Builds collaboration
- Reduces test anxiety (shared responsibility)

**After Each Question:**
- Show correct answer
- Discuss: "Why is B the best answer?"
- Address misconceptions
- Review test-taking strategies:
  - "Eliminate obviously wrong answers first"
  - "Look for key words in the question"
  - "Go back to the text"

---

**Debrief (2-3 minutes):**
- Announce top scores (celebrate!)
- "What question types were hardest?"
- "What strategies helped you?"
- "What do you need to review before the test?"

**Use Data:**
- Which questions did most students miss?
- Reteach those concepts
- Target small group instruction

---

### Modifications

**For Struggling Students:**
- Team mode (not individual)
- Reduce time pressure (longer per question)
- Fewer questions (10 instead of 15)
- Mix in easier DOK 1-2 questions

**For Advanced Students:**
- All DOK 3 questions
- Explain answer choice (not just select)
- Create their own questions for next Kahoot

**For No-Tech Classrooms:**
- Print questions, students answer on paper
- Read questions aloud, show on board
- Students hold up A/B/C/D cards
- Quick scan for understanding

---

### Question Design Tips

**Good Kahoot Questions:**
- Text-based (require going back to passage)
- Realistic (similar to actual test questions)
- Range of difficulty (some easy wins, some challenges)
- Clear correct answer (no ambiguity)
- Plausible distractors (wrong answers seem reasonable)

**Question Examples:**

*DOK 1 (Recall):*
"What is the setting of this text?"

*DOK 2 (Comprehension):*
"What is the author's main purpose in paragraph 3?"

*DOK 3 (Analysis):*
"How does the author's use of repetition affect the audience?"

---

### Materials Needed
- Kahoot or Quizizz account (free)
- Student devices (phones, tablets, Chromebooks)
- Projector/screen
- Game PIN code
- OR: Low-tech option (whiteboards, answer cards)

**Assessment:**
- Formative data (which questions missed?)
- Engagement check (participation)

## PROTOCOL INTEGRATION WITH 6-DAY CYCLE

### Recommended Schedule

| Day | Teacher-Led | Independent | Technology |
|-----|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **1** | Introduce benchmark + model | **Annotation Stations** | Read 180 |
| **2** | Guided practice with text | **Think-Pair-Share** or **Socratic Seminar** | Read 180 |
| **3** | Model organizer creation | **Gallery Walk** (create organizers) | Read 180 |
| **4** | Refinement strategies | **Gallery Walk** (feedback) or **Speed Dating** | Read 180 |
| **5** | Review test strategies | **Kahoot** or **Think-Pair-Share** | Read 180 |
| **6** | Final support/conferencing | Assessment (MC + CR) | Read 180 |

**Flexibility:** Mix and match based on class needs, adjust timing as needed

## TROUBLESHOOTING

### "Students are off-task during protocols"

**Causes:**
- Instructions unclear
- Activity too long/too short
- Lack of accountability
- Behavior management issues

**Solutions:**
- Model protocol explicitly first
- Use timer strictly (visible countdown)
- Build in accountability (collect work after)
- Assign roles within groups (everyone has job)
- Circulate constantly during activity

---

### "Only some students participate"

**Causes:**
- Dominant students take over
- Shy students don't feel safe
- Language barriers
- Confusion about expectations

**Solutions:**
- Use talking chips (forces equal participation)
- Assign roles (everyone must contribute)
- Start with Think-Pair-Share (lower stakes)
- Build classroom culture of respect first
- Use sentence frames for support

---

### "Protocols take too long - can't fit in 20 minutes"

**Causes:**
- Transition time too long
- Instructions taking too long
- Students unfamiliar with routine

**Solutions:**
- Teach protocols early in year (frontload time investment)
- Use same protocols repeatedly (students know routine)
- Streamline transitions (materials ready, clear signals)
- Cut reflection/debrief if running over time

---

### "I don't have materials/tech for these protocols"

**Low-Tech Alternatives:**
- Kahoot → Paper quiz with hand-raised answers
- Gallery Walk → Pass papers around class instead of walking
- Annotation Stations → Whole-class annotation (different colors for different focuses)
- Socratic Seminar → Think-Pair-Share (smaller scale)

## INTEGRATION WITH AGENTS/UNIT BUILDING

### When Building Units (Phases 1-3)

**Phase 1: Unit Planning**
- Select protocols for each day based on learning goals
- Day 1-2: Annotation Stations or Think-Pair-Share
- Day 3-4: Gallery Walk or Socratic Seminar
- Day 5-6: Kahoot or Speed Dating

**Phase 2: Lesson Planning**
- Include protocol instructions in Independent rotation
- List materials needed for each protocol
- Provide discussion questions/prompts
- Build in accountability (submissions, grades)

**Phase 3: Materials Building**
- Create Kahoot/Quizizz (save link in materials)
- Design station task cards
- Write discussion questions
- Prepare feedback frames
