# ELA.10.R.3.4 - Understanding Rhetoric (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.3.4**: Analyze rhetorical devices and their effects in speeches and other persuasive texts.

## Benchmark Clarifications
**Rhetoric** = The art of effective or persuasive speaking/writing. Students must identify rhetorical devices AND analyze their **effect** on the audience.

**Common Rhetorical Devices:**
- **Repetition**: Repeating words/phrases for emphasis
- **Parallelism**: Similar grammatical structures (e.g., "I came, I saw, I conquered")
- **Rhetorical questions**: Questions not requiring answers, used to make points
- **Antithesis**: Contrasting ideas (e.g., "Ask not what your country can do for you...")
- **Allusion**: Reference to historical/literary/cultural figures or events
- **Anaphora**: Repetition of words at the beginning of clauses (e.g., "We shall fight...")

**Rhetorical Appeals:**
- **Ethos**: Credibility, character, trustworthiness
- **Pathos**: Emotion, values, beliefs
- **Logos**: Logic, reason, evidence

**Key Focus**: NOT just identifying devices—analyzing **WHY** the speaker uses them and **WHAT EFFECT** they have

## Prerequisite Skills & Common Gaps

**Students may struggle with:**
- Distinguishing rhetoric terms (repetition, anaphora, parallelism)
- Understanding the difference between device (what) and effect (why)
- Analyzing audience instead of just personal response
- Connecting device to speaker's purpose

**Built-in Remediation:**
- Start with anchor charts defining each device with examples
- Use short, accessible speeches before complex texts
- Model effect analysis with "This makes the audience feel/think/do..."
- Practice identifying one device thoroughly before adding more

## 5-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Speaker's Purpose and Audience
Before analyzing devices, establish context:
- **Who is speaking?** (Establishes ethos)
- **Who is the audience?**
- **What does the speaker want?** (Purpose: inform, persuade, inspire, call to action)
- **When/where was this delivered?** (Historical context)

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the speaker's purpose and who is the audience?"

### Step 2: Identify Rhetorical Devices
Find specific devices in the text:
- Look for patterns (repetition, parallel structure)
- Notice questions that aren't really questions
- Find contrasts or comparisons
- Identify references to history, literature, religion

**Label the device**: "This is [repetition/parallelism/rhetorical question/etc.]"

**Organizer prompt**: "What rhetorical device is used? Quote the example."

### Step 3: Analyze the Effect on the Audience
Determine WHY the speaker uses this device:
- What emotion does it create?
- What idea does it emphasize?
- How does it strengthen the argument?
- What does the speaker want the audience to think, feel, or do?

**Key questions**:
- "What is the effect?"
- "Why did the speaker choose this device?"

**Organizer prompt**: "What effect does this device have on the audience?"

### Step 4: Identify Rhetorical Appeals (Ethos, Pathos, Logos)
Classify the appeal:
- **Ethos**: Does the device build credibility or trust?
- **Pathos**: Does it evoke emotion?
- **Logos**: Does it present logic or evidence?

**Organizer prompt**: "Which rhetorical appeal does this use?"

### Step 5: Evaluate Overall Effectiveness
Synthesize the analysis:
- How do multiple devices work together?
- Is the rhetoric effective for this audience?
- What is the cumulative impact?

**Organizer prompt**: "How effective is the speaker's use of rhetoric?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Identification Questions
- What rhetorical devices are used? (repetition, parallelism, rhetorical questions, appeals)
- Identify an example of [specific device] in the speech.
- Which appeal (ethos, pathos, logos) is most prominent?

### Effect Questions
- How do these devices affect the audience?
- What is the speaker's purpose in using [device]?
- What emotion or response does the speaker want to create?

### Analysis Questions
- How does [device] contribute to the speaker's argument?
- Why did the speaker choose this particular device for this audience?
- How effective is the speaker's use of rhetoric?

### Comparison Questions
- Compare the rhetorical strategies in [two speeches].
- How do different speakers use rhetoric to achieve similar purposes?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Analyzes sophisticated rhetorical devices and subtle effects
- Evaluates effectiveness for specific audience and context
- Explains how multiple devices work together
- Considers alternative rhetorical choices

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Identifies rhetorical devices with accurate labels
- Analyzes effects on audience with specific evidence
- Distinguishes ethos, pathos, and logos
- Connects devices to speaker's purpose

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies common rhetorical devices
- Describes basic effects (makes audience feel...)
- Recognizes appeals with some accuracy
- Provides some textual support

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Difficulty identifying devices beyond obvious repetition
- Confusion between device and effect
- Limited understanding of appeals
- Minimal analysis of audience impact

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Speeches (historical, political, ceremonial)
- Persuasive essays or articles
- Texts with clear rhetorical devices
- Texts where audience and context are identifiable
- Multiple texts for comparison

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Purely informational texts without persuasive purpose
- Literary fiction (unless analyzing character's speech)
- Texts requiring extensive historical knowledge not provided
- Technical writing without rhetorical elements

## Common Rhetoric Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- rhetoric (art of persuasion)
- device (technique or tool)
- appeal (method of persuasion)
- credibility (trustworthiness)
- emphasize (stress or highlight)
- evoke (bring out emotion/response)
- persuade (convince)
- effective (successful, impactful)

**Rhetorical Devices:**
- repetition (repeating words/phrases)
- parallelism (similar grammatical structures)
- anaphora (repetition at start of clauses)
- rhetorical question (question for effect, not answer)
- antithesis (contrasting ideas)
- allusion (reference to history/literature)
- hyperbole (exaggeration)
- metaphor/simile (comparison)

**Rhetorical Appeals:**
- ethos (credibility/character)
- pathos (emotion/values)
- logos (logic/reason)

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For excerpt from JFK's Inaugural Address (1961):**

| Quote/Example | Device Name | Effect on Audience | Appeal |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|--------|
| "And so, my fellow Americans: ask not what your country can do for you—ask not what you can do for your country." | **Antithesis + Parallelism + Repetition**: The parallel structure ("ask not...ask not") creates rhythm. The antithesis contrasts two opposite ideas (country serving you vs. you serving country). The repetition of "ask not" emphasizes the call to action. | This makes the audience reconsider their relationship with government. The contrast is memorable and quotable—it sticks in listeners' minds. The parallel structure gives it a formal, presidential tone while the call to service inspires civic duty. The device transforms a request into a challenge, making citizens feel personally responsible. | Primarily **Ethos**: JFK establishes himself as a leader who expects sacrifice, building credibility. Also **Pathos**: Appeals to patriotic emotion and sense of duty. The contrast suggests those who only take from government are selfish, motivating audience to be on the "right" side of the antithesis. |

## ESOL Scaffolds for Rhetoric

**Sentence frames:**
- "The speaker uses [device] when they say '_____.'"
- "This device makes the audience feel/think _______ because _______."
- "The speaker appeals to [ethos/pathos/logos] by _______."
- "This is effective because _______."

**Vocabulary support:**
- Create anchor chart with device names, definitions, and examples
- Color-code appeals (ethos = blue, pathos = red, logos = green) on text
- Provide "effect word bank" (inspires, motivates, emphasizes, challenges, evokes, convinces)

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Listen to speech excerpt together
- Partner identification of devices before individual analysis
- Discuss effect before writing

**Annotation practice:**
- Highlight/underline devices on printed speech
- Label in margins before completing organizer

## Prerequisite Mini-Lessons (Built-In Remediation)

**Day 1 Focus**: What is rhetoric? Define and provide examples from familiar contexts (ads, slogans, movie speeches)

**Day 2 Focus**: Ethos, Pathos, Logos - Practice identifying appeals in short texts/commercials before speeches

**Day 3 Focus**: Common devices - Create anchor chart with definitions and examples. Start with repetition only.

**Day 4 Focus**: Device vs. Effect - Model the difference between "what" (identifying device) and "why" (analyzing effect)

**Day 5+**: Apply to target speech with gradual release

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Analyze rhetorical devices and their effects in persuasive texts

**Key Process**: Identify purpose/audience → Identify devices → Analyze effects → Identify appeals → Evaluate effectiveness

**Essential Question**: "What rhetorical devices does the speaker use, and how do they affect the audience?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students identify devices but don't analyze **effect** OR they describe personal response instead of analyzing audience impact

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "Why did the speaker use THIS device for THIS audience?" and "What does the speaker want the audience to think/feel/do?"
