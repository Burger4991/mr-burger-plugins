# ELA.10.R.2.4 - Argument Analysis (Grade 10)

## Standard
**ELA.10.R.2.4**: Compare opposing arguments on the same topic and evaluate their reasoning and evidence for validity, relevance, and sufficiency.

## Benchmark Clarifications
This benchmark requires students to analyze **argumentative texts** and evaluate the quality of reasoning and evidence.

**Key Components of Arguments:**
- **Claim**: The position or assertion (what the author believes)
- **Evidence**: Support for the claim (facts, statistics, examples, expert opinions, logical reasoning)
- **Reasoning**: How the evidence connects to and supports the claim
- **Counterclaim**: Opposing viewpoint that author addresses
- **Rebuttal**: Author's response to counterclaim

**Evaluating Evidence Quality:**
- **Validity**: Is it accurate and truthful?
- **Relevance**: Does it directly relate to the claim?
- **Sufficiency**: Is there enough evidence?

**Key Focus**: Not just identifying claims/evidence—EVALUATING effectiveness and COMPARING opposing arguments

## Prerequisite Skills & Common Gaps

**Students may struggle with:**
- Distinguishing claim from evidence
- Evaluating quality of evidence (assuming all evidence is equal)
- Understanding that "more evidence" doesn't mean "better argument"
- Recognizing logical fallacies or weak reasoning
- Comparing arguments rather than just analyzing one

**Built-in Remediation:**
- Create anchor chart: Claim vs. Evidence vs. Reasoning
- Practice with obvious weak arguments first (recognizing bad evidence builds evaluation skills)
- Use CER framework (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) consistently
- Teach common logical fallacies with examples
- Use paired texts with opposing arguments from the start

## 6-Step Analytical Process (Grade 10)

### Step 1: Identify the Claim
Determine the author's position:
- What is the author arguing?
- What does the author want readers to believe or do?
- State the claim as a complete sentence

**Look for:**
- Thesis statement (often in introduction or conclusion)
- Opinionated statement
- Call to action

**Organizer prompt**: "What is the author's claim?"

### Step 2: Identify the Evidence
Find support for the claim:
- What facts, statistics, or data support the claim?
- What examples or anecdotes are provided?
- What expert opinions or quotations are used?
- What logical reasoning is presented?

**Types of evidence:**
- **Statistics**: Numerical data
- **Facts**: Verifiable information
- **Examples**: Specific instances
- **Expert testimony**: Quotes from authorities
- **Anecdotes**: Personal stories
- **Logical reasoning**: If/then, cause/effect logic

**Organizer prompt**: "What evidence supports the claim?"

### Step 3: Analyze the Reasoning
Evaluate how evidence connects to claim:
- Does the author explain HOW evidence supports the claim?
- Is the reasoning logical?
- Are there gaps in logic?
- Does evidence actually prove what author claims?

**Organizer prompt**: "How does the evidence support the claim? Is the reasoning sound?"

### Step 4: Evaluate Evidence Quality
Assess validity, relevance, and sufficiency:

**Validity**: Is the evidence accurate and trustworthy?
- Is the source credible?
- Is it factual or opinion presented as fact?
- Is it current/up-to-date?

**Relevance**: Does it relate directly to the claim?
- Does it address the claim?
- Or is it interesting but off-topic?

**Sufficiency**: Is there enough evidence?
- Does one example prove the point, or do we need more?
- Are multiple types of evidence provided?

**Organizer prompt**: "Is the evidence valid, relevant, and sufficient?"

### Step 5: Identify and Evaluate Counterclaims/Rebuttals
Analyze how author addresses opposing views:
- Does the author acknowledge opposing arguments?
- How does the author respond?
- Is the rebuttal effective?

**Strong arguments address counterclaims**; weak arguments ignore them.

**Organizer prompt**: "What counterclaims are addressed? How effectively?"

### Step 6: Compare Opposing Arguments
Evaluate multiple arguments on the same topic:
- What claims do authors make?
- What types of evidence do they use?
- Which argument is more convincing? Why?
- What are strengths and weaknesses of each?

**Organizer prompt**: "Which argument is stronger? Why?"

## Question Stems (from Planning Cards)

### Claim Identification Questions
- What is the author's claim or position?
- What does the author want readers to believe/do?

### Evidence Questions
- What evidence supports the claim?
- What types of evidence are used? (statistics, anecdotes, expert opinion, logical reasoning)
- Where is the strongest evidence presented?

### Evaluation Questions
- How effective is the argument? (validity, relevance, sufficiency)
- Is the evidence valid (accurate/trustworthy)?
- Is the evidence relevant (directly supports claim)?
- Is there sufficient (enough) evidence?
- Is the reasoning sound?

### Comparison Questions
- How do opposing arguments compare?
- What are the strengths and weaknesses of each argument?
- Which argument is more convincing? Why?

## Achievement Level Descriptors (Grade 10)

### Level 5 (Advanced)
- Evaluates complex arguments with sophisticated criteria
- Compares opposing arguments with nuanced analysis
- Identifies logical fallacies and weak reasoning
- Distinguishes between types of evidence and their effectiveness

### Level 4 (Proficient)
- Identifies claims and evidence accurately
- Evaluates validity, relevance, and sufficiency with evidence
- Compares opposing arguments effectively
- Analyzes reasoning and connections

### Level 3 (Basic)
- Identifies basic claims and some evidence
- Makes simple evaluations of argument quality
- Notes differences between arguments
- Provides limited analysis of reasoning

### Level 2 (Below Basic)
- Difficulty distinguishing claim from evidence
- Limited evaluation of argument quality
- Struggles to compare opposing arguments
- Minimal analysis of reasoning

## Assessment Limits

**Appropriate for Grade 10 Assessment:**
- Argumentative essays or opinion pieces
- Editorials or persuasive articles
- Speeches with clear claims
- Multiple texts with opposing arguments on same topic
- Texts with identifiable evidence and reasoning

**NOT Appropriate:**
- Informational texts without argumentation
- Purely narrative texts
- Texts requiring extensive background knowledge
- Technical texts without persuasive purpose

## Common Argument Vocabulary

**Tier 2 Academic Words:**
- claim (assertion, position)
- evidence (support, proof)
- reasoning (logic, explanation)
- valid (accurate, truthful)
- relevant (related, applicable)
- sufficient (enough, adequate)
- counterclaim (opposing argument)
- rebuttal (response to counterclaim)
- evaluate (assess, judge)
- effective (successful, convincing)

**Argument Components:**
- thesis (main claim)
- support (evidence)
- warrant (reasoning connecting evidence to claim)
- concession (acknowledging opposing point)
- refutation (proving opposing point wrong)

**Evidence Types:**
- statistic (numerical data)
- anecdote (personal story)
- expert testimony (authority quote)
- fact (verifiable information)
- example (specific instance)
- logical reasoning (if/then, cause/effect)

**Evaluation Terms:**
- credible (trustworthy)
- biased (prejudiced)
- logical fallacy (flawed reasoning)
- weak/strong argument

## Sample I Do Row (Teacher Model)

**For editorial: "Schools Should Ban Smartphones"**

| Claim | Evidence | Type of Evidence | Validity/Relevance/Sufficiency | Reasoning Quality |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-------------------------------|------------------|
| **Schools should ban smartphones because they distract from learning and harm student mental health.** | **Evidence 1**: "A 2022 study found that students in phone-free schools scored 15% higher on standardized tests" (para 2). **Evidence 2**: "Dr. Sarah Johnson, child psychologist, states 'constant social media access during school hours increases anxiety'" (para 4). **Evidence 3**: Author shares story about her daughter who "couldn't focus on homework" due to phone notifications (para 6). | 1. **Statistic** 2. **Expert testimony** 3. **Anecdote** (personal story) | **Valid**: The study citation appears credible; expert has relevant credentials. The anecdote is the mother's opinion, less objective. **Relevant**: All three directly address either distraction (academic performance) or mental health. **Sufficient**: Three pieces of evidence from different sources provide breadth. However, could use more statistics—one study isn't definitive. Anecdote is weakest evidence (one person's experience). | **Reasoning is moderate**: The author explains that test scores show phones distract, and the psychologist quote supports the mental health claim. However, the connection between banning phones and solving these problems isn't fully explained—correlation isn't causation. The author doesn't address counterclaims (e.g., phones can be educational tools) or acknowledge schools that successfully integrate phones. Overall: **Partially effective** argument with room for stronger evidence and reasoning. |

## ESOL Scaffolds for Argument

**Sentence frames:**
- "The author's claim is that _______."
- "The author supports this claim with _______ (evidence)."
- "This evidence is [valid/relevant/sufficient] because _______."
- "The reasoning [is/is not] logical because _______."
- "Argument 1 claims _______, while Argument 2 claims _______."
- "[Argument 1/2] is stronger because _______."

**CER Framework (for writing AND analysis):**
- **C**laim: What is the argument?
- **E**vidence: What proof is provided?
- **R**easoning: How does evidence support the claim?

**Vocabulary support:**
- Anchor chart with evidence types and examples
- Validity/Relevance/Sufficiency checklist
- Color-code: Claim = green, Evidence = yellow, Reasoning = blue

**Think-Pair-Share:**
- Partner identification of claim before individual analysis
- Discuss quality of evidence before writing evaluation

**Graphic organizers:**
- T-chart: Argument 1 | Argument 2
- Three-column: Evidence | Type | Evaluation

## Prerequisite Mini-Lessons (Built-In Remediation)

**Day 1 Focus**: What is an argument? Claim vs. opinion vs. fact - Use simple examples

**Day 2 Focus**: CER Framework - Claim, Evidence, Reasoning anchor chart with examples

**Day 3 Focus**: Types of evidence - Create chart with examples (statistics, anecdotes, expert quotes, etc.)

**Day 4 Focus**: Validity, Relevance, Sufficiency - Practice evaluating sample evidence

**Day 5 Focus**: Counterclaims - Strong arguments acknowledge opposing views

**Day 6+**: Apply to target texts with gradual release, starting with paired opposing arguments

## Quick Reference

**Standard**: Compare opposing arguments and evaluate reasoning and evidence for validity, relevance, and sufficiency

**Key Process**: Identify claim → Identify evidence → Analyze reasoning → Evaluate quality (validity, relevance, sufficiency) → Address counterclaims → Compare arguments

**Essential Question**: "What is the author claiming, what evidence supports it, and how effective is the argument?"

**Common Pitfall**: Students identify evidence but don't EVALUATE quality OR they think "more evidence = better argument" without considering validity/relevance

**Teacher Move**: Always ask "Is this evidence valid (accurate)? Is it relevant (related)? Is there enough (sufficient)?" Use CER framework consistently.

## Feedback Checkpoints

### Day 4 Checkpoint: Claim, Evidence, and Reasoning Analysis

**What to look for in student work:**
- **Claim identification**: Has the student identified an arguable position (not just the topic)?
- **Evidence types noted**: Has the student identified different types of evidence (statistics, expert opinion, anecdote, logical reasoning)?
- **Reasoning analyzed**: Has the student explained HOW the evidence supports the claim?
- **Effectiveness evaluated**: Has the student assessed whether the evidence provides strong vs. weak support?

**Strong Day 4 work shows:**
- Claim stated as a complete arguable position (not "This article is about phones" but "The author claims schools should ban smartphones")
- Evidence categorized by type (statistic, expert testimony, anecdote, etc.)
- Analysis of the connection between evidence and claim ("The statistic supports the claim because...")
- Preliminary evaluation of strength ("This evidence is strong because..." or "This is weak because...")

**Common gaps to address:**
- **Confusing topic with claim**: "The article is about school phones" instead of "The author claims..."
- **Listing evidence without analysis**: Just copying quotes without explaining reasoning
- **Missing evaluation**: Not assessing whether evidence is strong or weak
- **No connection to reasoning**: Identifying evidence but not explaining how it supports the claim

**Feedback focus:**
- Push students to identify the arguable claim (ask: "What does the author want you to believe?")
- Require explanation of reasoning (ask: "HOW does this evidence support the claim?")
- Demand evaluation (ask: "Is this evidence strong? Why or why not?")
- Use CER framework consistently: Claim-Evidence-Reasoning

### Day 6 Checkpoint: Argument Quality and Critical Evaluation

**What to look for in student work:**
- **Claims about effectiveness**: Does the student make claims about the argument's overall effectiveness?
- **Validity, relevance, sufficiency assessed**: Has the student evaluated evidence using all three criteria?
- **Critical analysis demonstrated**: Does the student question the argument rather than accepting it at face value?
- **Reasoning quality addressed**: Has the student evaluated whether the reasoning is logical or has gaps?

**Strong Day 6 work shows:**
- Clear judgment of argument effectiveness with specific criteria ("This argument is partially effective because...")
- Analysis of validity ("The source is credible because..."), relevance ("This evidence directly supports..."), and sufficiency ("The author needs more evidence because...")
- Critical thinking about weaknesses ("The author doesn't address counterclaims..." or "This reasoning has a gap...")
- Comparison to other arguments or recognition of what's missing

**Quality issues to address:**
- **Confusing topic with claim in final analysis**: Still describing what the article is "about" rather than evaluating the argument
- **Listing evidence without analyzing reasoning**: Cataloging quotes without explaining connections
- **Failing to evaluate effectiveness**: No judgment about whether the argument works
- **Accepting argument without critical analysis**: Assuming all evidence is equally valid or that more evidence = better argument
- **Ignoring logical gaps**: Not noticing when reasoning doesn't connect evidence to claim
- **Missing validity/relevance/sufficiency**: Only evaluating one criterion instead of all three

**Feedback focus:**
- Require critical evaluation (ask: "Is this argument convincing? Why or why not?")
- Push for validity assessment (ask: "Is this source credible? How do you know?")
- Demand relevance analysis (ask: "Does this evidence actually prove the claim?")
- Question sufficiency (ask: "Is there enough evidence? What's missing?")
- Highlight logical gaps (ask: "Does the reasoning make sense? Are there holes in the logic?")
- Encourage counterclaim consideration (ask: "What would someone who disagrees say? Does the author address this?")

**Teacher focus targets:**
- Students must identify the arguable claim (not just topic)
- Students must analyze reasoning and logic (not just list evidence)
- Students must evaluate evidence quality using validity, relevance, and sufficiency
- Students must demonstrate critical thinking by questioning the argument's effectiveness
